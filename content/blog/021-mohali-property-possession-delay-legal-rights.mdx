# Possession Delay in Mohali: Your Legal Rights and What to Do in the First Six Months

If your property possession in Mohali is delayed, your primary legal right under RERA Punjab is to either withdraw from the project with a full refund plus interest or stay in the project and claim monthly interest for every month of delay. According to Section 18 of the Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, 2016, the builder is liable to pay interest at the State Bank of India’s highest marginal cost of lending rate (MCLR) plus 2%. In the first six months of a delay, you must document all communication, send a formal legal notice, and if unresolved, file a formal complaint with the RERA Authority via the Form M (for interest/refund) or Form N (for compensation). Most builders will offer "delayed possession charges" as per an old buyer-agreement, but these are often ₹5–10 per sq. ft.—significantly lower than what you are legally entitled to under RERA.

## What are your immediate rights when a Mohali builder misses the deadline?

The moment the "Date of Possession" mentioned in your Registered Agreement for Sale passes without a valid Offer of Possession (backed by an Occupation Certificate), the developer is in default. In my years of navigating these approvals across GMADA and PUDA, I’ve seen that builders often try to mask this default by sending "notice of completion" letters that have no legal standing without an OC.

Under RERA Punjab, you have two distinct paths:

1.  **Withdrawal and Refund:** You can exit the project. The builder must return every rupee you’ve paid, along with interest (currently around 10.25% to 11% depending on the SBI MCLR) from the date of each payment until the date of refund.
2.  **Continuance and Interest:** If you want to keep the flat or plot, you are entitled to monthly interest for every month the possession is delayed beyond the committed date.

It is important to understand that these rights override any one-sided clauses in your original builder-buyer agreement. If your agreement says the builder will only pay ₹5 per sq. ft. for delays, but RERA says they must pay MCLR + 2%, the RERA rule wins. This protection is fundamental to the [Mohali property buyer protection guide](/blog/mohali-property-buyer-protection-guide).

## The First Two Months: Documentation and Formal Notice

In the first 60 days of a possession delay, your goal isn't just to wait—it's to build a paper trail that no developer can ignore in court. I’ve navigated builder-buyer conflicts from both sides, and the side with the better folder of evidence usually wins.

### Why you must document the 'Ready to Pay' status
One common builder defense is that the buyer was also in default. To counter this, ensure you have proof that you have paid all installments due as per the construction milestones. If the builder stopped sending demand letters because the project stalled, send an email asking for the next demand letter based on the current stage. This proves you are a "willing and ready" buyer.

*   **Audit Your Documents:** Ensure you have the registered Agreement for Sale, all payment receipts, and every email exchange regarding the possession date.
*   **Site Inspection:** Visit the site. Take photos and videos of the current construction stage. If the builder claims the project is "ready" but the lifts aren't working or the fire NOC isn't in place, document it.

*   **The Formal Notice:** Do not rely on WhatsApp messages. Send a formal legal notice through a lawyer or a strongly worded registered letter yourself. Demand the possession within a fixed timeframe or invoke your right to interest under Section 18. This notice is a prerequisite for showing "intent" if you later go to the [RERA Authority](/blog/rera-complaint-punjab-file-process-2026).

## Months Three and Four: Filing the RERA Complaint

If the builder hasn't provided a concrete, OC-backed timeline by month three, it’s time to move from conversation to litigation. Filing a complaint on the RERA Punjab portal (rera.punjab.gov.in) is a structured process that requires precision.

You will typically file **Form M** if you are seeking interest for the delay or a refund of your principal amount. This goes to the RERA Authority. If you are seeking additional compensation for mental agony or financial loss (like the rent you paid because you couldn't move in), you file **Form N** before the Adjudicating Officer.

### The documentation checklist for your RERA filing
When I advise clients on [real estate disputes in Punjab](/blog/real-estate-disputes-legal-rights-punjab-2026), I insist on a complete digital folder before clicking 'Submit' on the RERA portal:
1.  **Agreement for Sale:** The registered version is mandatory.
2.  **Payment Schedule:** A clear table showing date, amount, and mode of payment for every rupee sent to the builder.
3.  **Bank Statements:** Highlighting the payments to match the receipts.
4.  **Correspondence:** PDF copies of all emails where you asked for possession and the builder's replies.
5.  **Legal Notice:** A copy of the notice sent in Month 2 and the proof of delivery (Speed Post tracking).

In my experience handling project approvals, developers often use these two months to "negotiate" with buyers to keep them from filing. They might offer a waiver of club charges or a free car parking spot. Always calculate the value of these offers against the RERA interest you are owed. Usually, the interest amount is far higher.

## Months Five and Six: The Adjudicating Officer and the Resolution Process

Once your complaint is filed, the RERA Authority will issue a notice to the builder. The next 60 days involve hearings where the builder must explain the delay. "Force Majeure" (unforeseeable circumstances) is a common defense, but RERA Punjab is quite strict about what qualifies. A shortage of labor or a delay in getting a [PSPCL connection](/blog/separate-electricity-connection-mohali-pspcl) is generally not considered a valid excuse for the builder to avoid paying you.

### What happens during a RERA hearing?
The hearings are now mostly conducted via video conferencing. You (or your representative) will present your case first. The builder's lawyer will then present justifications for the delay. The Authority looks for one thing: Was there a valid Occupation Certificate on the date promised? If not, the builder is liable.

By month six, you should be looking at an order. A typical RERA order will specify the exact amount of interest the builder must pay you. If the builder fails to pay, you can move for "Execution" of the order, where the RERA Authority has the power to recover the money as arrears of land revenue through the Deputy Commissioner’s office.

## How is the RERA Punjab penalty actually calculated?

The math is simple but significant. Most buyers don't realize how much they are leaving on the table because they look at the absolute numbers the builder offers rather than the percentage-based legal right.

Let’s say you paid ₹1 Crore for a flat in Sector 82A. The possession was due on January 1st, but it is now July 1st—a six-month delay.
*   **The Builder's Offer:** Many old agreements offer ₹5 per sq. ft. On a 2,000 sq. ft. flat, that’s ₹10,000 per month. Total: ₹60,000.
*   **The RERA Right:** If the SBI MCLR is 8.25%, the RERA interest rate is 10.25% (MCLR + 2%). On ₹1 Crore, that is ₹10.25 Lakhs per year, or roughly ₹85,416 per month.
*   **The Difference:** For a six-month delay, RERA entitles you to approximately ₹5.12 Lakhs.

The builder is offering you ₹60,000 when the law says you are owed over ₹5 Lakhs. This is why builders hate RERA filings and why you must insist on your rights. You can find more details on this in our [Mohali real estate FAQ](/blog/mohali-real-estate-faq-2026).

## What builders typically offer vs. what you are legally entitled to

When you approach a developer in Mohali about a delay, you will encounter the "Standard Negotiation Script." Having been on the developer side, I know how these are drafted. They are designed to make the buyer feel that they are getting a "special deal" while actually saving the developer millions.

| Builder Offer | Your Legal Right (RERA Punjab) |
| :--- | :--- |
| ₹5–10 per sq. ft. as per agreement | SBI MCLR + 2% (approx. 10–11% on total paid) |
| Credit against future maintenance charges | Cash refund or monthly interest payment |
| "Force Majeure" due to material costs | Delay is builder's risk; costs are not an excuse |
| Possession "under protest" (signing away rights) | Right to snag-free possession with all NOCs |

### The trap of 'Maintenance Waivers'
A builder might offer you "Free maintenance for 3 years" instead of delay interest. On a typical 3BHK, maintenance might be ₹5,000 a month. Over 3 years, that's ₹1.8 Lakhs. However, as shown in the calculation above, your actual interest entitlement for a single year of delay could be over ₹10 Lakhs. Never accept a waiver without doing the math.

## Negotiating with the builder before going legal

I always tell my clients: a settlement today is often better than a judgment next year, provided the settlement is fair. If the delay is only 3–4 months and the builder is genuinely close to getting the OC, you might negotiate a middle ground.

### How to approach the negotiation table
Don't go in angry; go in informed. Show them the RERA calculation. Say, "I know I am entitled to ₹85,000 a month under RERA. You are offering me ₹10,000. If we can meet at ₹50,000 as a direct credit to my final payment, I will avoid the RERA filing."

Ask for:
*   A formal adjustment of the interest amount against your final "possession-linked" payment.
*   A written commitment on the date of providing the [Occupation Certificate](/blog/mohali-property-buyer-protection-guide).
*   Upgrades to the flat (better flooring, woodwork) that equal the value of the interest owed.

If the builder refuses to even acknowledge the RERA interest rate, stop negotiating. At that point, the builder is counting on your hesitation.

## Why you need to check the project's RERA status regularly

A possession delay is often a symptom of a deeper problem—usually a cash flow crunch or a [regulatory dispute](/blog/real-estate-disputes-legal-rights-punjab-2026). You should regularly check the project’s page on the RERA Punjab website.

Look for:
*   **Quarterly Progress Reports:** Is the builder actually reporting progress?
*   **Litigation:** Are there other complaints already filed against this project?
*   **Extension Requests:** Has the builder applied for an extension of the RERA completion date?

In my experience, if a builder is filing for an extension without informing the buyers, it's a major red flag. You can read more about these warning signs in our [guide to real estate guide 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026).

## The Risk of Accepting Possession Without an Occupation Certificate
Some builders will pressure you to take "interior possession" or "fit-out possession" while the OC is still pending. **This is illegal.** Taking possession of a building without an OC means you are occupying an unauthorized structure. You won't get a permanent electricity connection, your insurance will be invalid, and the Municipal Committee can technically seal the building. More importantly, once you take fit-out possession, the builder will argue in RERA that the "delay" ended on the day you took the keys.

## Conclusion: Don't Wait for the Builder to 'Feel Fair'
Possession delays are stressful, but they are a solvable financial problem if you act within the first six months. Do not let a developer’s sales staff convince you that you have no rights beyond a poorly drafted agreement. The law in Punjab is firmly on the side of the buyer, provided you document the default and follow the RERA process.

If you are facing a delay, the first step is clarity. Know your numbers, know your dates, and know that Section 18 was written specifically to protect you from the power imbalance that used to define Indian real estate.
