# Licensed Plot vs Unauthorised Colony in Mohali: Why the Distinction Destroys Resale Value

The fundamental difference between a licensed plot and an unauthorised colony in Mohali comes down to legal legitimacy and financial liquidity. A licensed colony has obtained a Change of Land Use (CLU) from the Town and Country Planning department, paid External Development Charges (EDC) to GMADA or PUDA, and secured RERA Punjab registration. This makes the plot a "bankable asset" eligible for home loans and easy resale. An unauthorised colony, often sold as "registry-ready" plots on agricultural land, lacks these approvals, meaning no bank will fund it, the government can demolish it at any time, and your resale pool is limited to cash-only buyers who are willing to take massive legal risks. In Mohali’s 2026 market, buying in an unauthorised colony isn't a "budget choice"—it is a guaranteed way to see your capital stagnate while licensed projects in the same sector see 20–30% annual appreciation.

## What actually makes a colony "licensed" in the eyes of GMADA?

When I talk to land sellers who have just received their acquisition payouts, they often see two plots side-by-side. One is priced at ₹60,000 per square yard in a GMADA-approved project, and the other is ₹25,000 per square yard just across the fence. The cheaper one looks like a bargain, but you aren't comparing apples to apples. You are comparing a legal urban asset with an illegal encroachment on agricultural land.

To become a licensed colony in Punjab, a developer must go through a rigorous, multi-stage process that I have personally navigated from the developer side. Having managed project approvals across PUDA, PSPCL, Municipal Committee, Forest Department, and Industrial Departments, I can tell you that "getting lucky" with approvals is a myth. It starts with the **Change of Land Use (CLU)**. The developer must prove to the government that the land is suitable for residential or commercial use and pay heavy fees to the state treasury. 

After CLU, the developer pays **External Development Charges (EDC)** and **Internal Development Charges (IDC)**. These fees fund the massive infrastructure—the wide Sector roads, the water treatment plants, and the electricity grids—that make Mohali what it is today. Once these are paid, the developer gets a "License to Develop." Only then can they apply for **RERA Punjab registration**. If a project doesn't have a RERA number, it simply isn't a licensed colony.

## How do you verify the licensing status of a Mohali plot before you pay?

I've seen too many buyers trust a colorful brochure or the word of a "dealer" who says the registry is open. A registry does *not* prove a colony is licensed. In Punjab, you can sometimes register a share of agricultural land (Khewat) without the colony being approved. This is the trap that catches many first-time investors from rural Punjab.

To protect your money, you must follow these three steps:

1.  **Check the RERA Punjab Portal:** Every licensed colony must be registered. If you can't find the RERA number on the official website, walk away. This is your first and most powerful shield.
2.  **Verify the Layout Plan:** A licensed colony has a GMADA-approved layout. This plan shows exactly where the parks, roads, and utilities are. In unauthorised colonies, "roads" are often just narrow strips of dirt that can be reclaimed by the neighboring farmer later. I have seen instances where the "park" in the brochure was actually a piece of land owned by a third party who had no intention of selling.
3.  **Ask for the CLU and License to Develop:** Don't take a photocopy of a photocopy. Ask to see the original approval letters from the Town and Country Planning department.

## Why does the "unauthorised" tag destroy your resale value?

In real estate, your exit is just as important as your entry. When you buy a licensed plot in a sector like 82A or along the Airport Road, your future buyer is likely a corporate professional or an NRI. These buyers require home loans. 

**Bank Loan Implications:** Nationalized and private banks (HDFC, ICICI, SBI) will never provide a home loan for a plot in an unauthorised colony. They only lend where the title is clear and the project is approved. If you buy an unauthorised plot for ₹30 lakh today, and five years later it is "worth" ₹60 lakh, you can only sell it to someone who has ₹60 lakh in cash. You have eliminated 95% of your potential buyers. As someone who has worked in equity markets and holds NCFM certifications, I evaluate real estate returns through the lens of liquidity. An asset you can't sell easily isn't worth the paper it's written on.

**Government Action:** The Greater Mohali Area Development Authority (GMADA) and the Punjab government are increasingly aggressive against "Kacha" colonies. I have seen demolition drives where boundary walls and paved paths are razed in a single afternoon. If the colony is sealed or demolished, your investment drops to zero.

## Licensed vs. Unauthorised: A Comparison for Land Sellers

If you are reinvesting money from a land acquisition, you are often looking for safety. You want to preserve the wealth for the next generation. Here is the reality of what you are buying:

| Feature | Licensed Colony (GMADA/PUDA/Private Approved) | Unauthorised Colony (Illegal/Kacha) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Legal Status** | Fully compliant with Punjab Apartment & Property Regulation Act (PAPRA) | Illegal; violation of Land Use laws |
| **Bank Finance** | 70–80% loan available from all major banks | Zero bank funding available |
| **Infrastructure** | Standardized roads, sewers, storm water, streetlights | Sub-standard or non-existent utilities |
| **RERA Protection** | Full legal recourse for delays or fraud | No RERA protection; you are on your own |
| **Resale Speed** | High (large pool of bank-financed buyers) | Very Low (limited to cash-only risk-takers) |
| **Price Appreciation** | Driven by market growth and infrastructure | Stagnant or volatile; prone to sudden drops |
| **Utility Connections** | Individual PSPCL and Water meters guaranteed | Often shared or illegal connections |

## What buyers discover too late: The "Internal Development" trap

In an unauthorised colony, the developer (often just a small-time local land-pooler) promises "all facilities." But who maintains them? In a licensed colony, the developer is legally bound to provide electricity via PSPCL and water via the Municipal Committee or GMADA. 

I’ve handled cases where buyers in unauthorised colonies found that their "electricity connection" was actually a single commercial meter shared by fifty houses, or that the "sewage system" was just a hole in the ground that overflowed every monsoon. To fix these issues, the government eventually asks for "Regularization Charges." I’ve seen these charges run into lakhs of rupees per plot—effectively erasing any "saving" the buyer made at the start.

## The "Regularization" Myth: Why It Isn't a Solution

Many dealers will tell you, "Don't worry, the government will regularize this colony soon." This is a dangerous gamble. Even if a regularization policy is announced, it only "legalizes" the illegal status—it does not create space for wide roads or parks where houses have already been built. 

Regularization usually involves paying a compounding fee (Development Charges) that can be as high as 10-20% of the property value. Even after paying, the colony remains a "B-grade" settlement. It will never have the appreciation potential of an [Aerocity](/blog/aerocity-mohali-commercial-property-2026) or an [IT City](/blog/kharar-it-city-property-investment-2026) because the fundamental planning is broken. In my years of navigating these approvals, I've seen that once a colony is built incorrectly, it stays that way forever.

## The Role of GMADA and PUDA in Approval

Understanding the [GMADA framework](/blog/gmada-mohali-explained-guide) is critical here. GMADA isn't just a tax collector; they are the planners. When they license a project, they ensure that the Sector has enough capacity for the population. They check the water table, the road widths for fire engines, and the distance from high-tension wires. 

An unauthorised colony ignores all of this. I've seen projects sold right under high-voltage lines or on low-lying land that floods every year. The developer doesn't care—they will be gone by the time the first rain hits. You, the buyer, are the one left holding the bag.
