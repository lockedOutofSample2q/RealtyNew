# Mohali SCO Investment Due Diligence: What Separates a Good Commercial Unit From a 10-Year Problem

To separate a high-yielding Mohali SCO (Shop-cum-Office) from a "10-year problem" that drains your capital, you must look past the brochure and evaluate four non-negotiable criteria: frontage width, parking-to-plot ratio, immediate catchment density, and floor-wise leasability. A successful SCO investment in 2026 is defined by a minimum 15-foot frontage, a dedicated parking corridor that doesn't choke during peak hours, and a location within a 2-kilometer radius of at least 5,000 residential units. Units that lack these fundamentals—regardless of how "premium" the developer claims the project is—often suffer from permanent 3rd-floor vacancy and ground-floor rental stagnation. In Mohali's current market, the difference between a 6% yield and a 2% struggle is determined entirely by whether the unit serves a functional retail need or just fills a slot on a developer's layout map.

## What are the primary criteria for SCO due diligence in Mohali?

When I evaluate a commercial unit, I don't start with the price. I start with the "why." Why would a brand pay you ₹2 lakh a month to be here? In my experience managing project documentation and sales for developers, I’ve seen how units are "manufactured" to fit a plot of land rather than designed for a tenant’s business.

The primary criteria for your due diligence must be:
1. **Frontage Width:** Narrow units are retail killers. If your unit is less than 15 feet wide, you are limiting your tenant pool to small service providers rather than high-street brands.
2. **Parking Capacity:** If a customer can't park, they won't stop. It is that simple. Evaluate the total parking spots against the total number of SCO units in the project.
3. **Catchment Area:** Is the residential population nearby already living there, or is it "promised" for 2029? For an SCO to thrive, it needs immediate footfall.
4. **Leasability by Floor:** You must have a clear strategy for the upper floors. While the ground floor carries the value, the upper floors determine the total ROI.

## Frontage and Visibility: Why 15 Feet is the Minimum You Should Accept

In the world of commercial real estate, frontage is currency. I have seen investors buy narrow units because they were "budget-friendly," only to find that no reputable brand wants to rent them. High-street retail brands—the ones who pay the highest rents—require visibility for their signage and enough glass area for window displays.

A 15-foot frontage is the bare minimum for a standard SCO. Anything wider, like the 16.5-foot or 18-foot units found in some [Aerocity Mohali commercial property](/blog/aerocity-mohali-commercial-property-2026) corridors, commands a significant rental premium. If you are looking at a unit with a 12-foot frontage, you are buying a 10-year problem. You will be stuck with low-rent tenants who don't care about branding, and your appreciation will be capped because the "big fish" will always choose your neighbor's wider unit.

## The Parking-to-Plot Ratio: The Silent Killer of Commercial Returns

I’ve walked through many older commercial markets in Mohali, like Phase 3B2 or Phase 7, and I’ve seen the same pattern: great location, zero parking. This leads to what I call "market stagnation." Customers eventually get tired of the chaos and move to newer, more organized corridors like Airport Road or IT City.

During your due diligence, look at the project's site plan. Is there a dedicated parking corridor in front of the SCOs? Is there additional surface parking nearby? In some of the newer developments we’ve handled, the parking ratio is much healthier, but you must verify this yourself. Don't take the salesperson's word for it. Measure the distance from the road to the shop front. If the developer has squeezed the parking to fit more units, walk away. A commercial unit without accessible parking is just an expensive storage space.

## Ground Floor vs Upper Floors: Understanding the 3x Rental Gap

Many CXO-level investors I advise make the mistake of calculating their ROI based on a flat rental average across all floors. This is a dangerous assumption. In Mohali, the ground floor typically earns 3 to 4 times more than the 2nd or 3rd floors.

If your ground floor rents for ₹150 per sq. ft., your 3rd floor might struggle to get ₹40. Why? Because accessibility matters. Unless the building has a high-quality, high-speed lift that is well-maintained, the upper floors will always have high vacancy rates. In older "staircase-only" buildings, the 3rd floor is often permanently vacant or occupied by low-paying offices that don't drive value. ## Catchment Area Analysis: Residential Density vs Office Footfall

An SCO does not exist in a vacuum. It lives off the people around it, and you must distinguish between "daytime footfall" and "evening footfall." Before you book a commercial unit, drive 2 kilometers in every direction. Count the number of occupied residential towers and assess the office density.

In corridors like Aerocity or the IT City belt, the catchment is a mix of high-income corporate employees and residential families. A project like the commercial clusters on Airport Road succeeded because it serves as a transit hub and a local market for the massive residential belt of Sector 76, 77, and 82. During your due diligence, look for "anchors"—is there a major hospital, a university, or a large IT park nearby? These anchors provide a baseline of footfall that sustains the market even during economic dips. If you are buying an SCO in a "future" sector where the houses aren't even built yet, you are paying 2026 prices for 2030 footfall. That is not an investment; that is a gamble on someone else’s construction timeline.

## Target Tenant Mix: Who Is Actually Going to Pay Your Rent?

A common mistake I see among CXO investors is buying an SCO without a target tenant profile. An SCO in a residential sector should be evaluated differently than one on a transit corridor. You need to ask: is this unit suitable for F&B (Food and Beverage), retail, or professional services like clinics and law firms?

1. **F&B Requirements:** These tenants need high electrical loads, gas bank space, and excellent drainage. If the developer hasn't provisioned for these, you are losing out on the highest-paying category of tenants.
2. **Retail Brands:** As we discussed, these need frontage. They also need "clean" floor plates without too many internal columns.
3. **Professional Services:** These usually occupy the 1st and 2nd floors. They care about the "address" and the quality of the common lobby.

If your SCO is located in a cluster that already has 50 other units offering the same thing, your "leasability" drops. Part of your due diligence should be a "competitor map." What is missing in that specific micro-market? If the area lacks a high-end pharmacy or a quality coffee shop, and your unit has the frontage to support it, your ROI is already secured.

## Lift vs Stairs: Why the 3rd Floor Often Stays Vacant in Older Corridors

I've seen this play out in dozens of transactions. A buyer thinks they are getting a great deal on a 3rd floor unit in a secondary market. Then they realize there is no lift, or the lift is a "cargo" style that customers hate. In today's market, if your SCO doesn't have a modern, passenger-ready lift service, your top two floors are essentially dead weight.

When I was on the developer side, we would emphasize the "ancillary" features like lift quality because we knew it made the upper floors leasable. If the developer is cutting corners on the common areas or the lift specifications, it’s a red flag. You aren't just buying a box of bricks; you are buying a business environment. If the environment is poor, the tenant quality will be poor.

## Aerocity vs Airport Road: A Comparative Due Diligence

This is a question I get asked often. Aerocity has the "brand" name, but Airport Road has the "vision." Aerocity SCOs are often priced at a significant premium because of their proximity to the airport and hotel clusters. However, the footfall in many Aerocity blocks is still developing.

Airport Road, specifically around Sector 82, saw units that were ₹3–4 crore a few years ago trade at ₹12–16 crore today. The due diligence lesson here is about "vision investing." The buyers who made money didn't buy when it was crowded; they bought when the infrastructure (the IT corridor, the airport expansion) was visible but not yet priced in. ## What specific documents should you verify before an SCO booking?

Before you sign an allotment letter, you must verify the following documents:
- **RERA Punjab Registration:** Every commercial project must be registered. Check for any pending complaints or extensions as outlined in our [Mohali real estate FAQ 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-faq-2026).
- **Approved Layout Map:** Ensure the unit you are buying matches the layout approved by GMADA or PUDA.
- **CLU (Change of Land Use):** Verify that the land is legally designated for commercial use.
- **Title Clarity:** Is the land owned by the developer or is it a joint venture? This impacts your long-term security.
- **Maintenance Agreement:** Commercial maintenance can be expensive. Understand what you are paying for—security, parking management, and common area lighting are essentials.

## How does the new corridor expansion impact SCO leasability?

Mohali is expanding toward the Rajpura and Banur belt, driven by the Bharatmala project. This expansion is creating new commercial "nodes." If you buy in a node that is on a major transit route, your leasability is driven by both local residents and transit travelers. This is a key factor when comparing [Mohali commercial vs residential investment](/blog/mohali-commercial-vs-residential-investment-2026).

However, be careful. As more supply enters the market, the older, poorly-designed SCOs will see their rentals fall. This is why the [developer due diligence checklist](/blog/mohali-developer-project-due-diligence-checklist) is so critical. You want a unit that stays relevant even when 500 more units are built nearby.
