Successful SCO investment due diligence in Mohali requires verifying four non-negotiable pillars: high-visibility frontage (minimum 16 to 22 feet), dedicated parking depth (not just roadside space), a 3 kilometer catchment density of over 10,000 households, and a clear "Basement plus Ground plus 3 or 4" configuration with independent access. A good commercial unit in Mohali is defined by its ability to attract anchor tenants like banks, healthcare clinics, or national retail chains that prioritize ground floor visibility. Conversely, a 10-year problem unit is often found in internal blocks with poor frontage, shared parking that chokes footfall, or areas where the residential catchment is too sparse to support high-ticket rentals. To ensure long term viability, investors must look beyond current GMADA auction prices and evaluate the project through the lens of tenant psychology and future infrastructure corridors like the Bharatmala Road or the Airport expansion.

## The Transformation of the Mohali SCO Landscape

Shop-cum-Office (SCO) units have become the most sought after asset class in the Mohali real estate market. Traditionally, commercial activity was confined to phase-wise markets like Phase 7 or 3B2. However, the expansion of Greater Mohali through Aerocity, IT City, and the PR7 Airport Road has shifted the center of gravity. What used to be available for 3 to 4 crore several years ago in sectors like Sector 82 now trades between 12 and 16 crore.

This massive appreciation has led many Corporate CXOs and HNI investors to jump into the market without performing deep due diligence. They often buy based on the developer's brochure rather than the ground reality of commercial operations. In my experience at Realty Holding and Management Consultants, the difference between a high-yield asset and a stagnant block of concrete lies in the details that a typical broker will never mention.

## Physical Parameters: Frontage and Visibility

In commercial real estate, frontage is everything. In Mohali, many private developers are squeezing plots to maximize numbers, leading to SCOs with narrow frontages of 12 to 14 feet. This is a red flag. A national brand or a high-end showroom typically requires a minimum frontage of 16.5 feet, ideally 20 to 22 feet, to create an impactful display window.

Narrow units create a tunnel effect. They are harder to lease because the display area is limited, and the floor plate becomes inefficient. When conducting your due diligence, measure the actual clear frontage from pillar to pillar. Units that sit on corners or face the main dividing roads always command a premium because their visibility is doubled. If you buy a unit in the rear block of a commercial complex, you are essentially betting that the front blocks will generate enough spillover footfall to sustain your tenant. That is a high-risk bet that rarely pays off in the first five years.



## The Silent Deal-Breaker: Parking and Approach

If a customer cannot park their car within 50 meters of the entrance, they will not visit the shop. It is that simple. Many commercial projects in Mohali, especially on the PR7 Airport Road, suffer from "parking fatigue." Developers often show "ample parking" on paper, but they include roadside spaces that are eventually restricted by traffic police or the Municipal Committee.

Check for dedicated basement parking or wide service lanes. A project where the ratio of SCO units to parking slots is skewed will see its rental value drop as the area matures. For instance, some blocks in Aerocity have seen stagnant rentals despite high capital appreciation because the congestion makes it impossible for high-spending customers to visit. If you are evaluating a specific project, visit the site during peak evening hours. If the cars are double-parked now, the situation will be catastrophic when the occupancy reaches 80 percent.

## Catchment Area Analysis: The 3km Rule

A commercial unit does not exist in a vacuum. Its value is a direct reflection of the residents living within a 3 to 5 kilometer radius. This is what we call the catchment area. For an SCO to be successful, it needs a healthy mix of residential density.

Take the example of IT City versus Aerocity. IT City has massive corporate campuses which provide daytime footfall, but the evening footfall depends on the nearby residential sectors. If those sectors are still under construction or have low occupancy, your SCO will struggle to find tenants for the upper floors. A Good Commercial Unit has a "24-hour catchment" — office goers during the day and residential families in the evening.

I often discuss this on our YouTube channel @Amritrealty: if you move without vision, you will buy at the wrong price. Investing in an SCO where the residential catchment is five years away means you will be carrying the interest cost or the loss of rental income for half a decade. That is the definition of a 10-year problem.

## Tenant Psychology: Who Pays the Rent?

Before you sign the cheque, ask yourself: "Who is the ideal tenant for this space?" A Ground Floor plus Basement is perfect for retail, pharmacies, or food outlets. The upper floors (1st, 2nd, and 3rd) are typically leased by dental clinics, CA firms, IT startups, or boutique offices.

However, if your SCO does not have a provision for a lift or has a very narrow staircase, you have effectively killed the rental potential of the upper floors. Modern corporate tenants in Mohali expect professional glass facades, independent electricity meters for each floor, and high-speed elevator access. If your unit lacks these, you will be forced to lease to low-paying tenants who do not maintain the property, leading to faster depreciation of the building's physical state.

## Stagnation vs. Appreciation Corridors

In Mohali, areas where expansion is possible see appreciation. Areas where expansion is not possible see stagnation. Old markets like Phase 3B2 have reached their peak. The prices are high, but the rental yields are compressed because there is no room for new infrastructure or parking expansion.

New corridors like the PR7 Airport Road or the upcoming Bharatmala Road are where the vision lies. However, even within these corridors, not every block is equal. Due diligence involves checking the GMADA Master Plan to see if a new flyover or a median cut will eventually block the approach to your SCO. A unit that is easily accessible today might become a "dead pocket" if a one-way traffic system is implemented in the future.

## Legal and Regulatory Checklist

No amount of good frontage can save you from a legal title dispute. Your due diligence checklist must include:
1. **RERA Punjab Verification:** Check the project's RERA number for any pending complaints regarding possession or development.
2. **GMADA/PUDA Approvals:** Ensure the Change of Land Use (CLU) and the Layout Plan are approved. "Applied for approval" is not the same as "Approved."
3. **Occupation Certificate (OC):** For ready units, never buy without verifying the OC. Without it, you cannot get legal electricity connections from PSPCL or apply for a trade license.
4. **Property Tax Clearance:** Ensure the seller has a No Dues Certificate (NDC) from the Municipal Committee.



## Case Study: The Sector 82 Success Story

A few years ago, we helped a client identify an SCO unit in the JLPL area of Sector 82. While others were buying interior plots because they were 20 percent cheaper, we insisted on a main-road facing unit despite the higher premium. At that time, the price was around 7 crore. Today, that same unit is valued at over 14 crore and is leased to a national banking chain.

The reason for this success was not luck. It was the verification of the frontage, the wide service lane for parking, and the realization that the Airport expansion would make this the primary commercial gateway. The cheaper interior plots, meanwhile, are still struggling with 40 percent occupancy and significantly lower rental yields. This is the difference between an asset and a liability.
