# Maintenance Charges in Mohali Luxury Projects: What's Fair, What's Excessive, and What You're Actually Paying For

If you are buying a luxury flat in Mohali, the sticker price is only the first number you need to evaluate. The recurring cost that determines your long-term satisfaction—and your property’s resale value—is the maintenance charge. In Mohali’s current luxury landscape, a fair maintenance charge typically ranges between ₹3 and ₹8 per square foot (psf) per month. For a 2,500 sq. ft. apartment, this means an annual commitment ranging from ₹90,000 to ₹2.4 Lakhs. Whether this is "fair" or "excessive" depends entirely on the service tier: ₹3-4 psf is standard for basic security and upkeep; ₹5-6 psf covers landscaping and active amenities; while ₹7-8 psf is the benchmark for true high-end facilities management including concierge, gym, and pool operations. Anything above ₹8 psf requires a very specific, high-end justification, or it may signal inefficiency or a hidden profit center for the developer.

## What are the typical maintenance price brackets in Mohali?

In my years of navigating project approvals and managing developments from the inside, I’ve seen how maintenance budgets are built. They aren't pulled out of thin air; they are a reflection of the "manpower vs. machinery" ratio of a project. In Mohali, we generally see three tiers of maintenance pricing:

### Tier 1: The Basic Standard (₹3–4 per sq. ft.)
At this price point, you are paying for the essentials. This includes 24/7 security at the main gate, daily cleaning of common corridors, lift maintenance (AMCs), and basic lighting in shared areas. At this level, don't expect a functional clubhouse or a sparkling swimming pool every day. This is the "upkeep" tier, common in older GMADA-allotted societies or mid-range private projects where the focus is on utility rather than luxury lifestyle.

### Tier 2: The Lifestyle Intermediate (₹5–6 per sq. ft.)
This is where most "luxury" projects in sectors like 82A or the Airport Road belt start. Beyond the basics, this covers professional landscaping (not just a few potted plants), periodic pesticide control in common areas, and the operation of basic amenities like a community hall or a small gym. Security is usually more robust, often including CCTV monitoring and gated access control for individual blocks.

### Tier 3: The Full-Service Luxury (₹7–8 per sq. ft.)
For high-HNI projects, this is the benchmark. This tier delivers a "hotel-like" experience. You are paying for a professional Facilities Management (FM) team, concierge services, 24/7 power backup maintenance, a fully operational swimming pool with lifeguards, a high-spec gym with trainers, and extensive manicured gardens. When you pay ₹8 psf, you aren't just paying for cleaning; you are paying for the preservation of a lifestyle.

## Why does the difference between GMADA and private maintenance matter?

One of the most common questions I get during a [mohali real estate guide 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026) consultation is why GMADA-sector maintenance feels cheaper than private developer maintenance. The answer lies in the infrastructure ownership. 

In a GMADA society, the external infrastructure—roads, streetlights, and main sewerage—is maintained by the government authority. The residents only pay for what happens inside their gated boundary. In a private "licensed colony," the developer is often responsible for the internal road network, the sewage treatment plant (STP), and even the backup power infrastructure for the entire township. This adds a layer of "hidden" maintenance costs that buyers often overlook until they see the first bill. I always advise buyers to read the [mohali property buyer protection guide](/blog/mohali-property-buyer-protection-guide) to understand exactly where the developer's responsibility ends and the residents' begins.

## What should you look for in a Maintenance Agreement?

Before you sign on the dotted line, you must ask for the draft Maintenance Agreement. As a former developer, I can tell you that the devil is always in the definitions. 

1. **The Scope of Work:** Does "security" mean two guards at the gate, or a multi-layered system with bouncers and digital monitoring? 
2. **Escalation Clause:** Most agreements include a clause that allows the developer or the RWA to increase charges by 10-15% annually or bi-annually. Ensure this is capped or linked to actual inflation.
3. **The Sinking Fund:** This is separate from your monthly charges. It’s a corpus for major future repairs (like repainting the entire building or replacing a lift). Ensure this is being kept in a separate, audited escrow account.
4. **GST Applicability:** Remember that if your monthly maintenance exceeds ₹7,500, you are liable for 18% GST on the entire amount. This can significantly bump up your real outgo.

## How is the Maintenance Corpus managed?

When you buy a luxury flat, you usually pay a one-time "Interest-Free Maintenance Security" (IFMS) or a maintenance corpus. This can be a substantial amount, often ranging from ₹2 Lakhs to ₹5 Lakhs depending on the size of the flat. 

In a transparent setup, this corpus is parked in a fixed deposit, and only the interest earned is used to offset maintenance costs. However, I’ve seen cases where developers use this corpus to fund their own cash flow gaps. A red flag is when a developer refuses to provide an audited statement of the maintenance fund. Under RERA Punjab, this fund must be handed over to the Residents' Welfare Association (RWA) once the project is fully possession-ready and the developer exits. If the developer is lingering on to the corpus without handing over the project to an RWA, it’s time to start asking hard questions.

## What is the specific breakdown of maintenance costs in a luxury project?

To understand if you are being overcharged, you must look at the four pillars of a maintenance budget. In a typical Mohali luxury project, the costs are usually distributed as follows:

### 1. Staffing and Manpower (40–50% of the budget)
This is the largest chunk. For a "full-service" project at ₹7–8 psf, you aren't just paying for a few guards. You are paying for a Facilities Manager, electrical and plumbing technicians on standby, specialized pool cleaners, a horticulture team for the landscaped gardens, and a multi-layered security detail. In high-end projects, the cost of "housekeeping" also includes specialized cleaning for Italian marble in lobbies and high-spec glass in common areas.

### 2. Utilities and Electricity (20–25% of the budget)
This covers the "Common Area Power" (CAP). Lighting up a massive basement, operating multiple high-speed lifts, and running a sewage treatment plant (STP) 24/7 consumes significant power. In Mohali, where summer temperatures hit 45°C, the cost of keeping common areas like the lobby or clubhouse air-conditioned can cause maintenance bills to spike if not managed efficiently.

### 3. Annual Maintenance Contracts (AMCs) and Repairs (15–20% of the budget)
Lifts, firefighting systems, water softeners, and DG sets all require professional AMCs with the original manufacturers. A luxury project that skimps on AMCs to "save" money today is a project that will face catastrophic failure in five years. I always tell my clients to ask for the list of AMC partners—if the lifts aren't being maintained by the OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer), it’s a red flag.

### 4. Administrative and Statutory Costs (5–10% of the budget)
This includes the property tax for common areas, insurance for the building structure, auditing fees, and the management fee charged by the professional agency running the show.

## The Transition: From Developer-Managed to RWA-Managed

The most volatile period for any Mohali project is the handover from the developer to the Residents’ Welfare Association (RWA). As a developer, I’ve sat on both sides of this table. 

Initially, the developer manages maintenance because they still have unsold inventory and want the project to look its best. However, their goal is often to keep the "quoted" maintenance low to attract buyers. Once the residents take over (the RWA), they often discover that the actual cost of running the facilities is much higher than what the developer was charging. This leads to "maintenance shocks" where charges might jump from ₹4 to ₹6 psf overnight.

To avoid this, look for a "Transitional Management" clause. A good developer will involve a core committee of residents at least six months before the official handover, giving them access to the books and training them on the technical systems like the STP and DG sync panels. If the developer is being secretive about the running costs, the RWA is likely inheriting a financial mess.

## How to negotiate maintenance clauses before you buy

Most buyers think maintenance is non-negotiable. While the "rate" psf is usually fixed for the whole project, the *terms* are where you have leverage. 

- **The Grace Period:** Negotiate for a "free maintenance" period of 12 to 24 months as part of your purchase deal. For a large luxury flat, this can save you ₹2–4 Lakhs.
- **Occupancy-Linked Charges:** If you are buying an investment property that will remain vacant, negotiate for a "vacant unit discount." Some projects allow a 25-50% discount on the service portion of maintenance if the unit is unoccupied, though you still pay for fixed costs like security and sinking funds.
- **The "Locked-In" Rate:** Ask the developer to guarantee the maintenance rate for the first three years post-possession. This protects you from the "teaser rate" trap where costs are artificially kept low during the sales phase only to explode later.

## Maintenance Corpus: The Sinking Fund Strategy

The Maintenance Corpus or Sinking Fund is the lifeblood of a building's future. In Mohali, most luxury projects collect a one-time IFMS (Interest-Free Maintenance Security). 

A fair strategy—one I practiced as a developer—is to use only the interest from this corpus for general upkeep, keeping the principal amount intact for major structural repairs or equipment replacements (like replacing a DG set after 15 years). If a developer tells you that the monthly maintenance is "free" because it’s being adjusted from the corpus, be very careful. They are essentially eating into the capital that should be protecting your building's long-term integrity. Within five years, that corpus will be gone, and the residents will be hit with a massive bill for basic repairs.

## GMADA Society Maintenance vs. Private Luxury Society

The distinction is sharp. In a GMADA-developed sector (like the older parts of Sector 70 or 71), maintenance is often handled by a local committee with very low overheads. You might pay ₹1,000 to ₹2,000 a month. But in a private luxury society on Airport Road or in New Chandigarh, you are paying for "Facilities Management." 

The difference is accountability. In a private setup, if a lift stops working, there is a dedicated FM manager you can call. In a GMADA setup, you might be waiting for the next committee meeting to approve the repair funds. For HNIs and CXOs, the premium for private maintenance is usually a trade-off for their own time and peace of mind.

## What is the "Fair" way to calculate these costs?

A fair calculation should be based on the actual expenditure plus a reasonable 10-15% management fee for the agency. When I advise HNIs on luxury purchases, I tell them to look at the "Per Unit Efficiency." A project with 500 flats can distribute the cost of a swimming pool much better than a boutique project with only 20 flats. In a boutique luxury project, your maintenance will naturally be higher because the "per head" cost of high-end security and facilities management is steeper.
