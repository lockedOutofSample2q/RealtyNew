# Project Site Visit Checklist for Mohali: What to Look for That Brochures Never Show You

Before you commit, read our [Mohali Real Estate Guide 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026) for the complete legal framework. What exactly should you look for during a Mohali project site visit? A project site visit in Mohali should verify six critical elements that brochures hide. You must check the actual construction pace against the promised timeline by counting completed floors. Observe the material quality, specifically the visible RCC and brickwork. Evaluate site hygiene by noting whether equipment and labor are active or idle. Inspect common area progress like lift shafts, lobbies, and parking basements. Assess the surrounding infrastructure reality, including road connectivity and neighborhood development. Finally, speak directly to existing residents if any earlier phase is already delivered. Document everything with photographs. A physical site visit confirms if the developer has the cash flow and intent to deliver. Never rely solely on a sample flat or a 3D rendering. If the ground reality does not match the sales pitch, walk away. 

## Why Is a Physical Site Visit Mandatory Before Buying Mohali Property?

Brochures sell a vision, but a physical site visit reveals the developer's actual financial health and intent to deliver. When I was managing project documentation and sales as a developer, I saw firsthand how marketing materials can diverge from ground reality. Sales teams are trained to show you the fully furnished sample flat. They want you to focus on the Italian marble, the modular kitchen, and the false ceiling lighting. They do not want you looking at the raw structure, the exposed wiring, or the quality of the cement being poured into the foundation.

A site visit is your only opportunity to verify if the project exists beyond paper and if the construction quality justifies your hard-earned money. Many buyers make the mistake of booking purely based on a presentation in an air-conditioned office, trusting a glossy printed booklet. You need to step into the dust. You need to see how the building is being put together from the ground up. This is the foundation of any serious [developer and project due diligence checklist](/blog/mohali-developer-project-due-diligence-checklist). If you skip this step, you are gambling with your capital on a promise rather than a proven product.

## How Can You Verify the Actual Construction Pace Against the Promised Timeline?

You verify construction pace by counting the number of completed slabs and comparing it to the developer's RERA filing and your proposed payment plan. Sales staff will always tell you that construction is moving in full swing. Your job is to verify that claim with your own eyes, objectively, without the influence of a sales pitch.

If the builder claims possession will be handed over in twelve months, but only two floors are cast on a ten-story tower, the math simply does not align. A standard floor slab takes roughly two to three weeks to cast properly under ideal conditions, allowing for curing time. You must calculate the remaining work based on this reality. Are the internal walls built? Is the plastering done? Is the plumbing and electrical wiring initiated? Have the window frames been installed? 

Compare what you see on the ground with the official updates. Ask for the construction schedule and match it against the current state. If you want to understand how this ties into a builder's history, read our guide on how to [check a developer's delivery track record](/blog/mohali-developer-delivery-track-record-check). Do not accept vague assurances about speeding up the work next month by adding more shifts. Construction delays are the first and most reliable indicator of financial trouble. When the money stops flowing, the construction stops moving.

## What Specific Material Quality and Site Hygiene Indicators Should You Check?

Look beyond the sample flat. You need to inspect the raw materials being used on the actual site, as these determine the structural integrity of your future home. Check the quality of the bricks. Are they using high-quality red bricks, or are they using cheap, poorly manufactured fly ash bricks that crumble easily? Look closely at the RCC (Reinforced Cement Concrete) work. Is the concrete finish smooth and uniform, or are there honeycombs, air pockets, and exposed rusting rebar? Poorly finished concrete indicates rushed work, low-grade formwork, and a severe lack of quality control by the site engineers.

Site hygiene is equally telling. It gives you a direct, unfiltered look into the developer's operational efficiency and cash flow. An active, well-funded site has organized material storage. The steel rebars are stacked properly, elevated from the mud to prevent rusting. The cement bags are covered and protected from moisture. The cranes are active and moving. There is a large, visible labor force working systematically across different areas of the project. 

Conversely, an idle site with scattered materials, rusted iron rods sinking into the dirt, uncollected debris, and minimal labor is a massive warning sign. It shows that contractors are either not being paid on time, the supply chain is broken, or the developer lacks fundamental project management skills. 

## What Does Common Area Progress Tell You About the Developer's Financial Health?

Developers often prioritize finishing individual flats to trigger construction-linked payment milestones from buyers. They will push hard to complete the basic structure and internal plastering of your apartment so they can legally demand the next installment of your payment plan. However, they frequently neglect the common areas because finishing these spaces does not immediately trigger a payment from you.

A financially healthy developer builds lobbies, lift shafts, staircases, and basement parking concurrently with the apartments. They understand that the project is an integrated whole. When you visit the site, look closely at these shared spaces. If the individual flats look near completion but the basement is flooded with rainwater, the lift shafts are empty dark voids with no guide rails installed, and the staircases lack basic finishing or safety railings, the developer is facing cash flow stress. They are trying to extract money from buyers without spending on the essential infrastructure required to make the building habitable and safe.

This is a critical part of the [complete Mohali real estate guide for 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026). You are not just buying an isolated apartment. You are buying into a society. If the common infrastructure is delayed, your possession will be delayed, regardless of how finished your specific unit looks. The local authorities will not grant an Occupancy Certificate (OC) if the fire safety systems in the common areas are incomplete.

## How Do You Evaluate the Surrounding Infrastructure Reality of a Project?

You evaluate surrounding infrastructure by driving the approach roads yourself. Do not rely on the map in the brochure or the large hoarding at the entrance. Brochures are notorious for compressing distances and illustrating wide, tree-lined avenues that do not actually exist. They will show an airport five minutes away when, in reality, it requires navigating through heavy traffic and narrow village roads.

Check if the promised connectivity to Airport Road, PR-7, or other major highways is a reality or just a distant government proposal that may take a decade to materialize. Look for the presence of basic utilities. Is there functioning drainage, or does the access road flood during the monsoon? Are there streetlights installed and working? How far is the nearest operational commercial market, pharmacy, or school? 

If the project is located in an underdeveloped sector, you need to assess the genuine timeline for that area's growth. Some projects look beautiful inside their boundary walls but are surrounded by vacant, unlit agricultural land with no proper approach road. This heavily impacts both your daily living experience, the safety of your family, and your future resale value. ## Who Should You Speak to During Your Site Visit?

Do not just speak to the sales staff. Their job is to manage your perception, highlight the positives, and close the deal quickly. They have a financial incentive to tell you what you want to hear. If the project has an already delivered phase, you must speak to the existing residents. They are your most valuable, unbiased source of truth.

Ask them direct questions. How was their possession experience? Was it delayed by months or years? Are the water and electricity supplies consistent, or do they rely heavily on expensive diesel generators? Is the maintenance team responsive to complaints, or do they ignore issues once the maintenance corpus is collected? Are there any hidden charges that appeared after possession, such as unexpected club membership fees or inflated common area electricity bills? 

If the project is entirely under construction and has no delivered phases, try speaking to the contractors or laborers on site, or visit a local tea stall near the gate. Ask if the work has ever stopped for long periods. Ask if there are usually this many workers on site, or if they were brought in just for the weekend when prospective buyers visit. ## What Are the Biggest Red Flags to Walk Away From?

There are certain observations during a site visit that should make you walk away immediately, regardless of the attractive pricing, the heavy discounts offered by the sales team, or the pressure to book before prices supposedly rise. 

First, an inactive site. If you visit on a Tuesday afternoon and there are only ten laborers working on a massive four-tower project, the developer is clearly out of money. Construction requires a massive, continuous deployment of labor.

Second, sales staff who refuse to let you see the actual construction area. If they insist you only sit in the office and look at the sample flat, claiming "safety reasons," they are hiding poor construction quality or stalled work. A confident developer will provide a hard hat and proudly walk you through the site.

Third, mismatched claims about RERA approvals and actual ground progress. If the RERA website shows 20% completion but the sales team claims they are at 60% and demanding payment accordingly, someone is lying. Always verify the RERA registration number and check the official quarterly updates filed by the developer.

Fourth, existing residents who complain about fundamental structural issues like severe water seepage, cracking walls, or persistent lift failures. You cannot fix a badly built structure after the fact. Cosmetic issues can be repaired, but structural flaws will haunt you for as long as you own the property.

Document everything during your visit. Take photographs of the construction pace, the material quality, the common areas, and any red flags you notice. These photos give you a baseline to compare against when you visit again next month. 

Buying property in Mohali requires looking past the polished marketing and the confident sales pitches. It requires asking hard questions, demanding proof, and verifying the answers with your own eyes on the ground. If the developer cannot withstand a basic site inspection, they do not deserve your capital. Your investment is too significant to base on a brochure.
