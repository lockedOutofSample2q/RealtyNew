# Prelaunch Pricing in Mohali: How to Tell If the 'Early Bird Price' Is Actually a Discount

![Calculating real cost and discounts for prelaunch property in Mohali](/images/blog/070-prelaunch-pricing-mohali-real-cost-calculation.jpg)

To tell if a prelaunch price in Mohali is a genuine discount, you must look past the "base price" and calculate the total all-in cost per usable square foot. A real prelaunch opportunity should be at least 15% to 20% cheaper than the current registered sale prices of comparable ready-to-move properties in the same micro-market. Most "early bird" offers are simply marketing frames where the base price is kept low to attract interest, while the developer recovers the margin through mandatory add-ons like EDC, IDC, PLC, and high car parking charges. To verify the truth, you should benchmark the offer against actual registered sale deeds on the IGRS Punjab portal for the same sector. If the gap between the prelaunch all-in cost and the market resale price is less than 10%, you are not getting a discount: you are simply being paid a very small premium to take on the developer’s construction and approval risk.

## What does 'All-In' actually mean in a Mohali prelaunch?

In my years on the developer side, I’ve seen how pricing sheets are designed. The number you see on the brochure or the one the sales agent quotes over the phone is almost never what you will actually pay. For an HNI buyer looking at a ₹3 crore or ₹5 crore ticket size, even a 5% "hidden" cost is a significant amount of money that could have been invested elsewhere. When a developer says "prelaunch," they are often selling a vision that hasn't even cleared all its regulatory hurdles yet.

When evaluating a prelaunch, you must build your own "Real Cost Sheet." Start with the base price, then add the following components:

1. **External Development Charges (EDC) & IDC:** These are government-mandated, but developers often quote them separately. In Mohali, these can range from ₹200 to ₹400 per sq. ft. Don't let them tell you these are "optional" or "to be decided": they are fixed by the authority.
2. **Preferential Location Charges (PLC):** If your unit faces a park, a wide road, or a corner, expect a 5% to 10% hike on the base price. In some luxury projects in Sector 82A, PLC can add upwards of ₹15-20 lakhs to the cost.
3. **Floor Rise Charges:** In high-rise luxury projects, the price often increases by ₹50 to ₹150 per sq. ft. for every floor you go up. By the 15th floor, your "discount" has completely evaporated.
4. **Car Parking:** In premium projects, a single basement spot can cost ₹3 lakh to ₹6 lakh. If you are an HNI with multiple vehicles, and you need three spots, that’s ₹15-18 lakhs right there.
5. **Club Membership & Security Deposits:** Often an upfront charge of ₹1 lakh to ₹5 lakh.
6. **GST:** Remember, under-construction property attracts 5% GST, whereas ready-to-move property (with a Completion Certificate) has zero GST. This 5% alone often wipes out half the "prelaunch discount."

Having managed project documentation for approvals across bodies like PUDA, PSPCL, and the Municipal Committee, I know that these costs are fixed for the developer. When they offer a "prelaunch discount," they are usually just squeezing the base price while keeping these add-ons firm. ## How do you benchmark prelaunch prices against market reality?

I often tell my clients over chai: "Don't listen to what the broker says the market price is. Look at what the government says people actually paid." The market in Mohali is currently flooded with "aspirational" pricing: prices that exist on brochures but not on sale deeds.

The most honest data point in Mohali real estate is the IGRS Punjab registry data. If a developer is offering a prelaunch at ₹8,500 per sq. ft. all-in, and the [Sector 82A price history](/blog/sector-82a-mohali-property-price-2026) shows that registered sales in a nearby, completed project are happening at ₹9,200 per sq. ft., your "discount" is only about 7.6%.

Is a 7.6% discount worth a 3-year wait and the risk of the project being stalled? Probably not. As someone who holds AMFI and NCFM certifications, I look at real estate through the lens of capital markets. If you park that same money in a liquid fund or a structured portfolio, you might earn more than that 7% with zero delivery risk.

In this real-world example, the marketed "30% cheaper" claim (₹6,500 vs ₹8,500) is actually only a 10.8% real discount when you account for the "invisible" costs.

To benchmark effectively:
- **Ask for the RERA registration number:** If they don't have it, it is not a prelaunch: it is a "soft launch" or "expression of interest," which carries significantly higher risk. Verify the project on the [RERA Punjab portal](/blog/how-to-check-rera-punjab-property) first.
- **Compare Apples to Apples:** Don't compare a luxury apartment's prelaunch price with a basic builder floor's resale price. Compare it with a project of similar construction specification and amenities.
- **Check the 'usable' area:** Developers often sell on 'Super Built-up Area'. Always calculate the cost per 'Carpet Area'. If the loading is 35%, your "cheap" prelaunch might actually be more expensive than a ready property with 25% loading. ## Why do developers offer prelaunch prices anyway?

It is important to understand the developer's motivation. They aren't giving you a discount out of the goodness of their heart. In the real estate business, the "Prelaunch" phase is a tool for capital management and risk distribution.

1. **Funding the Approvals:** Often, a developer needs quick cash to pay for government fees, CLU (Change of Land Use) charges, or to clear the final installment of the land cost. These are the "seed" funds that get the project moving.
2. **Establishing Sales Velocity:** To get a construction loan from a bank or an NBFC, a developer needs to show "proven demand." A 30% pre-sold status makes the bank more comfortable lending the remaining 70% at a lower interest rate. You are essentially helping them lower their borrowing costs.
3. **Market Testing:** It helps the developer gauge if their pricing strategy is right. If 100 units sell in a weekend at the prelaunch price, they know they can hike the price by ₹1,000 in the next phase. You are the "guinea pig" for their price discovery.

I've been on the other side of this. I know that the earliest buyers are essentially providing the developer with "interest-free capital." In exchange for this risk, you deserve a substantial reward. If the reward isn't there, you are better off waiting until the project is 50% complete. You can find more about this in our [mohali real estate guide 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026).

## What are the red flags in a prelaunch payment plan?

The price might look good, but the payment plan can be a trap. Having seen how projects can face delays, like the time I handled a project that faced [forest department approval cancellation](/blog/forest-department-approval-real-estate-punjab), I am very wary of "Time-Linked Plans."

- **Time-Linked vs. Construction-Linked:** A time-linked plan means you pay every 6 months regardless of whether the building is moving up or not. This is a massive red flag. Always insist on a Construction-Linked Plan (CLP). If the slabs aren't being poured, your money shouldn't be leaving your bank account.
- **The "Subvention" Trap:** No-EMI-until-possession schemes often come with a higher base price. You are essentially pre-paying the interest to the developer, who then pays it to the bank. If the project is delayed beyond the subvention period, the interest burden often falls back on you at a much higher rate.
- **Upfront Demands:** If a developer asks for more than 10% of the cost before the Agreement to Sale is registered, they are violating RERA. This signals cash-flow desperation. I've seen developers try to collect 25% under the guise of "booking amount": stay away from such deals.

## When is a Mohali prelaunch a genuine opportunity?

I don't want to sound like I'm against prelaunches. I have personally helped clients move into prelaunches that have seen 50% appreciation before the first brick was even laid. But these were cases of "Vision Investing": buying into a corridor before the infrastructure was visible to the masses.

A prelaunch makes sense when:
1. **The Corridor is Expanding:** If the project is in a sector like the [Kharar IT City belt](/blog/kharar-it-city-property-investment-2026) or near the new Airport Road expansion. When you buy in an area that is about to be connected by a major road project (like Bharatmala), the appreciation isn't just from the building progress, but from the land value jump.
2. **The Developer has a "Clean Exit" Track Record:** They have delivered the last 3 projects on time with Occupancy Certificates (OC). This is rare in Mohali, but these developers do exist.
3. **The All-In Price is truly a "Wholesale" Entry:** You are buying at a price that leaves enough room for a secondary market investor to buy from you at a profit before possession.
4. **Scarcity of Inventory:** In certain prime pockets of Sector 82A, if a specific type of unit (like a double-height ceiling penthouse) is launched, the prelaunch might be the only time it's actually available before being hoarded by long-term HNIs.

## The Math of the 'Exit'

Before you sign that booking form, ask yourself: "Who will buy this from me in 3 years, and at what price?" If you buy at ₹7,500 psf (all-in) and the current market for ready units is ₹8,500, you are starting with a ₹1,000 cushion. If the market grows at 8% annually, in 3 years, the ready price will be roughly ₹10,700. Your profit is the gap between ₹7,500 and ₹10,700.

However, if you buy a "ready" unit today at ₹8,500, you get rental income for those 3 years (~₹300 psf total). Your net cost becomes ₹8,200. The gap is now between ₹8,200 and ₹10,700. Suddenly, the "risk-free" ready property looks almost as good as the "risky" prelaunch. This is the level of analysis an HNI needs to perform.
