# What a Stalled Mohali Project Looks Like From the Inside: and the Warning Signs Six Months Before

![Warning signs of a stalled real estate project in Mohali from a developer's perspective](/images/blog/069-mohali-project-stalled-warning-signs-developer.jpg)

A stalled project in Mohali doesn’t stop overnight; it bleeds for six months before the gates finally lock. The primary warning signs include a measurable slowdown in construction pace (specifically when RCC work stops), visible gaps in material deliveries like bricks or steel, high turnover in the sales team, and sudden changes in payment plans where the developer pushes for upfront cash regardless of construction milestones. From my experience on the developer side, these signals indicate a breakdown in the project's escrow hygiene and cash flow. If you notice the developer filing for a RERA extension without prior communication or requesting "private meetings" to renegotiate your payment terms, the project is likely 180 days away from a complete halt. Early intervention, documenting the delay and seeking RERA mediation, is the only way to protect your principal before the developer's bank accounts are frozen.

## How does a developer's cash flow actually break?

To understand the warning signs, you have to understand the "Escrow Trap." Under RERA Punjab, a developer is supposed to keep 70% of buyer funds in a separate escrow account used only for construction. However, when a developer is over-leveraged, perhaps they bought a new land parcel in New Chandigarh or Sector 82A using funds meant for your project, the cycle begins to break.

They start using the 30% "promoter share" to pay interest on old debts instead of marketing the current project. When sales slow down because the market senses a delay, the developer loses the ability to feed the construction machine. This is when the "Internal Stall" happens. On the outside, the office looks grand, the brochures are glossy, but behind the scenes, the contractors haven't been paid for two months. Having been on the developer side, I can tell you that the first person to know a project is failing isn't the buyer: it is the brick supplier.

## What are the 7 warning signs of a project stalling in Mohali?

If you have invested in a project and are worried about its progress, look for these seven specific red flags. If you see more than three of these simultaneously, you are looking at a project in active distress.

### 1. The construction pace slows measurably (The "One Floor" Rule)
In an active, healthy project, you should see progress every 15 to 20 days. If the shuttering for the next floor stays up for 45 days without a concrete pour, something is wrong. Developers often try to hide this by keeping a skeleton crew of 10 workers on a site that requires 200, just to keep the "sound of hammers" alive for visiting buyers. Don't be fooled by noise; look for actual structural progress.

### 2. Visible material delivery gaps
A healthy site is messy with incoming supplies. If you visit a site and see no new stacks of bricks, no fresh TMT steel bars, or empty cement godowns, it means the suppliers have cut off the developer’s credit line. In Mohali's tight-knit supplier network, news of a developer defaulting on payments spreads in 48 hours. Once the credit stops, the construction stops.

### 3. High sales team turnover or sudden commission hikes
When a project is in trouble, the smart sales managers are the first to resign. They see the unpaid bills and the mounting buyer complaints. Conversely, if you suddenly see the developer offering "10% spot commission" to local brokers (when the standard is 2-3%), it’s a sign of desperation. They are trying to raise quick cash at any cost to plug a hole in their balance sheet.

### 4. Payment plans changed without notice
Have you been asked to "pay the next installment early for a 5% discount"? This is a classic desperation move. If the developer is moving away from a construction-linked plan to a time-linked plan, or pushing for payments before the milestone is achieved, they are likely out of cash. You can read more about [Mohali builder payment plan red flags](/blog/mohali-builder-payment-plan-red-flags) to see how these traps are structured.

### 5. RERA extension filed without communication
Check the RERA Punjab portal (rera.punjab.gov.in) every quarter. If the "Project End Date" has been extended but the developer hasn't sent you an official letter explaining why, they are buying time from the regulator while keeping buyers in the dark. A transparent developer communicates delays; a failing one hides them.

### 6. Developer requests meetings to renegotiate terms
If a developer invites you to a "one-on-one" to discuss "adjusting" your allotment or moving towers because "construction priorities have shifted," be careful. This is often "Inventory Shuffling": an attempt to consolidate buyers into one tower because they can no longer afford to build the others. If you are asked to sign a "Supplementary Agreement" or a layout change NOC, you may be signing away your right to complain about the original delay.

### 7. The "Private Settlement" push
If you ask for a refund and the developer offers a post-dated cheque (PDC) while asking you not to go to RERA, do not accept it without legal security. I have seen countless cases where these cheques bounce because the account is already under attachment. Without a formal settlement agreement registered with RERA, that piece of paper is often worthless.

## What should you do when you notice these signs?

Spotting the signs early gives you a "First Mover Advantage." In a stalled project, the buyers who act in the first six months often recover more than those who wait years.

1.  **Document and Formalize:** Stop having verbal conversations. Send a registered letter asking for the reason for the slowdown. This becomes critical evidence if you need to navigate [real estate disputes and legal rights in Punjab](/blog/real-estate-disputes-legal-rights-punjab-2026). Developers often pay the "loudest" buyers first to avoid public litigation.
2.  **The "Materials Audit":** Visit the site on a Tuesday morning, not a weekend. If a site that had 100 workers now has 15, it is in distress. Check for fresh supplies; if the developer has switched to local, non-ISI materials, they are cutting corners to save pennies.
3.  **Check Escrow and Compliance:** Verify if the developer has paid EDC and IDC to GMADA. If these are unpaid, you will never get an Occupation Certificate (OC), even if the building is finished. I've seen buildings ready for years where residents couldn't move in because the developer owed crores to the government.
4.  **Form a Strategic Buyer Group:** Strength lies in numbers, but only if organized. Hire a single legal counsel to represent a core committee. A united front can force a developer to the table or get faster attention from the RERA Punjab authority.

## Can a stalled project be revived?

Yes, but it usually requires a "takeover developer" or strict RERA intervention. This often comes with a "price escalation" request to existing buyers. If the project is at a complete standstill, your focus should shift from possession to recovery. Understanding [what happens to your booking amount if a project stalls](/blog/mohali-project-stalled-cancelled-booking-refund) becomes your primary objective. RERA Punjab is proactive, but enforcement is the bottleneck. You need a strategy for execution, not just an order.

## The "Internal Stall" Perspective
When I worked as a developer, I saw how a stall begins: usually with a "diverted fund" decision. The developer pulls money from Project A to finish Project B, but when Project B fails to sell, both projects stop. By the time you see construction halt, the financial rot has been there for months. This is why [due diligence on the developer](/blog/mohali-developer-project-due-diligence-checklist) matters more than the location of the flat.

## Why is "Wait and See" a failure strategy in a stalling project?
Most buyers wait too long because they want to believe the promises. In real estate, hope is not a financial strategy. If these signs are present, the developer is likely insolvent. Acting while the developer still has some liquidity, even if it means a small "exit haircut," is smarter than a multi-year legal battle with 0% recovery.
