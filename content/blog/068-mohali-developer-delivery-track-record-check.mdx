# How to Check a Mohali Developer's Delivery Track Record: The Data That Actually Matters

To check a Mohali developer’s delivery track record accurately, you must verify three specific data points: the RERA Punjab completion certificate (CC) dates against the original promised timelines, the volume and nature of litigation in the "Complaints" section of the RERA portal, and the physical state of past projects. Do not rely on marketing brochures that list "20 years of excellence." Instead, visit rera.punjab.gov.in, search for the developer's previous SPVs (Special Purpose Vehicles), and calculate the "Possession Gap"—the delta between the date on the Allotment Letter and the date the Occupancy Certificate (OC) was actually granted. A gap of more than 12 months across multiple projects is a systemic red flag. Finally, speak with existing residents at delivered sites to confirm if the "possession" offered was a legal handover with utilities or merely a "fit-out" possession used to stop penalty clocks.

## What does RERA Punjab actually tell you about a developer's past?

When I was a Director and Sales Head for a RERA-approved developer, I saw the documentation process from the inside. Many buyers think that if a project is registered with RERA, it is "safe." That is a misunderstanding. RERA is a transparency tool, not a guarantee of success. To see a developer's real history, you need to look past the current project.

Go to the RERA Punjab website and search for the promoter's name. You will often find a list of several companies. In real estate, developers create a new company—an SPV—for every project. If you only search for the current project, you are seeing a blank slate. You must find the names of their previous SPVs. 

Once you find a past project, look for the "Completion Certificate" or "Occupancy Certificate" in the uploaded documents. Compare the date on that certificate with the "Original Proposed Date of Completion" listed in the initial registration. This is where the truth comes out. If a developer promised delivery in December 2022 but the CC was issued in June 2024, you are looking at an 18-month delay. In my experience, developers who delay by 18 months once tend to repeat that pattern because their cash flow cycles are built on those extended timelines.

### Navigating the RERA Portal Like an Expert
When you are on the portal, don't just look at the registration certificate. Look for the "Quarterly Progress Reports" (QPR). These reports tell you how much money the developer has collected and how much they have actually spent on construction. If you see that 90% of the money has been collected but only 50% of the construction is done, you are looking at a "funds diversion" scenario. This is exactly why the [how to verify any Mohali property on RERA Punjab](/blog/how-to-check-rera-punjab-property) guide is the most important document for a first-time buyer.

## Why is the 'Promised vs Actual' possession gap the most important metric?

The reason I stress the possession gap is that it directly impacts your return on investment (ROI). As someone with a background in capital markets (AMFI and NCFM certified), I evaluate real estate through the lens of internal rate of return (IRR). 

If you buy a flat for ₹1.5 crore expecting possession in 3 years, but it takes 5 years, your "holding cost" has just destroyed your profit. You have lost two years of rental income—roughly ₹8 to ₹10 lakh in Mohali's current market—and you have likely continued paying interest on a home loan for an unproductive asset. 

| Milestone | Promised Date | Actual Date | Gap (Months) | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Structure Completion | June 2023 | Jan 2024 | 7 | Delayed internal finishing |
| Occupancy Certificate | Dec 2023 | Aug 2024 | 8 | Lost rental income |
| Possession Handover | March 2024 | Nov 2024 | 8 | Interest burden increases |

A developer with a clean track record of delivering within 6 months of the promised date is worth a 10-15% premium. A developer who consistently delivers 2 years late is actually selling you a more expensive product than the price on the paper suggests. When you are reading our [Mohali real estate guide 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026), you will see that sectors like 82A and Aerocity have moved fast precisely because the delivery of infrastructure and projects stayed relatively close to the promised windows.

## How to distinguish between serious and cosmetic RERA complaints?

Not all complaints on the RERA portal are equal. When you are doing your [developer and project due diligence](/blog/mohali-developer-project-due-diligence-checklist), you must click through to the "Complaints" or "Orders" section.

**Cosmetic Complaints:** These are often about minor snags, delay in club membership activation, or maintenance disputes. While annoying, they don't necessarily mean the project is a risk.
**Serious Complaints:** Look for orders related to "Refund with Interest" or "Non-execution of Sale Deed." If multiple buyers are asking for their money back because the project stalled, that is a terminal red flag. 

If you see a pattern where the RERA Authority has ordered a refund but the developer has not paid, it suggests a liquidity crisis. Having navigated project approvals across PUDA, PSPCL, and the Municipal Committee, I know that once the money stops flowing to the contractors, the regulatory clearances also stop. A developer who can't pay a RERA penalty is unlikely to have the funds to finish your flooring and lift installations.

## What should you look for during a physical site visit to a delivered project?

Brochures show you 3D renders; site visits show you the developer's character. I always tell my clients that the best way to predict your future is to look at someone else's present. 

Visit a project the developer delivered 3 to 5 years ago. Do not look at the paint; look at the "bones."
1. **The Basement:** Is there seepage? Seepage in the basement of a 3-year-old building in Mohali suggests poor RCC (Reinforced Cement Concrete) quality or a lack of proper waterproofing during the foundation stage.
2. **The Lifts:** Are they a Tier-1 brand or a local assembly? Lift maintenance is one of the highest recurring costs for a society. 
3. **The Common Areas:** Has the developer handed over the "Sinking Fund" and maintenance corpus to the RWA (Residents Welfare Association)? 

In many cases, I've seen developers hold onto maintenance funds long after possession, leading to disputes that leave the building looking 10 years old when it is only 2. If the previous project looks dilapidated, your future project will likely follow suit once the sales team has moved on to the next site.

## The 'Silent' track record: Why talking to current residents is non-negotiable

The most accurate data in Mohali real estate isn't online; it’s in the lobbies of delivered buildings. If you want to know the truth about a developer, talk to the people who already gave them money. 

Ask them three specific questions:
1. "Did the builder ask for extra 'hidden charges' at the time of possession?" (Refer to our list of [hidden costs in Mohali property](/blog/hidden-costs-mohali-property-purchase) for what to watch for).
2. "Was the electricity connection provided by PSPCL immediately, or were you running on DG sets for the first year?"
3. "How long did it take to get the Registry/Sale Deed done after you moved in?"

I have seen cases where possession was given but the [PUDA approved status](/blog/puda-approved-project-mohali-verify) was still in limbo, meaning residents couldn't get their properties registered for years. This "silent" delay is often more painful than the construction delay because you are living in the house but you don't legally own it yet. 

### The Resident Interview Checklist
*   **The Maintenance Quality:** Ask if the club and gym are functional as promised.
*   **The Documentation:** Did they receive the OC and CC before taking possession?
*   **The Hidden Charges:** Did the ₹10 lakh "parking and club" fee get charged twice?
*   **The Post-Sale Service:** Does the builder respond to leakage complaints after 1 year?

## How to interpret 'Group' history vs 'Specific Partner' history?

This is a nuance most brokers won't tell you. A developer might market themselves under a well-known brand name, but the partners for the specific project you are buying might be different. 

In Mohali, we see many "Joint Ventures" where a local land owner partners with a builder. The builder might have a good track record, but if the land owner (who is also a partner in the SPV) gets into a dispute, the project stalls. This is something I've navigated personally during my time as a developer. I’ve seen how partnership breakdowns can freeze a project for years, regardless of how "branded" the name on the gate is. 

Always ask for the list of Directors of the promoting company and check their individual track records. If one of the partners has a history of litigious behavior or failed businesses in other sectors, your risk profile changes. 

## The Reality of Escrow Accounts in Mohali
RERA mandates that 70% of the money collected from buyers must be kept in a separate escrow account and used only for construction and land costs. A developer with a strong track record will be transparent about this. If you ask for the escrow account number and the bank details, and the sales team gets defensive, you have your answer. 

When I evaluate projects for HNI and CXO clients, I look for "Escrow discipline." If a developer is using your money to buy more land instead of finishing your roof, they are not a developer; they are a land-banker playing with your capital.

## What are the red flags in a developer's RERA filings?

Beyond the dates, look at the "Quarterly Progress Reports" (QPR). RERA requires developers to update their progress every three months.
* **Red Flag 1:** Multiple missing quarters. If the developer hasn't updated their progress for 6 months, they are hiding something or their compliance team has checked out.
* **Red Flag 2:** Percentage of work completed stays the same for two quarters. If "Internal Infrastructure" is at 45% in Q1 and still at 45% in Q2, the site is likely "soft-stalled."
* **Red Flag 3:** Financial encumbrances. Check if the land is mortgaged to a bank. This is normal, but you need to ensure the developer has an "NOC for Sale" from the bank.

## The 'Experience vs Marketing' Trap
Don't be fooled by "award-winning" developers. Many awards in the real estate sector are purchased or based on marketing spend, not delivery speed. I prefer a developer who has delivered two projects on time over one who has won ten awards but has three ongoing litigations on Airport Road.

## The verdict on 'New' developers in the Mohali market

What if the developer has no track record? We see many new players entering Mohali, especially in the luxury segment. 
In this case, you are not buying a track record; you are buying the quality of the "Approvals" and the "Escrow." 
Ensure that the project is [properly verified on RERA Punjab](/blog/how-to-check-rera-punjab-property) and that they are using a construction-linked payment plan. Without a track record to lean on, you must never agree to a "Time-Linked" or "Down-Payment" plan. You only pay when the slab is cast. 

### Why Construction-Linked Plans are the Only Safety for New Developers
In a CLP, your payment is triggered by a site engineer's certificate. If the builder doesn't pour the concrete, you don't write the cheque. This shifts the risk of delay back to the developer. If they want your money, they have to build your house. For a new developer, this is the only way to establish trust in a market where track record is the primary currency.
