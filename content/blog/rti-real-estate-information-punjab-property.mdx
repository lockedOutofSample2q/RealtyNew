The Right to Information (RTI) Act 2005 is your most powerful tool for uncovering the absolute truth about a real estate project or land parcel in Punjab. To use it, you file an application with the Public Information Officer (PIO) of the specific government department holding the records—such as GMADA for allotment details, PUDA for licensing status, the Municipal Committee for building plan sanctions, or the Revenue Department for jamabandi (land records). The process requires a basic application fee of ₹10, plus ₹2 per page for copies of the documents requested. By law, the department must provide the information within 30 days. In my years of navigating project approvals and resolving property disputes, I have used RTI consistently because government records do not lie, and developers cannot hide what is written in an official government file. If you are facing a dispute or verifying a project, this is where you start.

## Why is RTI necessary for property buyers in Punjab?

When a developer tells you their project is approved, that is a marketing statement. When a government department gives you the approval status in writing, that is a fact. In my years of managing project documentation for approvals across PUDA, PSPCL, Municipal Committees, and the Forest Department, I have seen the massive gap between what is promised to buyers and what is actually filed in the government records. The Right to Information Act bridges that gap.

Many buyers rely on verbal assurances or generic "applied for" status letters when making decisions involving crores of rupees. An "applied for" PUDA approval is not a PUDA approval. A Change of Land Use (CLU) application does not guarantee that the layout plan will be sanctioned. By the time a buyer realises the developer lacked the necessary clearances, their funds are already locked in an escrow account—or worse, a non-escrow operational account. Using an RTI allows you to bypass the sales pitch and pull the exact regulatory standing of a property directly from the source. It removes ambiguity and replaces it with documented evidence.

## Which government authority should you approach for your RTI application?

The most common mistake people make is sending an RTI to the wrong department, which only results in the application being forwarded or rejected, wasting valuable time. You must target the specific authority that controls the jurisdiction and the function you are inquiring about. 

If you are dealing with plot allotments, draw results, or colony licensing within the Greater Mohali area, your RTI goes to GMADA. If the project is outside GMADA limits, PUDA handles the licensing and layout plan approvals. If you need clarity on how these specific jurisdictions operate and overlap, our [GMADA explained guide](/blog/gmada-mohali-explained-guide) breaks down the exact boundaries and responsibilities.

For building plan sanctions, completion certificates (OC/CC), and property tax records within city limits, the Municipal Committee is the correct authority. If your concern is regarding agricultural land, ownership history, or mutations (intkal), you must file your RTI with the Revenue Department. Finally, if you are looking at land near green belts or the Shivalik foothills, the Forest Department holds the records on whether a project has received clearance or violates buffer zone regulations. Directing your query to the correct Public Information Officer is the first step to getting a timely and accurate response.

## What specific information can you obtain through an RTI?

The scope of what you can extract from government files is extensive, provided you ask the right questions. You can ask for the exact status of a builder's license, the sanctioned building plans, and whether an Occupancy Certificate (OC) has actually been issued for a specific tower or phase. 

In cases of land purchases, you can request the jamabandi (fard) and mutation records from the Revenue Department. This is particularly crucial if you suspect an undisclosed encumbrance, a court attachment, or an unclear succession line among legal heirs. Title disputes often originate from details deliberately omitted by the seller. We cover the devastating cost of these omissions in our guide on [how to avoid title disputes in Punjab property](/blog/title-dispute-punjab-property-how-to-avoid). 

You can also use RTI to track the exact trail of property tax payments. I have personally navigated situations where unpaid tax liabilities were hidden during a floor-wise sale, requiring months of coordination to untangle. An RTI to the Municipal Committee asking for the property tax ledger of a specific ID number will immediately reveal if there are outstanding dues that could become your liability post-purchase.

## How do you file an RTI application for real estate records?

The application process is straightforward but requires precision. You can file it physically by drafting a letter to the Public Information Officer of the relevant department, or you can file it online through the dedicated RTI portals for Punjab state departments. 

The application fee is a nominal ₹10. However, if you are requesting physical copies of layout plans, ledger entries, or approval certificates, you will be required to pay an additional ₹2 per page. 

When drafting your application, do not ask open-ended or vague questions. Do not ask, "Is this project good?" or "Are there any problems with this land?" Instead, ask specific, factual questions: "Please provide a certified copy of the Completion Certificate issued for Tower B of Project X." "Please confirm if the Change of Land Use (CLU) has been approved for Khasra Number Y, and provide a copy of the sanction letter." The more specific your request, the harder it is for the department to provide a vague or evasive response. By law, the PIO has 30 days to furnish the information. 

## What are the common challenges when using RTI for property disputes?

While the law mandates a 30-day response, the reality of dealing with government departments is rarely that smooth. The most common challenge is the PIO providing an incomplete answer, citing exemptions under the RTI Act, or claiming that the file is not traceable. 

When a department gives an unsatisfactory reply, you have the right to file a First Appeal with the First Appellate Authority within the same department. If that also fails, you can escalate it to the State Information Commission. In my experience of using RTIs during liaisoning work, persistence is mandatory. You cannot send one letter and assume the job is done. 

Another challenge is the deliberate misinterpretation of your query. If your question leaves any room for interpretation, the department will likely choose the interpretation that requires them to disclose the least amount of information. ## How do RTI information help in legal disputes and RERA complaints?

If you are filing a RERA complaint for possession delay or a deficiency in service, your case is only as strong as your evidence. A developer will almost always blame delays on "force majeure" or delays in government approvals. 

An RTI response cuts through these excuses. If the developer claims that the Municipal Committee delayed their building plan sanction for two years, an RTI to the Municipal Committee asking for the date the application was submitted and the date it was approved will reveal the truth. Often, you will find that the developer applied for the sanction a year late, or their application was rejected multiple times for failing to meet basic zoning norms. 

When you present a certified RTI response to a RERA adjudicating officer, it carries the weight of official government documentation. It is not your word against the builder's word; it is the builder's word against the government's own records. This level of factual certainty is exactly what you need to force a settlement or secure a favourable order.

## Can an RTI protect you from buying an illegal or unapproved plot?

Absolutely. The best time to use an RTI is before you hand over a booking amount, not after you are stuck in a stalled project. 

Unauthorised colonies and unlicensed plots are a massive issue in the peripheries of Mohali and the wider Punjab region. Sellers will often show you a receipt indicating they have paid the regularisation fee to the government, implying the plot is now legal. However, applying for regularisation and actually receiving the No Objection Certificate (NOC) are two entirely different things. 

Before committing funds to a plot that seems suspiciously cheap, file an RTI with PUDA or the local Municipal Committee asking for the regularisation status of that specific Khasra number or colony name. The ₹10 you spend on that application could save you from locking ₹50 lakh into a property that you can never register, build upon, or resell.
