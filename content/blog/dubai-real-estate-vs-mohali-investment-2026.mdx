## WHY INDIAN INVESTORS ARE LOOKING AT DUBAI: AND WHAT IS ACTUALLY DRIVING THE SHIFT

The capital migration toward Dubai is not irrational. It is driven by a convergence of factors that, when presented selectively by a motivated seller, make an extremely compelling case.

Zero income tax. Rental yields ranging from 7% to 12% annually on the total investment value. A government-backed development ecosystem with infrastructure that makes Mohali's current road network look provisional. A Golden Visa pathway tied to a defined investment threshold of one million dirham. And a global tourism and residency magnet that continues to attract high-net-worth individuals from over 190 countries.

Add to that the 1% monthly payment plan structure that Dubai developers have perfected. This is a model that allows buyers to enter premium off-plan inventory by paying just 1% of the property value per month to the developer during construction, without traditional mortgage financing or interest, and you have an asset class that genuinely looks different from anything available domestically.

This is why firms like Emaar, Danube, and DAMAC are not just selling to Indians from afar. They are opening offices, hosting events in Chandigarh, and aggressively pitching inventory at the ₹8 crore entry point to a demographic that is simultaneously priced out of Chandigarh proper and increasingly frustrated with what the Tricity market offers.

Understanding why this pitch works is the starting point of any serious analysis. Dismissing it would be intellectually dishonest.

## THE ROI CASE FOR DUBAI: WHERE THE NUMBERS ACTUALLY STAND

On a pure yield basis, Dubai outperforms Mohali. This is not a contested point.

Gross rental yields on mid-to-premium residential property in Dubai's established micro-markets (Business Bay, Dubai Marina, Jumeirah Village Circle, and the newer off-plan corridors) consistently range between 6.5% and 11% depending on the asset type, location, and rental model (short-stay versus long-let). Tax-free gross yield is, in practical terms, net yield. There is no rental income tax, no capital gains tax on disposal, and no wealth tax. The regulatory environment for foreign property ownership in designated freehold zones is clearly codified and well-enforced.

Compare this to Mohali, where gross rental yields on independent floors, builder floors, and apartments typically range between 2.5% and 4.5% in established sectors, and where tax on rental income above the basic exemption threshold is applicable under Indian income tax law. Our [Mohali price trend tool](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/tools/price-trend) gives investors a current sector-by-sector read on where prices and rental values actually stand across the Tricity corridor.

On yield alone, the case is clear. If an investor deploys ₹5 crore and generates 8% annual rental income tax-free in Dubai versus 3.5% taxable income in Mohali, the arithmetic does not require a financial model to understand.

Capital appreciation is the more contested variable.

Dubai has seen strong appreciation cycles, particularly in the post-pandemic period, with prices in prime and mid-tier zones rising sharply between 2021 and 2024. However, Dubai's appreciation story is fundamentally different from a land-scarcity-driven appreciation market like Chandigarh or South Mumbai. Dubai's appreciation is demand-driven, tourism-linked, and heavily correlated to global liquidity cycles and regional geopolitical stability. The government's ability to continuously create new supply, including through land reclamation, means that the fundamental scarcity argument that drives appreciation in mature Indian markets does not apply with the same force.

In Mohali, capital appreciation in GMADA-authorised sectors, particularly in the Airport Road corridor, [Aerocity](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/flats/aerocity), and [IT City](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/flats/it-city), has been steady and structurally supported. Demand is aspirational, consistent, and geographically constrained by the Chandigarh effect, a demand dynamic explained in detail later in this piece.

The yield comparison favours Dubai. The appreciation certainty comparison is more nuanced. Neither is the complete picture.

## UNDERSTANDING THE 1% PAYMENT PLAN: STRUCTURE, RISK, AND REALITY

![Payment Plan Analysis](/assets/images/blog/payment-plan-audit.jpg)

The 1% monthly payment plan has become Dubai's most effective sales instrument for the Indian investor market. It deserves a careful, unsentimental reading.

The structure is straightforward. On a property valued at, say, ₹1 crore (or its dirham equivalent), the buyer pays 1% of the total property value per month (approximately ₹1 lakh monthly) directly to the developer. This continues through the construction and handover phase, and in some cases includes a post-handover instalment tail. There is typically no traditional bank financing involved in the construction phase, no EMI interest, and no requirement for a large upfront payment.

This sounds like an accessible, low-friction entry model. And for certain investor profiles, it genuinely is.

However, there are structural realities that this plan obscures.

First, the baseline premium. Off-plan inventory offered on the 1% scheme is typically priced at a meaningful premium to comparable ready inventory, which the market calls a baseline markup. The developer is, in effect, financing the buyer's installment convenience through the asset price itself. Buyers who do not benchmark the off-plan price against comparable ready units often discover they have paid a 15% to 20% premium for the payment flexibility. That is ₹15-20 lakh of lost equity on day one, just for the convenience of installments.

Second, forfeiture risk. In a standard Dubai off-plan purchase contract governed by the Real Estate Regulatory Authority (RERA Dubai), the developer is entitled to retain a defined percentage of the paid amount, often between 30% and 40%, if the buyer defaults or chooses to exit before handover and before reaching the resale eligibility threshold. For an Indian investor who hits a liquidity event back home and needs to exit an overseas position quickly, this clause can be severe.

Third, the minimum resale threshold. Most Dubai developers require a buyer to have paid a minimum of 30% to 40% of the total property value before granting a No Objection Certificate (NOC) for resale of the off-plan unit. This means an investor on the 1% monthly plan is locked into the asset for approximately 30 to 40 months before they have a viable exit mechanism. This is not a detail. It is a structural liquidity constraint that materially changes the investment thesis for anyone who might need capital flexibility within a three-year window.

Understanding these elements does not make the 1% plan a bad product. It makes it a specific product suited to a specific investor profile: one with stable income, a long time horizon, no near-term liquidity needs, and the operational bandwidth to manage an asset 3,000 kilometres away. ## THE GOLDEN VISA PROPOSITION: RESIDENCY IS NOT OWNERSHIP

The Dubai Golden Visa is a genuine and significant proposition. An investment of one million dirham (approximately ₹2.3 crore at current rates) in qualifying freehold property grants the investor a long-term UAE residency visa, typically for five to ten years, with a renewable structure. This provides the right to live, work, and remain in the UAE without employer sponsorship.

For a certain category of Indian professional (the entrepreneur relocating their business, the CXO exiting a liquidity event, the family seeking to optimise their children's education access) the Golden Visa is a genuinely transformative instrument.

But it is important to be precise about what it is and what it is not.

The Golden Visa does not confer citizenship. The UAE's naturalisation pathway for foreign nationals remains one of the most restricted in the world. After two, five, or ten years of residence, the foreign investor does not become a citizen and does not gain voting rights, full ownership rights to land outside designated freehold zones, or the right to establish a majority-owned business in most sectors without a local Emirati partner.

The business ownership restriction is the detail most consistently omitted from Golden Visa pitch presentations. In the mainland UAE economy (outside the free zones) a foreign national establishing a trading, retail, services, or real estate business is required to have an Emirati national as a local sponsor holding a defined ownership stake. This partner earns a return from the business without operational involvement. The free zone structure circumvents some of these restrictions, but free zone companies have limitations on trading directly with the mainland UAE market.

For a purely online or internationally-oriented business, the free zone model works well. For anyone seeking to run a domestic, market-facing operation in Dubai, the structural constraints are real.

This does not eliminate the Golden Visa's value. It contextualises it. The visa is a strong tool for investors whose primary objective is a stable base, school access, healthcare, and tax-efficient income routing. It is not, on its own, a business freedom instrument.

## THE LIQUIDITY PROBLEM: WHAT DUBAI AGENTS DON'T PUT IN THE BROCHURE

Of all the variables in the Dubai versus Mohali comparison, liquidity is the one that separates theory from reality with the greatest force.

Liquidity in real estate refers to the ease and speed with which an investor can convert their property into cash at or near its market value. It is the variable that matters most when something goes wrong: when circumstances change, when an alternative opportunity arises, or when an investor simply needs their capital back.

In Mohali or anywhere within the Tricity real estate corridor, an investor with a problem has options. They can physically reach the asset. They can engage a broker network they know and trust. They can be present for negotiations, walk a potential buyer through the property, apply legal pressure if required, and close a transaction within a timeframe that is a matter of weeks rather than months. The investor has proximity, network, and jurisdiction on their side.

In Dubai, all of these advantages evaporate.

An Indian investor holding a Dubai property must rely entirely on an overseas brokerage network, typically the same network that sold them the asset in the first place. They cannot inspect the asset in response to a potential buyer's question. They cannot attend a negotiation. They cannot enforce a sale through domestic legal channels. If the developer is in dispute, the investor is subject to UAE law and UAE courts, with no meaningful access to a local support ecosystem.

The secondary market for off-plan resales in Dubai is functional, but it is not liquid. Inventory levels in the off-plan secondary market have increased substantially as the number of foreign investors holding pre-handover assets has grown. The theoretical exit mechanism exists. The practical execution, especially at speed, is a different matter.

Consider the structural reality. The firms selling Indian investors Dubai property predominantly operate one-way pipelines. They come to Chandigarh, Ludhiana, Delhi, and Ahmedabad to sell. They do not come to buy. When the time comes to exit, the investor is navigating a foreign market remotely, with asymmetric information, no local enforcement leverage, and a dealer who has already earned their commission.

This is the liquidity problem. It is not hypothetical. It is the single most consequential risk in the Dubai investment thesis for retail and mid-market Indian investors.

## CAPITAL REPATRIATION: THE FEMA REALITY OF BRINGING DUBAI RETURNS HOME

![FEMA Capital Borders](/assets/images/blog/fema-repatriation.svg)

A question that is almost never raised at Dubai property roadshows in India deserves to be placed front and centre. Once your rental income or sale proceeds are sitting in a Dubai bank account, what does it cost to bring that money back to India, and what are the regulatory implications when it arrives?

Under the Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA), Indian residents are permitted to invest overseas under the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS), which allows up to USD 250,000 per financial year per individual for overseas investments, including real estate. Income earned on those investments, such as rental income, dividends, or capital gains on disposal, can be repatriated, but the remittance must be properly documented and reported.

The complication arises from tax residency.

Several Indian investors are attracted to the proposition of obtaining a Dubai Golden Visa, establishing UAE tax residency, and thereby removing their income from India's tax jurisdiction. This is legally possible. But it is not as clean as it sounds on a sales presentation.

To legitimately claim UAE tax residency and extinguish Indian tax residency, an individual must satisfy the conditions under India's residential status rules. This requires:
* Spending fewer than 182 days in India in a financial year.
* Or meeting the stricter conditions under the amended Finance Act 2020 for high-income individuals, which includes a 120-day threshold.

This means genuinely restructuring one's life around Dubai, not holding a visa while operating entirely out of Punjab.

For investors who achieve genuine UAE tax residency, the structure works. Rental income earned in Dubai on a Dubai-held property is not subject to UAE income tax (there is none) and is not subject to Indian income tax if the investor is a non-resident for that year.

But for the vast majority of Indian investors, those who hold Dubai property as one asset class among several, continue to live and work in India, and plan to repatriate rental income annually, the income becomes taxable in India upon repatriation, subject to the relevant Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA) between India and the UAE, and applicable Indian income tax rates. The tax-free narrative, in their specific case, is not accurate.

Understanding this distinction is not a reason to avoid Dubai investment. It is a reason to structure it correctly, ideally with proper legal and tax counsel before committing capital. ## THE GEOPOLITICAL VARIABLE: ASSESSING RISK IN AN UNSTABLE REGION

Dubai's extraordinary development story has unfolded against a backdrop of regional geopolitical complexity that is not going away. The UAE itself is a stable, well-governed state with robust institutional frameworks and a clear pro-investment orientation. But the broader Gulf region has demonstrated, repeatedly, that stability is not guaranteed and that volatility can materialise rapidly.

For an investor holding a physical asset in a foreign jurisdiction governed by different laws, different courts, and different political dynamics, geopolitical risk carries a specific implication: the inability to protect equity through legal recourse when the institutional environment deteriorates.

Within India, an investor facing a developer dispute, a title conflict, or a project delay has access to RERA, consumer courts, civil courts, and eventually the Supreme Court. The system is imperfect, but the investor is a citizen with full legal standing and local representation.

In Dubai, a foreign national investor facing a developer dispute, project cancellation, or asset devaluation resulting from external factors has limited leverage. UAE law provides some protection, such as the escrow account requirement for off-plan projects which is a genuine structural safeguard, but the enforcement mechanism, the timeline, and the cost of cross-border litigation make recovery materially harder.

This is a tail risk, not a baseline assumption. We are not suggesting imminent instability. We are observing that in any investment decision of meaningful scale, the tail risk must be priced. For investors whose entire deployable capital is going into a single Dubai off-plan unit, the inability to recover equity in a worst-case scenario is not an academic concern.

## WHY MOHALI REMAINS A FUNDAMENTALLY SOUND INVESTMENT MARKET IN 2026

Having examined the full picture of what Dubai offers, and what it withholds, the case for Mohali can be made on its own terms, without needing Dubai to fail.

Mohali's investment thesis is built on a structural demand dynamic that is deeply embedded in the social and aspirational fabric of Punjab. It is not driven by tax efficiency, yield optimisation, or global capital flows. It is driven by something more durable: a regional identity aspiration with no viable substitute.

### The Chandigarh Demand Corridor

Chandigarh is one of India's most aspirationally coveted addresses for anyone with roots in Punjab and Haryana. It is a city that represents a particular version of arrival: planned, clean, institutionally dense, socially desirable. The demand to live in or near Chandigarh is not a function of yield calculations. It is a function of who people want to be, and where they want their family to be planted.

The structural problem is supply. Chandigarh proper has no meaningful residential land left for new development at scale. What is available commands prices that place it beyond the reach of most buyers with ₹2 crore to ₹5 crore to deploy.

Mohali captures the spillover from this inelastic aspiration. It sits within the Chandigarh metro area and offers genuinely high-quality residential environments across a range of sectors and micro-markets. GMADA-authorised development, the Aerocity project, IT City expansion, and the proximity to Chandigarh International Airport all create a fundamentally sound demand structure.

In [Sector 82A](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/flats/sector-82a) and the adjoining airport road belt, premium inventory has absorbed sustained demand. We have seen commercial units on Airport Road that were available at ₹3-4 crore jump to ₹12-16 crore simply because buyers moved with vision before the infrastructure arrived. The [Aerocity corridor](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/flats/aerocity) continues to attract institutional attention. In [IT City](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/flats/it-city), the corporate rental market (driven by Infosys campus proximity and expanding tech employment) creates a rental yield case that is structurally different from speculative demand. [Sector 88](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/flats/sector-88), home to projects like [Purab Premium Apartments](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/purab-premium-apartments-sector-88-mohali), demonstrates the kind of state-supervised development credibility that gives investors recourse and confidence simultaneously.

[Sector 66A](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/flats/sector-66a) and projects such as [JLPL Falcon View](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/jlpl-falcon-view-sector-66a-mohali) represent the premium residential segment that continues to attract buyers who want Chandigarh adjacency with Mohali pricing. And in the broader inventory picture, options across [Sector 70](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/flats/sector-70) and [Sector 78](https://www.realtyconsultants.in/properties/flats/sector-78) provide entry-level and mid-range access points for investors who are not yet ready to commit at the ₹3 crore-plus level.

When Mohali becomes too expensive for a segment of buyers, as it increasingly is, they move to Zirakpur, Kharar, or Dera Bassi. They do not move to Dubai. The demand cascade flows inward, not outward, because the aspiration is rooted in regional identity, not yield.

### The Proximity Advantage

There is a form of investment intelligence that sophisticated investors develop over time, and it is almost never discussed in yield comparison analyses: the value of being close to your asset.

At Realty Holding & Management Consultants, we have seen what proximity enables. It enables a site visit within two hours when a construction issue is flagged. It enables a same-day meeting with a builder when a clause is in dispute. It enables a rapid physical inspection when a potential buyer is evaluating the property. It enables legal intervention through a local network when something goes wrong.

These are not romantic arguments for localism. They are practical arguments about asset management efficiency. Real estate (unlike equity or debt instruments) is a physical asset that benefits from an owner's active presence and the ability to respond to events in real time.

For investors who are still in an active operational phase, running businesses, managing legal obligations, maintaining family commitments that require physical presence in the region, the overhead of managing a foreign asset is not trivial. Every problem that requires resolution becomes a flight and a hotel stay. Every potential buyer requires remote coordination. Every documentation requirement crosses international borders.

### The Liquidity Advantage

Where Dubai's liquidity profile is a limitation, Mohali's is an advantage. A well-priced residential asset in a prime Mohali sector, correctly positioned, with clean title documentation and GMADA approvals in order, finds buyers. The local broker network is active and dense. A seller with market knowledge can move an asset within a reasonable timeframe without relying on an overseas intermediary who may not be motivated to prioritise a secondary resale over fresh inventory commissions.

This liquidity advantage is most valuable precisely when you need it most: in moments of personal financial pressure, alternative investment opportunity, or when market conditions shift and you need to act quickly. These are assets that carry strong long-term appreciation logic within a jurisdiction where title clarity and resale are operationally manageable.

### The GMADA and RERA Structural Framework

Mohali's regulatory environment, while not without its early challenges, has matured significantly. GMADA provides a defined framework for plot development, sector authorisation, and infrastructure delivery. RERA Punjab provides a complaints mechanism for buyers dealing with delayed possession or unmet developer commitments.

These are not equivalent to the track record of Dubai's RERA system in terms of speed or enforcement consistency. But they represent a regulatory backstop that gives an Indian investor standing to fight, with local courts, local advocates, and local knowledge of the system. That standing is worth something that a foreign jurisdiction simply cannot replicate for a retail investor operating at a distance.

## A NOTE ON WHAT DUBAI DEVELOPERS ARE REALLY DOING IN INDIA

The arrival of Emaar, Danube, DAMAC, and a cohort of smaller Dubai developers in Indian cities, holding events, hosting roadshows, and marketing aggressively to Tier 1 and Tier 2 city audiences, is not evidence that Dubai property is the right investment for every Indian with surplus capital.

It is evidence that Dubai developers have identified Indian high-net-worth individuals as a target-rich source of off-plan capital, and that the Indian market's combination of growing wealth, tax burden anxiety, and aspiration for global assets makes it highly susceptible to well-packaged investment narratives.

Emaar's India expansion is not a vote of confidence in the Indian market over Dubai. It is a global developer optimising its distribution strategy by taking its product to where buyers are. The fact that they are simultaneously building in India reflects the scale of Indian residential demand, not a preference signal for the local market over their own.

When a Dubai developer hosts a Chandigarh event at ₹8 crore entry level, they are not selling a diversified investment strategy. They are moving inventory. Understanding whose interest is being served in that room is the starting point of any intelligent response.

## THE MARKET PROFILE SPLIT: WHO SHOULD ACTUALLY BE INVESTING WHERE

![NRI Remote Due Diligence](/assets/images/blog/nri-remote-due-diligence-checklist.jpg)

The Dubai versus Mohali question does not have a universal answer. It has a conditional answer, and the condition is the investor's profile.

The Dubai thesis is viable and potentially strong for investors who:

* Are genuinely relocating to Dubai or the UAE, or plan significant time there annually (at least six months), enabling authentic UAE tax residency and on-the-ground asset management.
* Have substantial liquid capital already deployed across multiple asset classes in India, such that the Dubai deployment represents a genuine diversification rather than a concentration bet.
* Are in a phase of life where domestic operational obligations are minimal: no active legal disputes, no businesses requiring physical management, no family obligations requiring consistent regional presence.
* Have a time horizon of seven to ten years minimum on the asset, with no near-term liquidity requirements that could force a sub-optimal exit.
* Have access to professional cross-border tax and legal advisory support to ensure FEMA compliance, correct residency structuring, and DTAA-optimised income routing.
* Are buying completed, ready-to-rent inventory rather than off-plan units, thereby avoiding the forfeiture risk, construction delay risk, and minimum payment lockout period associated with under-construction purchases.

The Mohali thesis remains the stronger choice for investors who:

* Are Punjab-based, Tricity-rooted, or have aspirational and family ties to the Chandigarh metro area that make the regional demand dynamic personally meaningful.
* Are managing active businesses, legal obligations, or operational commitments that require their presence and attention in northern India.
* Are first-time or early-stage real estate investors who do not yet have the cross-border operational infrastructure to manage a foreign asset competently.
* Are deploying capital in the ₹50 lakh to ₹3 crore range, the entry point at which Dubai's high transaction costs (4% Dubai Land Department registration fee, trustee fees, agency commissions, and ongoing service charges) make the net yield advantage materially narrower than the gross numbers suggest.
* Prioritise asset proximity, liquidity, and the ability to exercise legal recourse locally over pure yield maximisation.
* Are investing for legacy and inheritance planning, where land and physical property within Indian jurisdiction carries title clarity and succession planning simplicity that overseas assets cannot match.
