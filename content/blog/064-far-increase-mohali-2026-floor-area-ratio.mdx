# FAR Increase in Mohali 2026: What the Floor Area Ratio Change Means for Property Owners and Buyers

The FAR (Floor Area Ratio) increase in Mohali for 2026 is one of the most significant regulatory updates for property owners in recent years, essentially allowing for more built-up area on existing residential and commercial plots. For the average homeowner, this change means you now have the legal right to build more than what was previously permitted: often translating to an additional 1–2 rooms or a larger living hall on each floor. For investors, it represents an immediate unlock of "hidden" value in older properties where the construction was capped by tighter regulations. To benefit from this, you must navigate a map revision process through the Municipal Committee or GMADA and pay the prescribed permission fees. Building without this approval remains illegal and will lead to complications during resale or when applying for separate utility connections.

## What is FAR and why did the government increase it?

In my decade of navigating Punjab's real estate regulations, I've seen many buyers get confused by technical jargon. FAR stands for Floor Area Ratio. It is a simple mathematical formula: Total covered area of all floors divided by the total plot area. If you have a 1,000 square foot plot and the FAR is 1.5, you can build a total of 1,500 square feet across all floors.

The 2026 increase in Mohali is a response to the growing demand for housing density. As land becomes scarcer and more expensive on the [Mohali real estate guide 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026), the government uses FAR to allow owners to build "upwards" rather than "outwards." This is a win-win: the city accommodates more people, and property owners see their asset value rise. 

When I was managing project approvals as a developer, we often hit walls where we had the land but weren't allowed to build enough units to make the economics work. This increase solves that problem for both large-scale developers and individual plot owners in sectors like 82A, 76, and 77.

## How much has the FAR actually increased?

The increase isn't a flat number across the board. It is categorised by plot size and location. In many GMADA-governed sectors, residential FAR that hovered around 1.65 to 1.9 has been pushed toward 2.0 or higher in specific zones. 

What does this look like in real life? Take the example of a client I recently advised on a map revision. On his 300 sq. yard plot, the previous cap meant he could only have three bedrooms with modest sizes. With the new 2026 FAR guidelines, he was able to add a proper servant room and expand his master bedroom by 150 square feet. That extra space changes the entire character of the home and its eventual resale price.

If you are looking at our [Mohali real estate FAQ 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-faq-2026), you'll see that "Can I build an extra floor?" is one of the most common questions. The answer is often tied directly to these new FAR limits.

## The Map Revision Process: Don't start the bricks without the paper

I've seen too many owners in Mohali make the mistake of starting construction as soon as they hear about a policy change. They think, "The law has changed, so I'm safe." 

That is a dangerous assumption.

Even if the government allows higher FAR, your specific building plan is what the Municipal Committee (MC) or GMADA looks at. If you build first and ask for permission later, you are looking at "compounding fees," which are significantly higher than "permission fees." In some cases, if you violate setbacks (the empty space you must leave on the sides), the MC might even order a demolition of the extra area.

The correct process is:
1. Hire a registered architect to draft a revised map based on the 2026 FAR rules.
2. Apply for "Map Revision" with the Municipal Committee or GMADA.
3. Pay the permission fees (often called purchasable FAR).
4. Wait for the sanctioned map and the official permission letter.

Only then should you call the contractor. In my experience, this paperwork takes 45–60 days if handled correctly. It’s a small price to pay for a lifetime of legal peace.

## Permission Fees: The cost of doing things right

People often ask me, "Amritpal, why should I pay the government for area I already own?" 

The logic is simple: you own the land, but the city provides the infrastructure — the roads, the sewage, the electricity. When you build more area, you put more load on that infrastructure. The permission fee helps cover those costs. 

In Mohali, these fees are calculated based on the circle rate of your sector. For a standard residential plot, you might be looking at a few lakhs of rupees. While that feels like a lot today, consider the "Sandeep case" I often reference. We were dealing with a property tax and mutation issue where the lack of proper documentation on construction years and approved areas held up a major transaction for months. By paying your fees and getting a revised map, you ensure your property has a clean title and a clear [Mohali property registration process guide 2026](/blog/mohali-property-registration-process-guide-2026).

## Why getting approval matters for resale and utility connections

If you plan to sell your property in the next 5–10 years, the FAR approval is your biggest asset. Today’s buyers, especially the ones I deal with in the [municipal committee property tax mohali NDC](/blog/municipal-committee-property-tax-mohali-ndc) category, are very savvy. They will ask for the No Dues Certificate (NDC) and the Occupation Certificate (OC). 

If you have built an extra floor or rooms using the increased FAR but haven't updated your map, you won't get a clean NDC. Without an NDC, the registry won't happen. You'll be forced to pay the compounding fees at the last minute, often under pressure, or lose the buyer.

Furthermore, if you want a separate electricity connection for the newly constructed area — a common requirement for multi-floor buildings — the PSPCL will ask for the approved building plan. Shared meters lead to disputes, and separate meters require legal construction.

## The Opportunity: Hidden value in older sectors

The biggest beneficiaries of the FAR increase aren't the new buyers in Aerocity or IT City, but the owners in established sectors. Older houses in Mohali were often built with much lower FAR. If you own an old property that is now in a prime commercial or high-density residential zone, you might be sitting on "dead" square footage. 

By applying for the 2026 FAR increase, you can legally add area that makes the property much more attractive for rental or for large families. It is effectively "creating" real estate where none existed before. This is what I call "Vision Investing" — seeing the regulatory change as a value-add to your existing portfolio.

## A warning on setbacks and structural stability

While the government has increased the FAR, they haven't removed the rules about setbacks. You still need to leave specific distances from your plot boundaries. You cannot just cover every inch of your land. 

Also, as someone who has been on the developer side, I must give you a blunt warning: just because the law says you *can* build an extra floor doesn't mean your foundation *should* carry it. Many older houses in Mohali were built for G+1 or G+2. Before you use the new FAR to add a third floor, get a structural engineer to check your pillars and foundation. A legal house is no good if it isn't a safe one.

---

The 2026 FAR increase is a major gift to Mohali property owners, but it is one that must be unwrapped carefully. Don't let the excitement of extra space lead you into the trap of illegal construction. Follow the map revision process, pay the fees, and secure your property's legal and financial future. If you are evaluating a property to buy, always check if the existing construction matches the approved map — and if there is still "leftover" FAR you can use to add value later.
