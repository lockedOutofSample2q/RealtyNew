# FAR Increase in Mohali 2026: What the Floor Area Ratio Change Means for Property Owners and Buyers

The FAR (Floor Area Ratio) increase in Mohali for 2026 is a pivotal regulatory update that permits property owners to build more covered area on their existing residential and commercial plots. For the average homeowner, this change provides the legal right to expand their living space beyond previous limits: often allowing for an additional 1 to 2 rooms or a larger floor plate. For investors, it unlocks hidden value in older properties where construction was previously capped by tighter rules. To utilize this increased FAR, you must apply for a map revision through the Municipal Committee or GMADA and pay the required permission fees. Building without this formal approval remains illegal and will complicate future resales or utility connections. Having managed complex project approvals as a developer, I know that navigating these zoning changes correctly is the difference between an appreciating asset and a structural liability.

## What is FAR and why did the government increase it?

In my decade of navigating Punjab's real estate regulations, I have seen many buyers get confused by technical jargon. FAR stands for Floor Area Ratio. It is a simple mathematical formula: total covered area of all floors divided by the total plot area. If you have a 1,000 square foot plot and the FAR is 1.5, you can build a total of 1,500 square feet across all floors.

The 2026 increase in Mohali is a response to the growing demand for housing density. As land becomes scarcer and more expensive on the [Mohali real estate guide 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026), the government uses FAR to allow owners to build upwards rather than outwards. This is a benefit for all: the city accommodates more residents, and property owners see their asset value rise. When I was managing project approvals as a developer, we often faced constraints where we had the land but were restricted in the units we could build. This increase solves that problem for both developers and individual plot owners in sectors like 82A, 76, and 77.

## How much has the FAR actually increased?

The increase is not a flat number across the board; it is categorized by plot size and location. In many GMADA-governed sectors, residential FAR that previously sat around 1.65 to 1.9 has been pushed toward 2.0 or higher in specific zones. 

What does this look like in real life? Take the example of a client I recently advised on a map revision. On his 300 sq. yard plot, the previous cap meant he could only have three bedrooms with modest sizes. With the new 2026 FAR guidelines, he was able to add a proper servant room and expand his master bedroom by 150 square feet. That extra space changes the entire character of the home and its eventual resale price. If you are looking at our [Mohali real estate FAQ 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-faq-2026), you will see that "Can I build an extra floor?" is one of the most common questions. The answer is now tied directly to these new FAR limits.

## The Map Revision Process: Do not start the bricks without the paper

I have seen too many owners in Mohali make the mistake of starting construction as soon as they hear about a policy change. They assume that because the law has changed, they are safe. That is a dangerous assumption. Even if the government allows higher FAR, your specific building plan is what the Municipal Committee (MC) or GMADA evaluates. If you build first and ask for permission later, you face compounding fees: which are significantly higher than standard permission fees.

The correct process is:
1. Hire a registered architect to draft a revised map based on the 2026 FAR rules.
2. Apply for Map Revision with the Municipal Committee or GMADA.
3. Pay the permission fees, often called purchasable FAR.
4. Wait for the sanctioned map and the official permission letter.

Only then should you start construction. In my experience, this paperwork takes 45 to 60 days if handled correctly. It is a small price to pay for a lifetime of legal peace.

## Permission Fees: The cost of doing things right

People often ask me why they should pay the government for area they already own. The logic is simple: you own the land, but the city provides the infrastructure, such as the roads, sewage, and electricity. When you build more area, you put more load on that infrastructure. The permission fee helps cover those costs. 

In Mohali, these fees are calculated based on the circle rate of your sector. For a standard residential plot, you might be looking at a few lakhs of rupees. While that feels like a lot today, consider the "Sandeep case" I often reference. We were dealing with a property tax and mutation issue where the lack of proper documentation on construction years held up a major transaction for months. By paying your fees and getting a revised map, you ensure your property has a clean title and a clear [Mohali property registration process](/blog/mohali-property-registration-process-guide-2026).

## Why getting approval matters for resale and utility connections

If you plan to sell your property in the next 5 to 10 years, the FAR approval is your biggest asset. Today’s buyers, especially the ones I deal with in the [Municipal Committee property tax NDC](/blog/municipal-committee-property-tax-mohali-ndc) category, are very savvy. They will ask for the No Dues Certificate (NDC) and the Occupation Certificate (OC). 

If you have built an extra floor using the increased FAR but have not updated your map, you will not get a clean NDC. Without an NDC, the registry will not happen. You will be forced to pay compounding fees at the last minute, often under pressure, or lose the buyer. Furthermore, if you want a separate electricity connection for the newly constructed area: a common requirement for multi-floor buildings: the PSPCL will ask for the approved building plan.

## The Opportunity: Hidden value in older sectors

The biggest beneficiaries of the FAR increase are often the owners in established sectors. Older houses in Mohali were often built with much lower FAR. If you own an old property that is now in a prime commercial or high-density residential zone, you might be sitting on dead square footage. 

By applying for the 2026 FAR increase, you can legally add area that makes the property much more attractive for rental or for large families. It is effectively creating real estate where none existed before. This is what I call Vision Investing: seeing the regulatory change as a value-add to your existing portfolio.

## A warning on setbacks and structural stability

While the government has increased the FAR, they have not removed the rules about setbacks. You still need to leave specific distances from your plot boundaries. You cannot cover every inch of your land. 

Also, as someone who has been on the developer side, I must give you a blunt warning: just because the law says you can build an extra floor does not mean your foundation should carry it. Many older houses in Mohali were built for G+1 or G+2. Before you use the new FAR to add a third floor, get a structural engineer to check your pillars and foundation. A legal house is no good if it is not a safe one.
