# What Happens to Your Booking Amount If a Mohali Project Gets Stalled or Cancelled

If your Mohali real estate project is stalled or cancelled, you are legally entitled to a full refund of your booking amount along with interest at the SBI Highest Marginal Cost of Lending Rate (MCLR) plus 2%, as mandated by RERA Punjab. Under Section 18 of the Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, if a developer fails to give possession of an apartment, plot, or building in accordance with the terms of the agreement for sale, they must return the amount received with interest if the buyer chooses to withdraw. If you choose to stay in the project, the builder is liable to pay interest for every month of delay until possession is handed over. The practical recovery path involves filing a formal complaint through the RERA Punjab portal, followed by an adjudication process that typically takes 6 to 12 months for a final order.

## What is the difference between a stalled project and a cancelled one?

In the Mohali market, we see two distinct situations that affect your money. A stalled project is one where construction has stopped due to cash flow issues, partnership disputes, or temporary regulatory hurdles, but the project registration remains active. A cancelled project is far more serious: this usually happens when a critical approval, like a Forest Department NOC or a Change of Land Use (CLU) permission, is revoked by the government.

I have personally navigated a situation where a project faced a Forest Department approval cancellation. It is a stressful period for any buyer. When a project is cancelled, the developer's legal right to build is gone. In these cases, the refund is not just a right: it becomes the only logical exit. Stalled projects often tempt buyers to "wait and watch," but if the delay exceeds six months without a clear restart date, your capital is essentially being held hostage without compensation. Understanding which category your project falls into is the first step in our [complete buyer protection guide](/blog/mohali-property-buyer-protection-guide).

## How does RERA Punjab protect your booking amount?

Before RERA, a builder could sit on your money for years with a measly 5 rupee per square foot penalty clause. Today, the law is weighted in your favour. RERA Punjab (rera.punjab.gov.in) mandates that 70% of all project collections must be kept in a separate escrow account. This money is meant solely for construction and land costs.

If the project fails to meet its milestones, you can invoke Section 18. This section is your strongest weapon. It states that if the promoter fails to complete or is unable to give possession by the date specified in the agreement, they shall be liable, on demand, to return the amount received with interest. This interest is not a small sum: it is currently calculated at nearly 10 to 11% per annum. Whether you are looking at a luxury flat or a [GMADA licensed plot](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026), these rules apply across the board. The [Mohali real estate FAQ](/blog/mohali-real-estate-faq-2026) covers many such nuances for different project types.

## What is the step-by-step process for claiming a refund?

The recovery of your money is a process of persistence, not just paperwork. Do not rely on verbal promises from a sales manager who is likely looking for another job. You must follow a formal sequence:

1. Send a formal legal notice: Notify the developer in writing that you are withdrawing from the project due to the delay or cancellation and demand a refund with interest within 45 days.
2. File a RERA Complaint (Form N): If the developer does not respond or refuses, you file an online complaint at the RERA Punjab portal. You will need your allotment letter, payment receipts, and the builder-buyer agreement.
3. Attend Adjudication: An adjudicating officer will hear both sides. If the delay is proven, the officer will pass an order for refund plus interest.
4. Execution of Order: If the builder still does not pay, you apply for the execution of the order, which allows the RERA authority to recover the money as arrears of land revenue through the Deputy Commissioner's office.

This process is detailed further in our guide on [how to file a RERA complaint in Punjab](/blog/rera-complaint-punjab-file-process-2026).

## How long does it actually take to get your money back?

I will be honest with you: it is rarely a 30-day affair. While the law aims for quick resolution, the reality on the ground in Mohali is that an adjudication process takes about 6 to 9 months. If the developer is in deep financial trouble, the execution of the order can take another 3 to 6 months.

However, persistence works. I recall a case where a client had paid 65% of the purchase price on two plots. The builder had cancelled the plots for non-payment by the previous owner, without informing the new buyer. We didn't just wait for the system to move: we followed up persistently with the developer and the regulatory bodies. It took eight months of chasing, but we finally got the plots reinstated and reallocated to an adjacent location. ## Which specific documents must you preserve as evidence?

In a dispute, your documentation is your only shield. Many buyers lose their cases because they cannot prove their payments or the developer's promises. You must have an organized file containing:

- The original Allotment Letter and the Builder-Buyer Agreement (BBA).
- Every single payment receipt, including bank statements showing the debits.
- All correspondence with the developer — emails are better than WhatsApp, and registered letters are better than emails.
- The brochures and marketing material you were shown at the time of booking (these prove misrepresentation if the final project is different).
- Photos of the site taken at regular intervals to prove the lack of construction progress.

If you have lost any of these, try to retrieve them immediately. Developers often "lose" records when a project goes south.

## What interest rate are you entitled to on your refund?

Under the Punjab RERA Rules, the interest rate is defined as the "State Bank of India Highest Marginal Cost of Lending Rate plus two percent." If the SBI MCLR is 8.5%, you are entitled to 10.5% interest. This interest is calculated from the date the developer received each payment from you, not from the date the project stalled.

For a buyer who has invested ₹1 crore over three years, the interest alone can be upwards of ₹30 lakhs. Builders know this, and often they will try to offer you a "credit note" for another project or a refund without interest. My advice is simple: do not settle for less than what the law provides. Your capital has an opportunity cost, and the interest is there to cover that loss.

## When should you stop making payments to a stalled project?

This is a delicate question. If you stop payments without a legal basis, the developer can cancel your allotment for "default" and forfeit your booking amount (usually 10-20% of the total cost). However, continuing to pay into a sinking ship is equally dangerous.

The general rule is: if the developer has missed a RERA milestone and is more than six months late on a construction stage, you have a legal right to withhold further payments until the milestone is achieved. You must communicate this in writing, citing the RERA project timeline. Do not just stop the cheques silently. If the project is officially cancelled by a regulatory body like GMADA or the Forest Department, you should stop all payments immediately and file for a refund.

## Can the developer force you to take another property instead of a refund?

No. This is a common tactic used by Mohali developers who are facing "inventory overhang." They will tell you that the project you booked is "on hold" but they can shift your booking to another project in New Chandigarh or Sector 82A.

You are under no legal obligation to accept this. A contract for a specific flat on a specific floor is a unique contract. If the developer cannot deliver that specific unit, they have breached the contract. You have the right to say no and demand your money back. Shifting your booking often means resetting your RERA timeline and potentially entering another project that might face similar issues. 

## What happens if the project is stalled due to a partnership dispute?

Real estate development is a capital-intensive business, and partnership breakdowns are more common than people realize. When partners fight, construction usually stops. The banks freeze the accounts, and the sales team stops receiving commissions.

In this scenario, your rights remain the same. The "Promoter" (which is the entity registered with RERA) is responsible to you, regardless of who is fighting in the boardroom. If the partnership dispute leads to a delay beyond the RERA completion date, you can file for a refund. I have seen these disputes freeze projects for years. If you notice signs of internal conflict — such as frequent changes in the authorised signatory or conflicting instructions from the site office — it is time to review your position.

## How do you verify if your project is officially stalled?

Do not wait for a letter from the builder. They will rarely admit a project is stalled until they have no choice. Instead, monitor the "Quarterly Progress Reports" (QPR) on the RERA Punjab website. Every registered project must upload photos and data showing construction progress every three months.

If the QPR has not been updated for two quarters, or if the photos show no progress in the RCC structure for six months, the project is effectively stalled. You can also visit the site and check the hygiene: are there active workers, is material being delivered, or is the equipment rusting? Broader market data in our [Mohali property price history](/blog/mohali-property-price-history-2026) can also show if a particular belt is facing systemic delays.

## What is the recovery path for projects that never got RERA registration?

This is the "Red Zone" of Mohali real estate. If you booked in a pre-launch project that never received RERA registration, you cannot approach the RERA authority. In these cases, you are looking at a consumer court complaint or a civil suit for recovery of money.

The recovery is harder and takes longer. This is why I always tell my clients: never pay more than a nominal token amount until you see the RERA registration number. If you are already in this situation, the best path is often a criminal complaint under Section 138 for any bounced cheques or a complaint for cheating if the land ownership itself was misrepresented. It is a hard road, but as someone who has navigated regulatory cancellations, I know that sitting silent is the only way to definitely lose your money.
