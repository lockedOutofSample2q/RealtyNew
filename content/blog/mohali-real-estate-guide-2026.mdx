## The Regulatory Framework: GMADA, PUDA, and RERA Punjab

Navigating the Punjab real estate market requires an understanding of the five regulatory bodies that govern every square foot of land. Relying on "builder promises" is a legacy mistake that 2026 investors cannot afford.

### Greater Mohali Area Development Authority (GMADA)
GMADA remains the ultimate authority for urban planning in the core Mohali sectors. It controls plot allotments through draws and auctions, manages colony licensing, and maintains official property transfer records. A critical process detail for 2026: a property transfer requires a certified copy with an official Tehsildar stamp. Digital photos of documents are insufficient for legal standing. The transfer process typically takes 4 to 8 weeks when documentation is complete, and OTP-based verification is sent to the last registered buyer.

### PUDA and Municipal Committees
While GMADA handles the core, the Punjab Urban Development Authority (PUDA) oversees licensing in areas outside GMADA jurisdiction. It is vital to distinguish between a project that has "applied for PUDA approval" and one that is "PUDA approved." CLU (Change of Land Use) and layout approvals are separate milestones. Furthermore, the Municipal Committee governs building plan sanctions and completion certificates (OC). Recent changes in 2026 have seen an increase in Floor Area Ratio (FAR) across Mohali, allowing for additional construction, but these revisions must be cleared through the Municipal Committee to avoid litigation.

### RERA Punjab Compliance
RERA has matured into a powerful tool for buyer protection. Before committing capital, investors should verify the project registration number on the official RERA Punjab portal. In 2026, the focus has shifted toward checking the promoter's past delivery track record and the nature of existing complaints. Common disputes often involve possession delays or deficiencies in service. While the RERA authority targets a 60-day resolution window, practical experience suggests a timeline of 6 to 12 months for complex adjudications.

## Property Categories and Investment Thesis

The Mohali market in 2026 is categorized into six distinct property types, each with a unique risk-reward profile.

1.  **Residential Flats:** The focus is on luxury segments in GMADA-approved projects. The acceptable loading factor is now standardized at 25 to 30 percent. Anything exceeding 35 percent is a significant red flag for buyers.
2.  **GMADA and Licensed Plots:** These remain the most liquid assets. Plots in Sector 76 to 80 and the New Chandigarh corridor continue to see consistent demand.
3.  **Commercial SCOs:** In the JLPL Sector 82 area, SCOs that were available for Rs 3-4 crore a few years ago now trade between Rs 12-16 crore. This appreciation is tied directly to the IT corridor growth.
4.  **Industrial Plots:** Red zone industrial plots near Rajpura and Banur are emerging as the "vision investment" of 2026. Industrial land purchased at Rs 18.70 lakh per vigha has seen appreciation to Rs 45 lakh per vigha post-registration within a six-month window.
5.  **Agricultural Land:** Reinvestment from government land acquisition payouts remains a massive driver of liquidity in the Mohali market.
6.  **Pre-launch Bookings:** These offer the highest potential returns but carry the highest risk. Upfront payments are typically 25 percent, and due diligence on the developer's financial health is mandatory.

## The Buyer Process: Costs, Paperwork, and Pitfalls

A professional transaction in 2026 involves more than just a price agreement. The technicalities of registration and post-sale support are where the true value of an advisor is found.

### Stamp Duty and Registration
In Punjab, the stamp duty is 6 percent of the circle rate or the agreement value, whichever is higher. Registration fees add another 1 percent. Buyers must also account for hidden costs like Floor Rise Charges (FRC) and Club Membership fees, which can add 5 to 10 percent to the base price.

### The Importance of Separate IDs
For multi-floor buildings, it is critical to obtain separate Municipal Committee property tax IDs and separate PSPCL electrical connections for each floor. We have handled cases where back-dated property tax appeared as a collective liability for all floors, stalling transfers for months. A separate ID is essential for obtaining a No Dues Certificate (NDC) during resale.

### Maintenance and Loading
Maintenance charges in premium Mohali projects range from Rs 3-4 per square foot for basic services to Rs 7-8 per square foot for full-service luxury amenities. Understanding the "carpet area" versus "super built-up area" is vital. If the loading factor is too high, your effective price per usable square foot may be much higher than the market average.

## Special Considerations for NRIs

For the NRI diaspora, Mohali remains a top-tier investment destination, but the 2026 tax and legal environment requires careful navigation.

Under FEMA regulations, NRIs can purchase residential and commercial property without RBI approval, but agricultural land and farmhouses are restricted. All payments must be routed through NRE or NRO accounts.

The biggest hurdle for NRIs is the TDS (Tax Deducted at Source). On long-term capital gains, the TDS rate is 22.66 percent (20 percent plus surcharge and cess). On short-term gains, it jumps to 30 percent plus applicable surcharges. Repatriation of sale proceeds is limited to USD 1 million per year through the NRO account, requiring Form 15CA and 15CB.

Remote management is another concern. In 2026, we provide digital walkthroughs and use Power of Attorney (POA) frameworks to allow NRIs to close deals without traveling to India. However, the POA must be properly adjudicated in India to be valid for registration.

## Guidance for Land Sellers: The 24-Month Window

Land sellers in Punjab, particularly those receiving government acquisition payouts, face a unique challenge: the "reinvestment trap." Many sellers park their money in Fixed Deposits (FDs), which offer approximately 7 percent gross returns. After a 30 percent tax slab and 6 percent inflation, the net real return is near zero or negative.

Under Section 10(37), capital gains on compulsorily acquired agricultural land are often exempt. However, to preserve other exemptions under Section 54F, sellers must reinvest in residential property within a 2-year window (for purchase) or a 3-year window (for construction).

The 24-month window is the most critical timeframe. If a reinvestment decision is not made, the proceeds should be parked in the Capital Gains Account Scheme to maintain tax benefits.
