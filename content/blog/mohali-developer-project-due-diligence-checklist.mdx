# Developer & Project Due Diligence Guide for Mohali 2026: The Complete Checklist Before Booking

To perform comprehensive due diligence on a Mohali developer in 2026, you must verify seven critical layers: RERA Punjab registration status, clear land title ownership, the developer's actual delivery track record, escrow account compliance, the financial feasibility of the payment plan, construction quality indicators, and future exit liquidity. Most buyers stop at the RERA number, but that is only the beginning. You must investigate the internal health of a project: checking if the developer has the cash flow to finish and ensuring the land is free of undisclosed encumbrances. In Mohali's current fast-moving market, the risk is not just losing money but being stuck in a project that stalls months before possession. Having served as a director and sales head for a RERA-approved developer, I know exactly which documents hide the real truth of a project’s progress.

## What is the first step in Mohali project due diligence?

The first step is always RERA Punjab Verification. Do not take a "RERA Applied For" status as a green light. In my experience as a former developer, I know that many projects get stuck in the application phase due to layout issues or pending CLU (Change of Land Use) approvals.

Go to the RERA Punjab portal and search for the specific project. Check the Quarterly Progress Reports (QPR). If the developer has not updated these in two quarters, it is a significant red flag. You should also check the Promoter Profile to see if there are ongoing litigations or if they have defaulted on other projects. A RERA number confirms the project is legal, but the QPR tells you if it is alive.

## How do you verify land title and ownership in Punjab?

You cannot rely on the developer's word for land ownership. You must ask for the Allotment Letter (if it is a GMADA plot) or the Sale Deed and Mutation (Intkal) records. In Punjab, a title dispute can take years to resolve. I have seen cases where developers sell projects on collaborated land where the original landowner has not been fully paid. If the partnership breaks down, the buyer is the first to suffer. Always ask:
1. Is the land owned by the developer or is it a Joint Development Agreement (JDA)?
2. Is there an Encumbrance Certificate (EC) for the last 13 to 30 years?
3. Is the land free from any lien or bank mortgage that has not been disclosed?
4. Has the project status been verified against the physical layout?

For more on avoiding these traps, read about [title disputes in Punjab property](/blog/title-dispute-punjab-property-how-to-avoid).

## How do I check a Mohali developer's delivery track record?

Do not look at their current fancy office; look at their last three finished projects. Go there and talk to the residents. Ask them if the developer delivered on the promised date and if the super built-up area matched the initial promises. A developer might have a branded name, but if their last two projects in New Chandigarh or Aerocity were delayed by 24 months, you are likely looking at a similar fate.

## What does a "Stalled" project look like before it fails?

Most people only realize a project has failed when the gates are locked. But there are warning signs six months before. Having been on the developer side, I can tell you that the first sign is usually a slowdown in material deliveries at the site. If the cement trucks stop arriving, the cash flow has likely dried up.

Another sign is a sudden change in the payment plan. If a developer suddenly offers a massive discount for 100% upfront payment, they are likely desperate for liquidity to pay off old debts. This is especially critical for commercial investments where [Mohali SCO investment due diligence](/blog/mohali-sco-investment-due-diligence-commercial) requires higher scrutiny. You can read my full list of [warning signs of a stalled Mohali project](/blog/mohali-project-stalled-warning-signs-developer) to protect your capital.

## Is the Escrow Account actually protecting your money?

Under RERA, 70% of the money collected from buyers must be deposited into a separate escrow account used only for construction and land costs. Ask the developer for the escrow account details and verify that your cheques are being deposited there.

If a developer asks you to make payments to a different company name or an individual's account, walk away. This is the most common way money is diverted to buy more land instead of finishing your building. This is why [prelaunch pricing](/blog/prelaunch-pricing-mohali-real-cost-calculation) can be so risky: the money often goes toward getting the approvals that should have been there in the first place.

## Why is "Exit Liquidity" a part of due diligence?

You might be buying for your own use, but you should always think like an investor. If you needed to sell this property in three years, who would buy it?

- Is the project accessible via a major road like Airport Road?
- Does the project have unique features that will maintain its value?

## Case Study: The 8-Month Plot Recovery

I once handled a case where a client had paid 65% of the purchase price on two plots in a licensed colony. The seller had not disclosed that the builder had already cancelled the plots for non-payment by the previous owner. We only discovered this when we initiated the transfer process. It took eight months of persistent follow-up with the builder and the regulatory authorities to reinstate those plots. This is why [buyer protection](/blog/mohali-property-buyer-protection-guide) is not just a phrase: it is a process of verifying every document before the first cheque is signed.
