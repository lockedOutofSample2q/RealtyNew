# 10 Questions to Ask Any Mohali Property Advisor Before Trusting Them With Your NRI Investment

Trust is the single most expensive currency in Mohali real estate, especially when you are investing from thousands of miles away. For an NRI in Canada, the UK, or Australia, the fear of being cheated often outweighs the desire for ROI. To ensure your investment is safe, you must ask a prospective advisor these 10 critical questions: How do they earn their income? Will they tell you not to buy a project? What happens after the sale? How is RERA verification handled? Do they provide live video site visits? What is their experience with GMADA and regulatory bodies? How many NRI deals have they closed? Can they provide references? What specific documents do they review? And finally, what is their long-term communication commitment? If an advisor cannot answer these with data and specific experience, they are likely a broker for the developer, not a consultant for you.

## How do you earn your income: is it through commissions or a flat fee?

In my years of navigating the Mohali market, I have seen that the way an advisor gets paid dictates the advice they give you. If someone is working strictly on a commission paid by the developer, their primary goal is to close the deal. They are, effectively, an extension of the developer’s sales team. There is nothing inherently wrong with commissions, but there must be transparency.

A true consultant should be able to tell you: "I earn a standard commission here, but I have also turned down commissions from three other projects this month because the delivery record was poor." When you are looking at our [Complete Mohali Real Estate Guide 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026), you will see that the depth of due diligence required is massive. If your advisor's only goal is the transaction, they might overlook the red flags that an independent fee-based or transparent advisor would catch. You are paying for their ability to say "no" as much as their ability to find a "yes."

## Will you tell me not to buy if you think a specific Mohali project is wrong for my goals?

Most brokers will tell you every project is "the best opportunity in Mohali." But in a market where [appreciation vs stagnation zones](/blog/mohali-property-appreciation-vs-stagnation-zones) are separated by just a few kilometres, everything cannot be a "buy." I often tell my clients that "Je aapaan vision ton bina challaange taan aapaan mehngi cheez khareeddaange" (If we move without vision, we will buy at the wrong price).

Ask your advisor to show you a project they recently recommended against. If they cannot name one, they aren't advising you: they are selling to you. A consultant who values the long-term relationship will warn you about a developer with cash flow stress or a project with pending Forest Department clearances, even if it means they lose a commission today. This is the structural difference I discuss in our guide on the [difference between a broker and a consultant](/blog/real-estate-broker-vs-consultant-mohali-difference).

## What specific support do you provide after the transaction is completed?

For an NRI, the real work begins after the booking amount is paid. This is where most advisors disappear. I have handled cases, like the one for a client named Sandeep, where the sale was just the beginning. We spent three months coordinating between three different floor owners to separate property tax IDs, drafting an MOU to allocate liability, and ensuring electricity meters were transferred correctly.

Ask if your advisor will help with:
*   GMADA ownership transfer and mutation (Intkal)
*   Obtaining the No Dues Certificate (NDC)
*   Separating property tax IDs for multi-floor buildings
*   Liaisoning with PSPCL for new electricity connections

If their answer is "that's the developer's job," you are speaking to a broker. A consultant knows that these post-sale hurdles are exactly why an NRI needs an advisor in the first place. This is a core part of the [NRI Property Guide for Mohali](/blog/nri-property-guide-mohali-2026) that every offshore investor should study.

## How do you handle RERA Punjab verification for new projects?

Checking a RERA number is the bare minimum. A professional advisor dives into the [RERA Punjab portal](/blog/how-to-check-rera-punjab-property) to look at the details most people miss. They should be checking the quarterly progress reports, the number of active complaints against the promoter, and whether the land is free of encumbrances.

When I was a director for a RERA-approved development firm, I saw firsthand how these filings are managed. I know that a "clean" RERA record on paper doesn't always reflect the ground reality of construction speed. Your advisor should be able to compare the RERA completion date with the actual pace of material delivery at the site. If they aren't doing this level of technical due diligence, you are flying blind.

## Do you accompany clients to site visits via video call?

You shouldn't have to rely on a glossy PDF brochure. Remote due diligence requires a live, unfiltered view of the property. I regularly take my NRI clients on video walkthroughs where we don't just look at the show flat; we look at the lift shafts, the basement waterproofing, and the quality of the bricks being used.

Ask your advisor: "Will you go to the site at 10 AM on a Tuesday and show me the actual number of workers on the floor?" This transparency is the only way to verify a developer's claims. If they are hesitant to do this, they are likely not spending enough time on the ground themselves.

## Who is your primary contact point for regulatory matters at GMADA?

In Mohali, you are often dealing with five different regulatory bodies: GMADA, PUDA, PSPCL, the Municipal Committee, and sometimes the Forest Department. An advisor must understand the internal processes of each. For example, [GMADA plot transfers](/blog/gmada-plot-transfer-ownership-process) require specific certified copies and OTPs that only go to the last registered buyer.

Having navigated these approvals as a developer, I know that "knowing people" is less important than "knowing the process." Your advisor should be able to explain exactly why a mutation is stuck or how to resolve a back-dated property tax dispute. If they use vague language about "managing the officials," be careful. Real expertise is procedural, not just social.

## How many NRI transactions have you personally handled in the last three years?

The NRI buyer segment has unique legal requirements. From FEMA compliance to the [22.66% TDS rate on property sales](/blog/nri-tax-property-sale-mohali-tds-capital-gains), the financial math is different. An advisor who primarily handles local residents might not be familiar with the nuances of NRO/NRE account payments or the apostille requirements for a Power of Attorney executed in Canada or the UK.

I have personally closed over 180 transactions, many of which involved NRIs navigating these exact complexities. Experience is the only thing that prevents expensive mistakes in repatriation and tax compliance.

## Can you share references or anonymised case studies of past NRI clients?

Specifics are the only credential that matters. Don't settle for "I have many happy clients." Ask for a story. For instance, I often share our [8-month plot recovery case study](/blog/mohali-plot-cancelled-booking-recovery-process). A client had paid 65% for two plots, only to find the builder had cancelled them due to a previous owner's default. It took eight months of persistent chasing to get those plots reinstated and reallocated.

These stories prove that your advisor knows how to fight for you when things go wrong. If they don't have these "battle stories," they likely haven't handled enough complex transactions to protect you effectively.

## What specific documentation do you review before recommending a property?

A brochure is not a legal document. Before I recommend any project, I look at the land title (Jamabandi/Fard), the approved layout plan from PUDA or GMADA, the Change of Land Use (CLU) certificate, and the No Objection Certificates (NOCs) from relevant departments.

If an advisor tells you a project is "fully approved" but hasn't seen the Forest Department clearance for a project near a green belt, they are putting your capital at risk. I’ve seen projects face cancellation because a buffer distance wasn't maintained. You can read more about these risks in our [Mohali Real Estate FAQ 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-faq-2026).

## What is your communication commitment once the payment is made?

The biggest complaint I hear from NRIs is that their "advisor" stopped taking calls once the commission was settled. You need a commitment on paper or in a clear message:
*   How often will you receive photos and videos of construction progress?
*   Will they notify you of upcoming payment milestones before the builder sends the demand letter?
*   Who handles the coordination for the registry and possession day?

At RHMC, I believe the post-sale relationship is where we earn our name. Whether it is resolving an MC tax ID issue months after the sale or helping you find a tenant, the advisory must be continuous.
