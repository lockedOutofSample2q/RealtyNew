# The Worst Mohali Property Advice Circulating Right Now — and Why It's Wrong

![Analyzing Mohali real estate trends for 2026](/images/mohali-real-estate-trends-2026.jpg "Alt: Senior property advisor Amritpal Singh analyzing Mohali real estate appreciation trends for 2026")

The worst property advice currently circulating in Mohali is that "location is everything," "prelaunch always offers the best ROI," and "prices will never stop rising." These myths lead buyers into stagnant investments or high-risk defaults. In reality, Mohali’s 2026 market is driven by the **Expansion Principle**: property appreciates where growth is still possible, such as new corridors linked by Bharatmala or IT City, while land-locked areas like Phase 7 often stagnate. Furthermore, prelaunch "discounts" are frequently offset by payment plan risks, and a developer’s brand name is secondary to their actual RERA delivery record. To protect your capital, you must look past these surface-level slogans and evaluate the specific infrastructure triggers, regulatory approvals, and exit liquidity of every deal.

## Is "Location, Location, Location" still the golden rule in Mohali?

We have all heard the cliché: "Buy the worst house in the best location." In the Mohali of ten years ago, this might have held weight. Today, it is dangerous advice because it ignores how cities actually grow. If you buy in a "prime" location that is already fully built-out with no room for new infrastructure, new corporate offices, or new residential expansion, you are likely buying into a stagnation zone.

I have seen commercial units in the old Phase 3B2 and Phase 7 markets stay at the same price for several years while rental yields compressed. Why? Because there is no room to grow. The "prime" factor is already fully priced in, and there is no "delta" left for appreciation. Compare this to the [mohali-property-appreciation-vs-stagnation-zones](/blog/mohali-property-appreciation-vs-stagnation-zones) I have mapped out for my clients. The buyers who made real, life-changing money didn't just buy a "good location"; they bought in the path of expansion before the crowd arrived.

The "Expansion Principle" is simple: appreciation follows the capacity for more. When I look at Sector 82A or the IT City corridor, I see room for thousands of employees and hundreds of new businesses. That capacity drives demand, which drives prices. I recall the Airport Road commercial units in Sector 82 that were available at ₹3–4 crore a few years ago. Today, those same units trade at ₹12–16 crore. The infrastructure—the airport expansion, the IT corridor, and the Bharatmala connectivity—was always coming. The buyers who made money were the ones who saw it before it arrived, while those stuck in "prime" residential phases saw only nominal gains. If you aren't buying in an area that can expand its infrastructure, you are buying a bond, not a growth asset.

## Why is "Prelaunch is always the safest way to double your money" a dangerous myth?

![Mohali construction site representing prelaunch risk](/images/mohali-construction-site-prelaunch.jpg "Alt: A Mohali construction site illustrating the risks associated with prelaunch property investments")

This is perhaps the most expensive lie told in Mohali sales galleries. Brokers love prelaunch because the ticket size feels smaller and the "early bird discount" looks attractive on a glossy spreadsheet. But as someone who has sat on the developer side of the table as a director and sales head, I know that a prelaunch discount is simply the "risk premium" you are being paid to take on the developer’s uncertainty.

When you buy at prelaunch, you are often buying before the final regulatory clearances are in hand. I have personally navigated the complexity of obtaining project approvals from PUDA, PSPCL, the Municipal Committee, and the Forest Department. I have seen situations where a forest department approval was granted and then contested or reversed, stalling projects for months or even years. If a project stalls, that 15% "discount" evaporates instantly under the weight of your bank loan interest and the opportunity cost of your capital.

In our [mohali-builder-payment-plan-red-flags](/blog/mohali-builder-payment-plan-red-flags) guide, I explain how a developer’s cash flow health is the real indicator of your safety. A project that relies on prelaunch funds to start construction is inherently fragile. The safest way to invest in Mohali isn't necessarily the earliest; it is the most verified. I have seen clients pay 65% of the purchase price on plots only to discover months later that the builder had cancelled the allotment due to a previous owner’s default. Verification at the point of purchase, including a thorough RERA search and title check, is non-negotiable.

## Does a "Branded Developer" always guarantee a better investment?

"Just buy with a big name and you won't have any problems." I hear this from NRIs and CXOs every week. While a brand name is a signal, it is not a guarantee of ROI or quality. In my decade of closing 180+ transactions, I have seen local Mohali developers deliver superior construction quality and faster possession because their entire reputation is tied solely to this one city.

A national brand often has its capital and management bandwidth stretched across projects in Gurgaon, Noida, and Bangalore. If one of those projects hits a legal or financial snag, the Mohali site's progress might slow down to compensate. A local developer with a clean RERA record and 3-4 successfully delivered projects in the Tricity is often a more liquid and reliable bet. They are physically here, and they cannot afford to leave a project incomplete.

Before you pay a 20-30% "brand premium," look at the actual RERA Punjab delivery data. How many months past the promised date did they actually hand over the keys? In my experience, the post-sale relationship is where an advisor earns their name. Whether it is resolving property tax ID issues or separate electricity connections—as I did in a recent case that took three months of coordination with the Municipal Committee and PSPCL—a local player or a dedicated advisor can often solve problems that a corporate "brand" would simply ignore. A brand might sell you the flat, but they won't help you separate your property tax ID when the seller leaves a mess.

## Is buying "near the International Airport" a guaranteed commercial win?

![Mohali International Airport Road commercial corridor](/images/mohali-airport-road-commercial.jpg "Alt: High-visibility commercial showrooms along Airport Road in Mohali near the International Airport")

This is a classic "vision" mistake. People see the airport expansion and assume any SCO or showroom within a 5-kilometer radius will become a goldmine. While I am bullish on certain airport corridors, I have also seen units just one sector away struggle to find even a basic tenant for years. Proximity to the airport is a driver, but it is not a destination in itself.

Proximity to the airport only works if it is coupled with a clear catchment area, high-visibility frontage, and future-proof connectivity. If the road connectivity—like the upcoming Bharatmala links—bypasses your sector, your "airport-adjacent" property becomes a quiet backwater. I often use the example of the Rajpura industrial belt, where 62 vigha was purchased at ₹18.70 lakh per vigha. Within six months, as the Bharatmala road connectivity became visible on the ground, the rate jumped to ₹45 lakh per vigha. That is vision investing.

In my years of [mohali-real-estate-guide-2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026), I have stressed that you aren't just buying distance from a runway; you are buying the movement of people and capital. A showroom on a 200-foot road with easy parking and a growing IT catchment is an asset. A showroom tucked away in a sector with "airport views" but no footfall is a liability that will drain your maintenance funds. Always ask: "Who is the tenant, and why would they pay rent here?" If you can't answer that, the airport won't save you.

## Will Mohali property prices "always go up" because of Chandigarh's saturation?

This is the macro-myth that prevents people from doing their due diligence. Yes, Chandigarh is saturated. Yes, HNI families from across Punjab are moving to Mohali for better schools and healthcare. But that does not mean every property in Mohali is a winner. Just because the tide is rising doesn't mean every boat is seaworthy.

We are currently seeing a [mohali-property-bubble-2026-analysis](/blog/mohali-property-bubble-2026-analysis) in certain overpriced resale pockets where the asking price has far outstripped the actual rental yield. As someone with an AMFI and NCFM background, I look at property the way a capital markets professional looks at equity. If a flat costs ₹3 crore but only earns ₹60,000 in monthly rent, your gross yield is just 2.4%. After tax and maintenance, you are earning less than a basic savings account.

When the speculation cools, those prices will stagnate. I have seen it happen in sectors that lack a genuine "infrastructure trigger." If there is no new road, no new corporate anchor, and no new expansion capacity, why should the price keep rising? The wealth erosion that looks like "safety" in an FD is similar to the stagnation that looks like "prime property." You must calculate the net return after inflation and tax, not just the headline price. If the numbers don't work on paper, they won't work in your bank account.

## How to distinguish honest advisory from "broker talk"

The difference between a broker and an advisor is structural. A broker earns a commission when you buy; their incentive is to close the deal today. A property consultant earns their reputation by ensuring you don't make a mistake that costs you for the next decade. I have often told my clients—HNI business owners and NRIs alike—that the most valuable advice I ever gave them was when I told them *not* to buy a specific project.

My background in capital markets (AMFI/NCFM) and as a developer allows me to see the "hidden mathematics" of a deal. I look at IRR (Internal Rate of Return), opportunity cost, and regulatory risk. Most advisors can't calculate your net return after tax and maintenance; they just show you a 5-year appreciation chart. But if that chart ignores the loading factor or the hidden costs of EDC and IDC, it is a work of fiction.

Real estate is complex. Regulatory bodies like GMADA and PUDA have specific rules that can change. Title disputes, succession issues, and encumbrances are real risks that require on-ground experience to navigate. Whether it is an 8-month plot recovery case or a 3-month property tax resolution, the work I do after the sale is what defines the advisory relationship.
