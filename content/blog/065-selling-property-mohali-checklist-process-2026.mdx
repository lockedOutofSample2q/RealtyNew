# Selling a Mohali Property: The Complete Checklist — NOC, Loan Closure, Tax, and What Sellers Miss

Selling a property in Mohali in 2026 requires a sequenced checklist that starts with title clarity and ends with capital gains compliance. To sell successfully, you need four critical clearances: a clean Encumbrance Certificate (EC), a No Objection Certificate (NOC) from the relevant authority (GMADA or Municipal Committee), a loan foreclosure letter if the property is mortgaged, and updated property tax records with a unique Property ID. While the buyer typically pays the 7% stamp duty and registration fees in Punjab, the seller is responsible for the 1:2% brokerage, legal drafting fees, and capital gains tax: which can be as high as 22.66% for NRIs. The process usually takes 45 to 60 days from the initial Agreement to Sale to the final registry at the sub-registrar's office. Missing even one document, like an updated mutation (intkal), can stall the transaction for months.

## What are the mandatory documents required to sell a property in Mohali?

In my years of navigating project approvals and closing transactions, I have seen many deals collapse at the last minute because the seller assumed their paperwork was complete. It is not enough to just have the original allotment letter or sale deed. You need a set of supporting documents that prove the property is liquid and free of liabilities.

The first thing a sophisticated buyer or a bank will ask for is the Fard or Jamabandi. This is the record of rights maintained by the revenue department. It tells the world who actually owns the land. If you are selling a plot in a GMADA sector, you need the Permission to Transfer (PTT) from the Greater Mohali Area Development Authority. This is non-negotiable. Without it, the sub-registrar will not record the transaction.

Next is the Encumbrance Certificate (EC). This document proves that the property is free from any legal dues or mortgages. If you have ever taken a loan against the property, the EC must show that the lien has been removed. I often tell my HNI clients: do not wait for a buyer to ask for these. Get them ready as soon as you decide to sell. It shows you are a serious seller and prevents unnecessary price negotiations based on "paperwork risk."

## How do you handle an outstanding home loan during a sale?

If your property has an active mortgage, the bank holds your original documents. You cannot sell what you do not technically "possess" on paper. The process here is specific. You must first request a Foreclosure Letter or a List of Documents (LOD) from your bank. This letter states exactly how much money is needed to close the loan as of a specific date.

In a typical Mohali transaction, the buyer’s payment is often used to close this loan. This requires a three-way coordination between you, the buyer, and the bank. The buyer pays the bank directly to clear the dues: the bank then releases the original documents and an NOC (No Objection Certificate) stating the loan is closed. Only after this can the property be registered in the buyer’s name. 

I have managed these closures for several clients where the timing was tight. If there is a delay in the bank releasing the papers, the registry gets postponed, and in a volatile market, that can be a problem. Always factor in 10 to 15 working days for the bank to return original documents after the payment is made.

## The Capital Gains Trap: What is your actual net realization after tax?

Most sellers calculate their profit by subtracting the purchase price from the sale price. As someone who is AMFI and NCFM certified, I look at the math differently. Your "real" profit is what stays in your pocket after the government takes its share.

Under current Indian tax laws, if you have held the property for more than 24 months, it qualifies for Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG). The tax rate is 20% with indexation benefits. Indexation is a tool that allows you to adjust the purchase price for inflation, which reduces your taxable profit. However, if you sell within 24 months, the profit is added to your income and taxed at your applicable slab: which for most HNIs in Mohali is 30% plus surcharge.

To save on this tax, you have options under Section 54 and 54F of the Income Tax Act. You can reinvest the capital gains into another residential property within two years or buy specific 54EC capital gains bonds. If you are a land seller reinvesting in Mohali, understanding these windows is critical. Missing a deadline can mean a massive tax liability that could have been avoided with a bit of planning. ## What hidden costs do Mohali sellers often forget to budget for?

Sellers often focus on the big number: the sale price. But there are several smaller leaks that add up. 

1. **Brokerage:** The standard in Mohali is 1% to 2% of the total transaction value. For a Rs 5 crore property, that is Rs 5 to 10 lakhs. 
2. **Legal Fees:** You will need a lawyer or a professional document writer to draft the Agreement to Sale and the Sale Deed. While the buyer pays for the stamp duty, the seller usually bears the cost of ensuring their own legal interests are protected in the drafting.
3. **Property Tax Clearance:** You cannot sell without a No Dues Certificate (NDC) from the Municipal Committee or GMADA. If you have unpaid property tax from three years ago, it will surface now with interest. I handled a case for a client named Sandeep where a property tax dispute took three months of persistent coordination to resolve because the seller had not separated the property IDs for different floors. You can find more on this in our [guide to property registration and paperwork](/blog/mohali-property-registration-process-guide-2026).
4. **Maintenance Dues:** Most luxury societies in Sector 82A or Aerocity will not give you a clearance certificate unless all maintenance bills and "sinking fund" contributions are paid up to date.

### Does the seller pay for stamp duty and registration in Punjab?

In Punjab, and specifically in Mohali, the convention is that the buyer pays for the stamp duty and registration charges. In 2026, this typically totals around 7% of the property value (6% stamp duty + 1% registration fee). However, as a seller, you must ensure that the circle rate is correctly applied. If you sell below the circle rate, the government will still charge tax based on the circle rate, and both you and the buyer could face scrutiny. You can check the latest [Mohali circle rates and registration costs](/blog/property-registration-mohali-2026-stamp-duty) to stay updated.

## Selling as an NRI: Why the 22.66% TDS is just the starting point

If you are an NRI selling property in Mohali, the rules change significantly. The buyer is legally required to deduct TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) at a rate of 22.66% on the *total sale value*, not just the profit. This is a huge chunk of capital to have locked up with the tax department.

To avoid this, you can apply for a Lower Deduction Certificate (LDC) from the Income Tax department. This certificate allows the buyer to deduct TDS only on the actual capital gains rather than the total sale price. Getting this certificate takes 45 to 60 days, so you must start the process early. Without it, you will be waiting until the next financial year to claim a refund for the excess tax deducted. ## The Timeline: How long does it actually take from 'Decision' to 'Registry'?

From the day you decide to sell to the day you walk out of the sub-registrar's office with the money, expect a timeline of 45 to 90 days.

*   **Week 1-2:** Documentation gathering and finding a buyer.
*   **Week 3:** Price negotiation and signing the 'Agreement to Sale' (usually with a 10% token amount).
*   **Week 4-6:** Buyer secures a home loan (if needed). You secure the loan closure NOC from your bank.
*   **Week 7:** Final payment and Registry.

The biggest delays usually happen in the "paperwork" phase. I have seen transactions stalled for eight months because of cancelled bookings that the seller was not even aware of. One such case required persistent follow-up to reinstate a plot that had been cancelled by a builder for non-payment by a previous owner. These are the things that a standard checklist misses but an experienced advisor catches.

## FAQ: Common Questions from Mohali Property Sellers

### Can I sell my property if the mutation (intkal) is not done?
Technically, you can sign an agreement, but the registry cannot be completed in most cases without the mutation being updated in the revenue records. The mutation is what officially records you as the owner in the government's eyes. If this is pending, resolve it before listing the property.

### Do I need an NOC from GMADA for every sale?
If your property is in a GMADA-developed sector or a licensed colony, you generally need a Permission to Transfer (PTT) or an NOC. This ensures that all government dues, EDC (External Development Charges), and IDC (Infrastructure Development Charges) have been paid by the developer or the previous owners.

### What happens if the buyer backs out after the Agreement to Sale?
Usually, the Agreement to Sale includes a forfeiture clause. If the buyer backs out without a valid legal reason, the seller has the right to forfeit the token amount (usually 10%). Conversely, if the seller backs out, they often have to pay double the token amount back to the buyer.
