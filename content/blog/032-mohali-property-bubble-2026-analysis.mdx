# Is Mohali Real Estate in a Bubble? An Honest Answer With Five Years of Data

The short answer is no—Mohali is not in a systemic property bubble in 2026. However, it is increasingly becoming a "market of two halves." While prices in established sectors have reached levels that make even seasoned investors blink, this growth is not fueled by the "hot air" of speculation. Instead, it is anchored by three massive, physical demand drivers: the rapid expansion of the IT City corridor, a historic surge in NRI capital seeking safe havens, and a lifestyle relocation trend where rural Punjab’s wealth is consolidating in the Tricity. 

When you look at the data from the last five years, you see a clear distinction between "appreciation zones" that follow the expansion principle and "stagnation zones" where prices have hit a ceiling. If you are buying in the old, land-locked sectors of Phase 7 or 3B2, you might feel the heat of a micro-bubble. But if you are looking at the new growth corridors linked to Bharatmala and the Airport Road extension, you are looking at a market that is still being fundamentally repriced based on utility and infrastructure.

## What defines a property bubble in the Mohali context?

As someone who has navigated this market as a developer, a consultant, and a financial markets professional with NCFM and AMFI certifications, I look at bubbles differently than most brokers. A bubble isn't just "high prices." A bubble is a disconnect between price and value. 

In Mohali, a bubble would mean prices are rising while houses sit empty and offices have no tenants. That is not what we are seeing. In our [complete Mohali real estate guide](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026), we highlight that occupancy rates in new luxury projects are at an all-time high. When a project delivers, families actually move in. When a new SCO is built on Airport Road, businesses fight for the frontage. 

The risk in Mohali isn't a total market crash; the risk is "price fixation" in areas that have stopped growing. If you buy a property where there is no more room for a new road, a new park, or a new commercial hub, you are buying at the peak of that micro-cycle. That is where the bubble-like behavior exists—in sectors that have exhausted their "expansion capacity."

## Why is demand in Mohali still outstripping supply?

I often tell my clients over chai that the demand we see today is the result of a "perfect storm" that started back in 2019. There are three pillars holding up these prices:

1.  **The NRI Surge:** The Punjabi diaspora in Canada, the UK, and Australia has pivoted. Burned by volatile equity markets and complex overseas tax laws, they are bringing capital back to the land they know. For an NRI, a ₹3 crore flat in Mohali is a trust-signal for their family’s future.
2.  **The IT City Reality:** This is no longer a "proposed" project. With major corporate campuses now operational and thousands of high-earning professionals moving in, the rental demand has created a floor for property prices. 
3.  **HNI Relocation:** We are seeing a massive shift where successful business owners from districts like Ludhiana, Jalandhar, and Sangrur are moving their primary residences to Mohali. They aren't just investing; they are buying quality of life, proximity to PGIMER, and better schools for their children.

This isn't speculative trading. This is a structural shift in where Punjab’s wealth wants to live. You can see this reflected in our [investment analysis data](/blog/mohali-real-estate-investment-analysis-2026), which shows that rental yields in these high-growth zones are actually keeping pace with capital appreciation—a rare sign in a supposedly "bubbly" market.

## Is the supply pipeline from GMADA and private developers enough?

One of the strongest arguments against a bubble is the "scarcity of licensed land." Having managed project approvals across GMADA, PUDA, and the Forest Department, I can tell you that bringing a project to market in Mohali is an exhausting process. The regulatory hurdles ensure that supply doesn't just "flood" the market overnight. From the developer side, I know that getting a Change of Land Use (CLU) or a layout approval can take anywhere from 12 to 24 months. 

While we see new sector launches and the expansion of Mullanpur (New Chandigarh), the "absorption rate"—the speed at which new inventory is sold—remains high. In many pre-launch phases I’ve handled, 60% of the inventory is often committed before the first brick is laid. This isn't because of marketing hype; it’s because the window to buy "GMADA-licensed" land is narrow and highly competitive. We are also seeing a shift where developers are no longer launching massive 500-acre townships all at once; they are launching in phases, which prevents the market from becoming oversupplied. This "phased supply" approach is a sign of a maturing market, not one that is about to burst. We've seen projects where the developer intentionally holds back inventory to ensure that infrastructure development keeps pace with sales.

## Which areas in Mohali are at risk of price stagnation?

This is where I have to be blunt. Not every plot in Mohali is a gold mine. There are "stagnation zones" where I tell my clients to be extremely cautious. These are typically the older, established markets like Phase 7, 3B2, and parts of Phase 10. 

These areas are iconic, yes. But they have no expansion capacity. The roads cannot get wider. The parking cannot get bigger. The [appreciation vs stagnation zones](/blog/mohali-property-appreciation-vs-stagnation-zones) principle teaches us that when a micro-market hits its infrastructure limit, the price stagnates. You might see a price of ₹1.5 lakh per yard on paper, but if there is no one willing to buy at ₹1.6 lakh next year because the traffic is unbearable, you are in a stagnation trap. This is where the "bubble" talk actually carries weight.

## Where is the genuine undervaluation in 2026?

If the old sectors are heated, where is the value? The data points toward the "Vision Corridors." As I’ve written before, the buyers who made the most money in Mohali were the ones who saw the Airport Road potential when it was just a blueprint. 

Today, that value lies in the corridors linked to the Bharatmala project and the industrial zones near Rajpura and Banur. These areas are currently undergoing a "repricing." They are moving from "agricultural/industrial value" to "administrative/urban value." When a 62-vigha parcel is purchased at ₹18.70 lakh per vigha and sees offers of ₹45 lakh post-registration, that isn't a bubble—it’s the market recognizing a change in land use and connectivity.

Look at Phase 7. Only 20% growth over 5 years. That is barely beating inflation and FD returns. Compare that to IT City or Sector 82A. The reason for this gap isn't speculation; it's utility. The new sectors are where the new companies are, where the new roads are, and where the new money is flowing. 

## What does the absorption rate data tell us about the 2026 market?

Data from the last four quarters shows that while the "volume" of transactions has leveled off compared to the post-pandemic frenzy, the "value" has stayed firm. This means we are moving from a "quantity" market to a "quality" market. Buyers are no longer buying anything and everything; they are becoming selective. They are looking at the developer's track record, the specific sector's connectivity, and the secondary market liquidity.

A "bubble" pops when there are no more buyers left at the high price. But in Mohali, every time a quality project is announced by a developer with a clean delivery track record, the demand is overwhelming. We see this daily in our [FAQ sessions with buyers](/blog/mohali-real-estate-faq-2026)—the concern isn't "will the price fall," but "will I get the unit I want." The absorption rate for luxury flats in the ₹3–5 crore bracket is particularly surprising; these units are selling faster than mid-segment housing in some corridors. This indicates that the wealth at the top end of the Punjabi economy is looking for high-quality assets to store value, further insulating the market from a speculative crash. We are also seeing high absorption in the commercial sector, especially for SCOs with high frontage on the Airport Road, where vacancy is almost non-existent in delivered projects.

The reality of Mohali in 2026 is that we are in a period of "disciplined growth." The low-quality, unapproved colonies are struggling, as they should. But the licensed, infrastructure-backed growth corridors are thriving because they serve a real purpose in the Punjabi economy. Don't let the fear of a "bubble" keep you on the sidelines, but don't let the "hype" lead you into a land-locked stagnation zone either.
