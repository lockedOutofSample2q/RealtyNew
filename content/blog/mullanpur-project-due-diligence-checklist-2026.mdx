# Mullanpur Project Due Diligence: 6 Specific Things to Verify Before Any New Chandigarh Booking

Is your dream home in Mullanpur actually built on a solid legal foundation, or are you buying into a marketing vision that the infrastructure cannot yet support? To perform proper due diligence in Mullanpur (New Chandigarh) in 2026, you must verify six specific factors: the Change of Land Use (CLU) status for the specific land pocket, the permanent PSPCL load sanction for power, the source and reliability of the water supply, the physical status of connectivity roads (PR-4/PR-7), strict compliance with the Master Plan 2031 (Red/Green/Orange zones), and the Occupation Certificate (OC) status of the developer’s earlier phases. Failing to verify these can lead to "possession" without power, "luxury" living dependent on water tankers, or resale-destroying title issues. In a market where 15-20% of inventory is often marketed before all regulatory hurdles are cleared, these checks are the difference between a safe asset and a decade-long dispute.

## Why does Mullanpur require a different due diligence framework than the rest of Mohali?

Mullanpur, or New Chandigarh as it is popularly branded, is the most organized urban expansion in the Tricity. However, having managed project approvals across PUDA, PSPCL, the Municipal Committee, the Forest Department, and Industrial Departments, I can tell you that "organized" does not mean "complete." In my years of navigating these corridors, I’ve seen that Mullanpur's challenges are unique because it is a "virgin territory" where infrastructure is being laid down alongside the buildings.

When you buy in an established sector like 3B2 or Sector 70, the "Software"—the electricity, water, and roads—is already there. In Mullanpur, you are often buying the "Hardware" (the building) while the Software is still in the cloud. This makes due diligence a matter of verifying government commitments, not just developer promises.

## 1. Is the Change of Land Use (CLU) actually approved for your specific pocket?

The Change of Land Use (CLU) is the "Birth Certificate" of any real estate project in Punjab. Mullanpur was almost entirely agricultural land. For a developer to build even a single villa, they must obtain a CLU from the Department of Town and Country Planning.

I have handled transactions where a developer had a CLU for "Phase 1" and "Phase 2," but started booking "Phase 3" on land that was still technically agricultural. If the government decides to put a 200-ft road through that agricultural patch, your "booked" plot vanishes. From the developer side, I know that the pressure to generate cash flow often leads to "pre-launch" bookings on land where the CLU is still under query.

**Action Item:** Ask for the official CLU certificate. Check the survey numbers (Khasra numbers) on the document. If they don't match your allotment letter exactly, you are not buying a property; you are buying a promise. ## 2. Does the project have a permanent PSPCL load sanction to avoid the "generator trap"?

This is perhaps the biggest "unspoken" risk in New Chandigarh in 2026. I have seen clients move into luxury flats worth ₹2 Crore only to find that their air conditioners won't start because the project is running on a temporary 100 kVA transformer intended for construction.

PSPCL (Punjab State Power Corporation Limited) requires developers to pay heavy System Loading Charges and provide Bank Guarantees before they sanction a permanent load for a residential colony. In several projects I've audited, developers have delayed these payments to preserve cash. The result? Frequent power cuts, voltage fluctuations that kill your appliances, and exorbitant "backup" charges.

**Expertise Signal:** During my time managing project documentation, I learned that a "Load Sanction Letter" is the only document that matters. If the developer says "we have applied," it means you will be living on generators. Check our [mohali real estate faq 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-faq-2026) for a breakdown of typical electricity costs in these scenarios.

## 3. Is there a GMADA water connection or are you dependent on tankers?

Water is the lifeblood of any project, yet it is often an afterthought for buyers. In Mullanpur, the GMADA main line is the gold standard. However, many "outer" sectors are not yet connected. 

In one transaction I handled, the buyer was promised "24/7 pressurized water supply." On the day of possession, we discovered the "pressurized supply" was coming from a tractor-mounted pump because the internal tanks weren't connected to the GMADA grid. If the project relies on borewells, you must check for the Central Ground Water Authority (CGWA) NOC. Without it, the borewell is illegal and can be sealed at any time.

Always walk to the site's periphery and ask to see the GMADA water inlet point. If it doesn't exist, you are buying a property dependent on the local tanker lobby. ## 4. Is the connectivity road status real or just a promised "island project"?

Brochures in Mullanpur always show the project connected directly to the PR-4 or PR-7. The reality on the ground is often an "Island Project"—a beautiful complex surrounded by mud tracks because the 60-ft approach road passes through a village pocket that hasn't been acquired yet.

I've seen this play out in the 8-month plot recovery case study I handled. A client bought a plot that was supposedly on a 100-ft road. It took eight months of persistent follow-up to realize the road was stuck in a "status quo" order from the High Court. Never trust a "proposed" road. If the asphalt isn't laid and the streetlights aren't up, that road doesn't exist for your bank or your car.

## 5. How does Master Plan 2031 compliance affect your property's future?

The GMADA Master Plan 2031 is the most powerful document in Mohali real estate. It tells you what will happen to the land *next* to your project. 

I’ve had HNIs come to me wanting to buy "forest-facing" villas. My first question is always: "Is it a Green Zone or a Forest Department Buffer?" If it's a Forest Department Buffer, you might not get permission to build even a garden shed within 100 meters of the boundary. Having faced a Forest Department approval cancellation on a project personally, I can tell you that they are the most unpredictable regulatory body in Punjab.

- **Red Zone:** Safe for residential.
- **Orange Zone:** Institutional. Your quiet morning could be replaced by school bells or hospital sirens.
- **Green Zone:** Beautiful but restrictive. No infrastructure, no security, no streetlights.

Always cross-reference the developer’s layout with the official zoning map. If there's a discrepancy, the Master Plan always wins in court. See our [mohali developer project due diligence checklist](/blog/mohali-developer-project-due-diligence-checklist) for a deeper dive into zoning risks.

## 6. Does the developer have an Occupation Certificate (OC) on earlier phases?

If a developer tells you they are handing over Phase 2, ask for the Occupation Certificate (OC) of Phase 1. An OC is not a "completion letter" from the developer; it is an official document from GMADA or the Municipal Committee stating the building is safe, follows the approved maps, and has all utility clearances.

In Mullanpur, many developers give "possession for interiors" to stop the RERA delay penalty clock. Buyers happily take the keys, only to realize a year later they can't get a home loan top-up or sell the property because there is no OC. As someone who has closed 180+ transactions, I can tell you that a building without an OC is a "frozen asset." It has value on paper, but no liquidity.

Check if the developer’s previous projects in Mohali have received OCs. If they haven't, why would this project be any different?
