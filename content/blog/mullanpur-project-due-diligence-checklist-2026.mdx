## 1. CLU Status and License to Develop: The Foundation of Legality

The first and most critical step in the mullanpur project due diligence checklist 2026 is verifying the Change of Land Use (CLU). In Punjab, and specifically within the jurisdiction of the Greater Mohali Area Development Authority (GMADA), no developer can legally market a project without a valid CLU and a subsequent License to Develop.

Investors should request the original CLU certificate issued by the Department of Town and Country Planning, Punjab. This document confirms that the land, which was likely agricultural, has been legally converted for residential or commercial use. Furthermore, ensure the license is still valid and has not expired. Reports in the Economic Times have highlighted cases where developers continued bookings despite having lapsed licenses, leading to RERA interventions and heavy penalties.



## 2. PSPCL Load Sanction: The Permanent Electricity Problem

One of the most overlooked aspects of project verification in New Chandigarh is the electricity connection. Many developers operate on temporary construction power long after residents have moved in. This results in frequent outages, voltage fluctuations, and high electricity bills for the residents who are essentially paying commercial rates for domestic use.

Verify the PSPCL (Punjab State Power Corporation Limited) load sanction. A project must have a sanctioned bulk load capacity that matches the total number of units and planned amenities. Ask for the document showing the "Feasibility Clearance" for the required load. Without this, the developer cannot provide a permanent transformer or individual meters to buyers. On our YouTube channel @Amritrealty, we have frequently discussed how lack of power infrastructure is the primary reason why Occupation Certificates are denied by GMADA.

## 3. Connectivity Road Status: Master Plan vs. Ground Reality

The New Chandigarh Master Plan 2031 outlines a grid of 200-feet and 100-feet wide roads (such as the PR-4 and PR-7 extensions). However, the existence of a road on a map does not guarantee its existence on the ground. Land acquisition for these roads is often a staggered process.

A project might claim to be "on the 200-feet wide road," but if the government has not yet taken possession of the specific patches of land connecting that project to the main highway, your accessibility remains restricted. Use official GMADA portals to check the status of road possession in sectors like Omaxe New Chandigarh, Eco City 1, and Eco City 2. Physical site visits are mandatory to confirm that the approach road is not just a narrow village track but the planned arterial road.

## 4. Master Plan 2031 Compliance and Zoning Constraints

New Chandigarh is strictly governed by the Mullanpur Master Plan 2031. This plan defines residential zones, green belts, and low-density areas. A common trap for investors is "Pre-Launch" offers in areas that are actually zoned as green belts or agricultural zones in the master plan.

The mullanpur project due diligence checklist 2026 must include a cross-check of the project's layout plan against the master plan. Specifically:
- **Green Belt Proximity:** Ensure the project does not encroach on protected forest land near the Shivaliks.
- **Zoning:** Is the project located in a High-Density or Low-Density zone? This impacts the future population density and the load on civic amenities.
- **Catchment Areas:** Verify if the land is near the Patiala Ki Rao or other seasonal rivulets. Construction in natural drainage paths is not only ecologically damaging but legally precarious, as seen in recent Dainik Bhaskar reports regarding environmental clearances in the peripheral areas of Chandigarh.



## 5. Occupation Certificate (OC) on Earlier Phases

If you are buying from a developer who has already completed phases in Mullanpur, their past performance is your best data point. An Occupation Certificate (OC) is issued by GMADA only when the building is constructed according to the approved building plan and all basic services (water, sewerage, electricity) are operational.

If a developer has delivered 500 flats in Phase 1 but only has an OC for 100 of them, it indicates a significant regulatory or construction hurdle. Buying in Phase 2 of such a developer is high risk. Furthermore, check the registry status. If the developer has not facilitated the "Sale Deed" or "Registry" for previous buyers, it often means the EDC (External Development Charges) or LIC (License Fee) has not been cleared with the government.

## 6. The Six Mullanpur-Specific Questions to Ask

Beyond the standard legalities, New Chandigarh requires specific local intelligence. Before signing any booking form, ask the developer's sales team these six questions:

1.  **Bulk Water Supply:** Does the project have a committed bulk water supply from the Kajauli Water Works via GMADA, or is it solely dependent on borewells? (Groundwater levels in Mullanpur are a concern).
2.  **Sewerage Treatment:** Is the project connected to the GMADA main sewer line, or does it rely on an independent STP? If independent, who manages the maintenance?
3.  **EDC Status:** Is there a "No Dues Certificate" from GMADA regarding External Development Charges? Unpaid EDC is the most common reason for withholding OCs.
4.  **RERA Escrow Account:** Can I have the details of the RERA-designated escrow account where my 70% of payment will be deposited?
5.  **Forest Department Clearance:** Since Mullanpur is close to the Shivalik foothills, does the project require (and possess) a No Objection Certificate (NOC) from the Forest Department under the PLPA (Punjab Land Preservation Act)?
6.  **PGI Proximity Impact:** How does the project's proximity to PGI and MediCity affect its traffic management plan? (Crucial for commercial or high-rise residential projects).



## Financial Due Diligence: APF and RERA Verification

While technical and legal checks are paramount, financial due diligence cannot be ignored. Ensure the project has an APF (Approved Project Finance) code from leading banks like HDFC, ICICI, or SBI. Banks conduct their own legal vetting before approving home loans for a project. If major banks are refusing to fund a project in Mullanpur, it is a massive red flag.

Always cross-reference the project details on the RERA Punjab website (https://rera.punjab.gov.in). The information uploaded by the developer on RERA—including the quarterly progress reports, the list of approvals, and the proposed completion date—is legally binding. Discrepancies between the RERA filing and the marketing brochure are grounds for legal action and should be a deal-breaker for any prudent investor.
