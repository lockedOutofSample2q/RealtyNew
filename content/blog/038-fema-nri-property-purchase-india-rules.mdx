# FEMA Rules for NRI Property Purchase in India: What Actually Applies to Mohali Buyers

NRIs (Non-Resident Indians) and OCIs (Overseas Citizens of India) are permitted to purchase residential and commercial property in India under FEMA Section 6(3)(i) without seeking prior approval from the Reserve Bank of India (RBI). You are entitled to own as many residential or commercial units as your portfolio requires. However, FEMA strictly prohibits NRIs from purchasing agricultural land, farmhouses, or plantation property unless specific permission is granted by the RBI—a process that is rarely successful for pure investment. All payments must flow through regulated Indian banking channels via NRE, NRO, or FCNR accounts. Direct foreign transfers to builders or any form of cash transaction violate FEMA regulations and will lead to compounding penalties or severe restrictions when you attempt to repatriate your sale proceeds in the future.

## What types of property can an NRI legally buy in India?

The most common question I get from the Punjabi diaspora in the UK, Canada, and Australia is whether they need a "clearance" to buy in Mohali. The short answer is no, provided you are buying the right category of asset. 

Under the Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA), an NRI or OCI can purchase any number of residential houses or commercial properties. Whether you are looking at a luxury 4BHK flat in Sector 82A, a GMADA allotted plot, or a commercial SCO on Airport Road, the law treats you on par with resident Indians in terms of the right to purchase.

There is no limit on the quantity of properties you can hold. I have handled portfolios for NRIs who own multiple floors in New Chandigarh and commercial blocks in Aerocity simultaneously. The restriction only kicks in when the nature of the land changes. If the property is categorized as "Residential" or "Commercial" in the [Mohali real estate guide 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026), you are on safe ground.

It is important to distinguish between "Residential" and "Agricultural" at the intent stage. Some developers market "farmstay" or "weekend home" projects. If these projects are built on land that is still agricultural in the revenue records (Girdawari), an NRI cannot legally buy them. Always verify the Change of Land Use (CLU) status through the [developer due diligence checklist](/blog/mohali-developer-project-due-diligence-checklist) before committing any funds.

## Can an NRI buy agricultural land or a farmhouse in Mohali?

This is where many NRIs get into trouble. In Punjab, the emotional pull of "Zameen" (land) is strong. Often, a relative might suggest buying a few acres of agricultural land on the outskirts of Mohali or a "farmhouse plot" that isn't part of a licensed colony.

**FEMA is very clear: NRIs and OCIs cannot buy agricultural land, farmhouses, or plantation property.**

If you attempt to buy agricultural land, the registry might technically go through at the local Tehsildar's office because they don't always verify residency status, but the transaction will be a violation of FEMA. When you later try to sell that land or move the money back to your home country, the lack of RBI permission will block the entire process. 

The only exception is inheritance. If you inherit agricultural land from a resident Indian, you can legally hold it. However, if you are a citizen of Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Afghanistan, China, Iran, Nepal, or Bhutan, even inheritance requires RBI approval. For the majority of the Mohali-focused diaspora in the West, inheritance is the only legal route to owning agricultural land in Punjab.

If you are looking for the security of land, I always advise my NRI clients to stick to [GMADA plots or licensed colony plots](/blog/gmada-property-nri-allotment-process) where the land use is already converted to residential. These properties offer the same "land ownership" feel while being 100% FEMA-compliant.

## The correct payment route: NRE vs NRO vs FCNR accounts

Understanding the plumbing of your transaction is more important than the property itself. I've seen NRIs choose the right property but use the wrong bank account, leading to years of regulatory headaches.

1.  **NRE (Non-Resident External) Account:** Use this if you want full repatriability. If you pay for a Mohali flat using funds from your NRE account, you can take the principal amount back to your country of residence whenever you sell, without any $1 million cap on the principal. The funds in this account must originate from outside India.
2.  **NRO (Non-Resident Ordinary) Account:** This is for "Indian" income or funds. If you have rental income from other properties in India, dividends, or are using old savings from when you were a resident, this money stays in the NRO account. While you can use this to buy property, the repatriation of these funds (both principal and profit) is capped at USD 1 million per financial year.
3.  **FCNR (Foreign Currency Non-Resident) Account:** Similar to NRE, these are funds held in foreign currency and are fully repatriable. This is often used for large, one-time investments where you don't want to take an immediate hit on currency conversion.

**What you must never do:** Never send a wire transfer directly from your bank in Toronto or London to a developer's account in India. The developer might accept it, but you will not have the Foreign Inward Remittance Certificate (FIRC) in your name linked to the property purchase. Without this, proving the source of funds to the RBI during a future sale is nearly impossible.

## Can an NRI pay from a foreign bank account directly to the builder?

As mentioned above, the answer is a firm no. Even if a builder tells you "it's fine, we have an Escrow," you must resist. The payment must land in your own NRI account in India first, and then be issued to the builder or seller.

This is part of the [NRI property guide Mohali 2026](/blog/nri-property-guide-mohali-2026) for a reason. In my experience managing project approvals and developer sales, I’ve seen cases where the lack of a clear banking trail delayed the issuance of a No Objection Certificate (NOC) at the time of resale. 

If you are buying from a private seller (resale), the payment should go from your NRE/NRO account to the seller’s Indian bank account. Cash "under the table" is not just illegal; for an NRI, it is a catastrophic mistake. Since every rupee you take out of India must be accounted for, any "black" money paid today is money you can never legally repatriate tomorrow. Even if you "save" 6% on stamp duty by under-reporting the value, you lose far more in future repatriation ability and capital gains tax complications.

## FEMA rules for joint ownership with a resident Indian

Many NRIs prefer to buy property jointly with a brother, sister, or parent who lives in Mohali. This is permitted under FEMA, but there are specific conditions:
1.  **The Nature of the Asset:** If the property is residential or commercial, joint ownership is simple. If the property is agricultural, an NRI cannot be a joint owner even if the other owner is a resident.
2.  **The Resident Holder:** The joint holder (resident Indian) should be a genuine co-owner. In some cases, NRIs add a resident relative just for "convenience," but the tax authorities look at who actually paid for the property.
3.  **Payment Proportions:** The payment must be proportionate to the ownership share, or if the NRI is paying the full amount, it must come from the NRI's accounts. 

A common trap is an NRI buying property jointly with a resident spouse. If the spouse is a resident Indian, they can hold any type of property, including agricultural land. But if the NRI’s name is on the deed of an agricultural property, it becomes a FEMA violation. Always keep the asset categories separate if you are blending NRI and resident ownership.

## Inheriting Property: What FEMA Says About Your Ancestral Home

Many NRIs in the UK and Canada eventually inherit their ancestral homes or land in Mohali and surrounding villages. FEMA allows you to inherit any type of property—including agricultural land—from a person who was a resident of India. 

However, selling that inherited land is where the rules get specific. You can only sell inherited agricultural land to a resident Indian. You cannot sell it to another NRI. When you sell such property, the proceeds must land in your NRO account. You can then repatriate up to $1 million per financial year after paying the [NRI tax on property sale](/blog/nri-tax-property-sale-mohali-tds-capital-gains).

## Repatriation of sale proceeds: How much can you take back?

The end-goal of any investment is the ability to use the money. FEMA rules on repatriation are actually quite fair if you follow the process:

*   **Residential Property:** You can repatriate the sale proceeds of up to two residential properties without any special permission. For the third property onwards, you may need RBI approval if you intend to take out more than the $1 million annual limit.
*   **The $1 Million Rule:** For properties bought using NRO funds (or inherited properties), you can repatriate up to USD 1 million per financial year. This includes the principal and the capital gains, provided all taxes have been paid and Form 15CA and 15CB are filed by a Chartered Accountant.
*   **Principal vs. Profit:** If the property was bought from NRE funds, the principal is usually fully repatriable. The profit (capital gains) always falls under the $1 million per year window.
*   **Repatriation Timeline:** This isn't an "instant" transfer. You need a completion certificate of the sale, tax clearance, and a certificate from a CA. Only then will your bank process the outward remittance.

## What happens if you don't follow FEMA rules during the purchase?

If you violate FEMA—for instance, by buying agricultural land or using a non-compliant payment route—the penalties are significant. The RBI can impose a penalty of up to thrice the sum involved in the violation or Rs 2 lakh if the amount is not quantifiable. Furthermore, the violation continues every day it isn't rectified, leading to additional daily penalties.

More practically, you get "stuck." I remember a case involving a [plot recovery that took 8 months](/blog/mohali-plot-cancelled-booking-recovery-process). While that was a builder-buyer dispute, I’ve seen similar timelines when NRIs try to fix "illegal" purchases made years ago. By the time they realize the mistake, the property has appreciated, and the tax and penalty liabilities are massive. The property becomes unsellable to any informed buyer because their lawyer will flag the FEMA violation during the [due diligence process](/blog/mohali-property-registration-process-guide-2026).

Always ensure your purchase is compliant from Day 1. It is much cheaper to pay a consultant for a due diligence report now than to pay a lawyer to settle a FEMA violation five years later.

One reason I recommend Mohali to NRIs is the transparency of the [GMADA framework](/blog/gmada-mohali-explained-guide). When you buy a GMADA-allotted plot or a flat in a GMADA-approved project, the title is usually clear, and the land use is already residential. The proximity to the Chandigarh International Airport and the development of the IT corridor means that these compliant assets have high liquidity. In an emergency, a FEMA-compliant property in a prime Mohali sector can be sold and the funds moved out much faster than a disputed piece of land in a rural belt.

## Documentation checklist for FEMA compliance in Mohali

To ensure your purchase is "FEMA-proof," keep the following documents in a digital vault:
1.  **Passport and OCI Card copies:** Proof of your status at the time of purchase.
2.  **FIRC (Foreign Inward Remittance Certificate):** Issued by your Indian bank for every foreign transfer.
3.  **NRE/NRO Bank Statements:** Showing the debit for the property payment.
4.  **Registered Sale Deed:** Showing the full value of the transaction (no under-valuation).
5.  **Allotment Letter/Agreement to Sell:** To prove the timeline of the transaction.
6.  **TDS Certificates:** Proof that tax was deducted and deposited if you are buying from another NRI.

Whether you are buying in a new sector in Mullanpur or an established block in Sector 70, the rules of the game remain the same. The Indian government wants your investment, but they want it through the front door.
