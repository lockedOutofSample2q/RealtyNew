# How to Get a Separate Electricity Connection for Your Floor in a Multi-Storey Mohali Property

To get a separate electricity connection for an individual floor in Mohali, you must apply to the Punjab State Power Corporation Limited (PSPCL) under the 'Single Point' or 'Individual Metering' category, depending on your property type. The process requires submitting a registered sale deed or allotment letter to prove ownership, an Aadhar card, and a load sanction request—typically 5kW for a standard residential floor. You must pay the Security Consumption and Security Meter (SC/SM) fees, after which PSPCL conducts a site inspection to verify the wiring and load feasibility. Having a separate meter is critical for obtaining a No Dues Certificate (NDC) and ensuring a smooth resale, as it decouples your liability from other floor owners in the building.

## Why is a separate meter essential for Mohali floor owners?

When you buy an independent floor in sectors like 82A, 76, or 77, the building often starts with a single high-load connection used by the developer during construction. If you don't transition to a separate meter in your own name, you remain tethered to a collective bill. This is not just an administrative nuisance; it is a significant financial risk.

In my years of navigating project approvals and handling [Mohali property registration](/blog/mohali-property-registration-process-guide-2026), I have seen many buyers ignore this until they decide to sell. At the time of resale, the buyer's bank will demand an NDC (No Dues Certificate) from the Municipal Committee. If your electricity bill is shared and there is a dispute on any floor, your NDC will be blocked. A separate meter ensures that your "account" with the state is clean, independent, and ready for transfer.

## What documents do you need for a new PSPCL connection?

![A checklist of required documents for a new PSPCL connection in Mohali](https://placehold.co/600x400/png?text=PSPCL+Documents+Checklist)

The paperwork for PSPCL is precise. They do not accept WhatsApp photos or blurred copies; they need verified documentation. Based on my experience coordinating with PSPCL sub-divisions in Mohali, here is what you must have ready:

1.  **Proof of Ownership:** A certified copy of your Registered Sale Deed or the Allotment Letter from GMADA/PUDA.
2.  **Identity Proof:** Aadhar Card is the standard. Ensure the name matches the Sale Deed exactly.
3.  **No Objection Certificate (NOC):** If the building is still under the developer's name, you need an NOC from the developer.
4.  **Test Report:** A certificate from a licensed electrical contractor certifying that the internal wiring of your floor is safe and meets the requested load (e.g., 5kW).
5.  **Photograph:** Recent passport-size photograph of the applicant.

## What is the step-by-step process for application?

![The step-by-step process of applying for a new electricity connection through the PSPCL portal](https://placehold.co/600x400/png?text=PSPCL+Application+Steps)

The process has moved significantly online, but on-ground follow-up remains a reality of the Punjab real estate landscape. 

*   **Step 1: Online Registration:** Visit the PSPCL Consumer Portal and register using your mobile number. Select 'New Connection'.
*   **Step 2: Load Selection:** For most 3BHK floors in Mohali, a 5kW load is sufficient. If you have multiple heavy appliances (4+ ACs), consider 7kW.
*   **Step 3: Fee Payment:** Once the application is submitted, you will receive a demand note for security deposits. These fees are regulated and based on the kilowatt load requested.
*   **Step 4: Field Inspection:** A Junior Engineer (JE) from the local PSPCL office will visit the site. They check if the meter box location is accessible and if the load requested matches the wiring.
*   **Step 5: Meter Installation:** Post-approval, the meter is usually installed within 7-15 days.

## Sub-meter vs. Direct PSPCL Connection: A Financial Comparison

Many investors ask me: "Why spend ₹25,000–35,000 on a new connection when I can just install a ₹2,000 sub-meter?"

The answer lies in the slab-based billing system of Punjab. PSPCL uses a progressive tariff where the cost per unit increases as your consumption crosses specific thresholds (e.g., 0-100 units, 100-300 units, and so on). 

When three floors share one main meter, the total consumption often hits the highest tariff slab (₹8+ per unit) within the first 10 days of the month. Even if you use a sub-meter to "split" the bill, you are likely paying for every single unit at the highest possible rate. In contrast, with a separate connection, each floor gets its own fresh start at the lowest slab. Over a 12-month period, the saving on electricity bills often exceeds the initial cost of the connection. For an NRI landlord managing a property remotely, this also eliminates the monthly "calculation friction" with tenants.

## The Liaisoning Reality: What Happens at the PSPCL Sub-Division?

Having managed these approvals across various Punjab government regulatory bodies—including PUDA, the Forest Department, and the Municipal Committee—I can tell you that the PSPCL office in Mohali operates on a "document-first" principle. 

Liaisoning isn't about shortcuts; it's about anticipation. For example, the Junior Engineer (JE) isn't just looking at your meter; they are looking at the 'Distribution Transformer' (DT) capacity in your area. If the transformer is overloaded, your connection might be delayed until a capacity upgrade is approved. This is why I always check the local infrastructure status before a client commits to a high-load requirement (like 10kW for a home office).

When we represent a client at the sub-division office, we don't just file the application. We ensure the 'Service Connection Order' (SCO) is issued promptly. If the SCO sits on a desk for two weeks, your meter doesn't move. We track the file from the Revenue Superintendent to the JE to the Lineman. This persistence is what reduced the timeline for clients like Sandeep from the standard 30-45 days to just under 15 days.

## How much load should you actually sanction? The Load Math.

One common mistake I see in [Mohali real estate FAQ](/blog/mohali-real-estate-faq-2026) sessions is under-sanctioning load to save on security fees. If you sanction 2kW but run 3 ACs, the meter will trip, and you risk a penalty for "unauthorized load extension" during PSPCL raids.

Here is a simplified math for a standard Mohali floor:
*   **Air Conditioner (1.5 Ton):** ~1.5 to 2.0 kW per unit.
*   **Geyser:** ~2.0 kW.
*   **Refrigerator/Microwave/Misc:** ~1.0 kW.
*   **Total for 2 ACs + Geyser:** ~6.0 kW at peak.

Since all appliances rarely run at the exact same moment, a 5kW sanction is generally the "sweet spot" for 2BHK and 3BHK floors. However, if you are building a luxury floor with a modular kitchen (heavy chimney/oven) and central heating, you should not settle for less than 7.5kW.

As someone who has managed project approvals across PUDA and the Municipal Committee, I always advise clients to factor in their peak summer usage. It is cheaper to sanction the correct load at the start than to apply for a load enhancement later.

## Technical Specifications: The Meter Box and Wire Gauge

PSPCL Mohali has strict guidelines on where the meter is placed. It must be at the "entry point" of the property. For multi-floor buildings, there should be a centralized 'Meter Cabinet' on the ground floor. 

I’ve seen possession delays in projects simply because the developer used 4sq mm wire instead of the required 6sq mm or 10sq mm for the main incoming line. When the JE visits for inspection and finds the wire gauge insufficient for the sanctioned load, the file is returned with a "Technical Deficiency" note. As a buyer, always verify the 'Electrical Map' of your floor against the PSPCL requirements before the walls are plastered. It saves you from the nightmare of tearing down a finished wall to upgrade a conduit.

## What are the common reasons for PSPCL application rejection?

Rejections usually happen due to technicalities, not lack of merit. Having navigated these approvals personally, I've seen three recurring issues:

1.  **Name Mismatch:** If the name on your Aadhar doesn't match the Sale Deed (e.g., "Sandeep Singh" vs "S. Singh"), the JE will flag it.
2.  **Unpaid Dues on Land:** If the developer or the previous owner has unpaid bills on the "parent" connection for that plot, PSPCL will refuse new connections until the old dues are cleared.
3.  **Inaccessible Meter Box:** In Mohali, PSPCL requires the meter to be outside the main gate or in a common area where the meter reader can access it without entering the house. If your developer built the meter cabinet inside a locked garage, you will face delays.

## Does a separate meter help with Municipal Committee (MC) Tax?

Yes, indirectly. While electricity and property tax are handled by different bodies (PSPCL and MC), they both rely on independent identification of the unit. When you apply for a separate Property ID for tax purposes, having an independent electricity bill in your name serves as a strong evidence of possession and independent utility usage.
