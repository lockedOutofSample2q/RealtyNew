# Mohali Property Price History 2019–2026: Sector-Wise Appreciation Data and What Drove It

Between 2019 and 2026, Mohali real estate transformed from a Chandigarh satellite into a primary investment destination, with capital appreciation in key corridors exceeding 200%. This growth was primarily driven by the completion of the 200-foot Airport Road, the expansion of the IT City corridor, and a structural shift in NRI investment toward Punjab’s most organized urban framework. While established sectors like 82A and Aerocity saw prices double every three to four years, the appreciation was not uniform; it followed a clear "Expansion Principle" where sectors with future connectivity and infrastructure capacity outpaced stagnant, land-locked areas.

If you are looking at Mohali property today, understanding this trajectory is the only way to distinguish between a sector that has already peaked and one that is still on its way up. In my experience personally closing over 180 transactions, I have seen that the buyers who made the most money were not the ones who followed the crowd, but those who bought where the infrastructure was "always coming" but not yet priced in.

## What Drove the Mohali Price Surge?

To understand the numbers, you have to understand the triggers. Real estate does not move in a vacuum. In Mohali, the price history of the last seven years is a story of connectivity.

![Aerial view of the 200-foot Airport Road in Mohali showing commercial development and traffic flow](airport-road-mohali.jpg)

### The Airport Road Catalyst (JLPL and Beyond)
The single biggest driver was the 200-foot Airport Road. Before 2019, large stretches of this corridor were aspirational. Today, they are the commercial heartbeat of the city. I remember when commercial units in and around Sector 82 (JLPL area) were available at ₹3–4 crore. At the time, many hesitated, thinking it was "too far" from Chandigarh.

By the mid-point of this cycle, I seated a party in one of these units at ₹7 crore when the market was still catching up. Today, those same units trade between ₹12–16 crore. The infrastructure — the airport expansion, the IT corridor, and the Bharatmala connectivity — was always coming. The buyers who made money were the ones who saw the vision before the asphalt was even dry.

### The IT City and Knowledge Park Expansion
The shift of corporate offices from Chandigarh’s Sector 17 and IT Park to Mohali’s Sector 101 and IT City created a captive rental demand that Mohali had never seen before. This brought in the HNI and CXO buyer profiles — professionals who wanted luxury apartments near their workplace. This demand pushed residential PSF (per square foot) rates in Sector 82A from ₹4,500 in 2019 to over ₹13,500 in 2026.

## Deep Dive: Why Some Sectors Stagnated While Others Tripled

As an advisor with a background in capital markets (AMFI and NCFM certified), I look at real estate returns through the lens of opportunity cost. One of the most common mistakes I see buyers make is assuming that "location always wins." In Mohali, that hasn't been true. 

### The Expansion Principle in Action
I call it the **Expansion Principle**. Areas where expansion is possible appreciate. Areas where expansion is blocked stagnate. 

Look at the old established commercial corridors like Phase 7 or the 3B2 markets. These were the "it" places ten years ago. Today, prices there have seen "fixation." Rental values have actually dipped in real terms when adjusted for inflation. Why? Because everything that was ever going to come to those sectors has already arrived. There is no more land for new parking, no more space for new office blocks, and no more "newness" to attract the next generation of brands.

Compare that to the Airport Road or the new [Sector 82A](/blog/sector-82a-mohali-property-price-2026) projects. Here, new companies, new retail brands, and new infrastructure projects are arriving every quarter. The expansion creates a "pull" for capital. If you move without vision — as we say, *"Je aapaan vision ton bina challaange taan aapaan mehngi cheez khareeddaange"* — you will end up buying in a stagnated zone at a peak price.

### Sector 76 and 77: The GMADA Success Story
Sector 76 and 77 are excellent examples of how government-led infrastructure completion can trigger a price correction. In 2019, these sectors were often viewed as "neglected" compared to the glitz of Aerocity. However, as GMADA (Greater Mohali Area Development Authority) completed civil services, sewerage, and road networks, the gap closed rapidly. The PSF jumped from ₹3,500 to over ₹10,500. This 200% appreciation was driven by pure utility — people actually being able to build homes and live there.

### Mullanpur (New Chandigarh): The 2031 Vision
New Chandigarh’s trajectory has been unique. It marketed the "lifestyle" before the infrastructure. Between 2019 and 2021, prices moved on hype. But between 2023 and 2026, the movement has been based on the [New Chandigarh Master Plan 2031](/blog/new-chandigarh-master-plan-2031-property). The zoning of red, green, and orange belts has given investors clarity. Those who bought near the Medi-City or the upcoming VR Punjab extension saw their investments cross the ₹9,500 PSF mark.

## The NRI Factor: Shifting the Ceiling
The price history of Mohali is also the history of NRI trust. Between 2021 and 2024, there was a massive influx of capital from the Punjabi diaspora in Canada, the UK, and Australia. 

Having navigated the [NRI property guide](/blog/nri-property-guide-mohali-2026) requirements personally, I've seen how their preference for "branded" developers pushed prices in the luxury segment. They weren't just buying four walls; they were buying the security of RERA-approved projects and the transparency of a developer with a solid [delivery track record](/blog/mohali-developer-delivery-track-record-check). This demand shifted the price ceiling for Mohali from ₹1.5 crore to ₹4 crore+ for premium apartments.

For an NRI buyer, the price history of the last few years isn't just about appreciation; it's about the "peace of mind premium." They were willing to pay ₹10,000 PSF in a branded project while the local market was at ₹8,500 PSF, simply because the [developer due diligence](/blog/mohali-developer-project-due-diligence-checklist) gave them confidence from 8,000 miles away.

## Case Study: The Rajpura Industrial Corridor
While residential and commercial SCOs were grabbing headlines, a different story was unfolding in the industrial zones. In 2023, 62 vigha of industrial land in a red-zone category near Rajpura was purchased at ₹18.70 lakh per vigha. Six months later, even before the registration was finalized, buyers were approaching with offers of ₹33 lakh per vigha. Post-registration, the rate touched ₹45 lakh per vigha.

Why? The Bharatmala Road. This national highway connects Airport Road directly toward Patiala, bypassing the traffic of Rajpura and Banur. It reduced the travel time from the airport to this industrial zone by half. This is the definition of [vision investing](/blog/mohali-property-investment-vision-corridor). You don't buy the land; you buy the road that hasn't been finished yet.

## What Does This Mean for 2026 and Beyond?

If you look at our [Mohali real estate guide 2026](/blog/mohali-real-estate-guide-2026), you’ll see that the market is entering a phase of "rationalization." The wild 30-40% year-on-year jumps of the post-COVID era are being replaced by steady, infrastructure-led growth.

### Identifying the Next Growth Corridor
If you are an investor, you need to look for sectors where the "delta" — the gap between current price and future utility — is still large. For example, the [Kharar IT City corridor](/blog/kharar-it-city-property-investment-2026) still trades at a significant discount compared to Sector 82A, despite being only 10 minutes away. As corporate demand spills over, that gap will close. That is a price history waiting to be written.

### The Land Seller's Perspective
Conversely, if you are a [land seller](/blog/land-seller-reinvestment-guide-punjab-2026) looking to park your acquisition payout, don't just default to the most expensive sector because it looks "safe." Sometimes the safest move is the one that offers the most liquidity. In my years of [government liaisoning](/blog/rhmc-amritpal-singh-mohali-real-estate-consultant), I have seen families freeze their capital in high-priced sectors where resale takes 18 months, while others doubled their money in upcoming corridors with 30-day liquidity.
