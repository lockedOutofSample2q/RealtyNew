# Developer & Project Due Diligence Guide for Mohali 2026: The Complete Checklist Before Booking

To perform complete due diligence on a Mohali developer in 2026, you must verify seven critical layers: RERA Punjab registration status, clear land title ownership, the developer's actual delivery track record (not just their marketing brochures), the escrow account compliance, the financial feasibility of the payment plan, construction quality indicators, and future exit liquidity. Most buyers stop at the RERA number, but that is only the starting point. You must investigate the "internal health" of a project—checking if the developer has the cash flow to finish and if the land is free of encumbrances. In Mohali's current fast-moving market, where prices in sectors like 82A and Airport Road have tripled, the risk is not just losing money but being stuck in a project that stalls six months before possession.

Doing your homework means looking past the granite in the lobby and checking the approvals from GMADA, PUDA, and PSPCL. If a project lacks an Occupation Certificate (OC) or has pending dues with the Municipal Committee, your "dream home" can quickly become a legal liability. This guide provides the exact checklist I use when evaluating projects from a developer's perspective.

## What is the first step in Mohali project due diligence?

The first step is always **RERA Punjab Verification**. Do not take a "RERA Applied For" status as a green light. In my experience as a former developer, I know that many projects get stuck in the application phase due to layout issues or pending CLU (Change of Land Use) approvals.

Go to the RERA Punjab portal and search for the specific project. Check the "Quarterly Progress Reports" (QPR). If the developer hasn't updated these in two quarters, it’s a red flag. You should also check the "Promoter Profile" to see if there are ongoing litigations or if they have defaulted on other projects. A RERA number confirms the project is legal, but the QPR tells you if it is alive.

## How do you verify land title and ownership in Punjab?

You cannot rely on the developer's word for land ownership. You must ask for the **Allotment Letter** (if it's a GMADA plot) or the **Sale Deed** and **Mutation (Intkal)** records. In Punjab, a title dispute can take years to resolve. 

I have seen cases where developers sell projects on "Collaborated Land" where the original landowner hasn't been fully paid. If the partnership breaks down, the buyer is the first to suffer. Always ask:
1. Is the land owned by the developer or is it a Joint Development Agreement (JDA)?
2. Is there an Encumbrance Certificate (EC) for the last 13 to 30 years?
3. Is the land free from any "Lien" or bank mortgage that hasn't been disclosed?
4. Has the [PUDA approved project Mohali](/blog/puda-approved-project-mohali-verify) status been verified against the physical layout?

For more on avoiding these traps, read about [title disputes in Punjab property](/blog/title-dispute-punjab-property-how-to-avoid).

## How do I check a Mohali developer's delivery track record?

Don't look at their current fancy office; look at their last three finished projects. Go there and talk to the residents. Ask them:
- Did the developer deliver on the promised date?
- Was the "Super Built-up Area" what was promised in the [Agreement to Sale](/blog/mohali-real-estate-glossary-terms)?
- Did they get the OC (Occupation Certificate) on time?

A developer might have a "Branded" name, but if their last two projects in New Chandigarh or Aerocity were delayed by 24 months, you are likely looking at a similar fate. Sometimes, a [branded developer Mohali premium](/blog/branded-developer-mohali-premium-worth-it-2026) is worth it for the peace of mind, but only if the data backs up the name. ## What does a "Stalled" project look like before it fails?

Most people only realize a project has failed when the gates are locked. But there are warning signs six months before. Having been on the developer side, I can tell you that the first sign is usually a slowdown in material deliveries at the site. If the cement trucks stop arriving, the cash flow has dried up.

Another sign is a sudden change in the payment plan. If a developer suddenly offers a "massive discount" for 100% upfront payment, they are likely desperate for liquidity to pay off old debts. This is especially critical for commercial investments where [Mohali SCO investment due diligence](/blog/mohali-sco-investment-due-diligence-commercial) requires a higher level of scrutiny. You can read my full list of [warning signs of a stalled Mohali project](/blog/mohali-project-stalled-warning-signs-developer) to protect your capital.

## Is the "Escrow Account" actually protecting your money?

Under RERA, 70% of the money collected from buyers must be deposited into a separate escrow account used only for construction and land costs. Ask the developer for the escrow account details and verify that your cheques are being deposited there.

If a developer asks you to make payments to a different company name or an individual's account, **walk away**. This is the most common way money is diverted to buy more land instead of finishing your building. This is why [prelaunch pricing](/blog/prelaunch-pricing-mohali-real-cost-calculation) can be so risky—the money often goes toward getting the approvals that should have been there in the first place. This includes checking for [Forest Department approval](/blog/forest-department-approval-real-estate-punjab) which can often be a silent killer for projects near green belts.

## Why is "Exit Liquidity" a part of due diligence?

You might be buying for your own use, but you should always think like an investor. If you needed to sell this property in three years, who would buy it? 
- Is the project in a [high-growth corridor like Sector 82A](/blog/sector-82a-mohali-property-price-2026)?
- Is the project accessible via a major road like Airport Road?
- Does the project have unique features that will keep its value, or is it just another "matchbox" apartment?

I remember the [Airport Road commercial trajectory](/blog/mohali-real-estate-investment-analysis-2026). Units that were available at ₹3–4 crore a few years ago are now trading at ₹12–16 crore. The buyers who made money were those who verified the "Vision" of the infrastructure—the airport expansion and the IT corridor—before it was fully built. ## Case Study: The 8-Month Plot Recovery

I once handled a case where a client had paid 65% of the purchase price on two plots in a licensed colony. The seller had not disclosed that the builder had already cancelled the plots for non-payment of installments by the previous owner. We only discovered this when we initiated the transfer process. 

It took **eight months of persistent follow-up** with the builder and the regulatory authorities to reinstate those plots. We had to negotiate a reallocation to an adjacent location just to secure the client's investment. This is why [buyer protection](/blog/mohali-property-buyer-protection-guide) isn't just a phrase—it’s a process of verifying every single document before the first cheque is signed.

## What should I check during a site visit?

A site visit isn't for looking at the show flat. It's for looking at the "bones" of the building. 
- Check the brand of steel and cement being used. 
- Look at the safety equipment for workers. 
- Check the thickness of the walls and the quality of the plumbing fixtures.
- Verify if the project has the necessary [PSPCL load sanctions](/blog/mohali-real-estate-glossary-terms) for the number of units planned.

If you are unsure what to look for, take my [site visit checklist](/blog/mohali-project-site-visit-checklist) with you. It lists exactly what to photograph and what to ask the site engineer.
